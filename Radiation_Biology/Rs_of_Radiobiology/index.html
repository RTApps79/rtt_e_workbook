The Four R's of Radiobiology

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: Why Fractionate at All?

Delivering a full course of radiation as a single massive dose would be far more damaging to normal tissue than delivering the same total dose split into daily fractions over weeks. Time Dose Fractionation (TDF) exploits four biological processes — the "Four R's of Radiobiology" — that happen differently in tumor versus normal tissue between fractions.

Page 2: Repair

Repair refers to a cell's ability to fix sublethal damage between fractions. Normal tissue generally repairs sublethal damage more efficiently and completely than tumor tissue — this differential repair capacity is one of the central reasons fractionation favors normal tissue survival while still controlling the tumor over the course of treatment.

Page 3: Reassortment (Redistribution)

Recall from the Cell Survival Curves lesson that radiosensitivity varies by cell-cycle phase (most sensitive in G2/M, most resistant in late S). Immediately after a fraction, the surviving cells are disproportionately those in resistant phases.

Reassortment (redistribution) is the process by which surviving cells continue to move through the cell cycle between fractions, redistributing back into sensitive phases (G2/M) by the time of the next fraction — effectively re-sensitizing the population.

Page 4: Reoxygenation

Recall from RBE and OER that hypoxic cells are significantly more radioresistant. Killing off the well-oxygenated cells in a tumor with one fraction can shrink the tumor mass and improve blood supply to previously hypoxic regions before the next fraction.

Reoxygenation is this process of previously hypoxic tumor cells becoming better oxygenated (and therefore more radiosensitive) between fractions — one of fractionation's biggest advantages specifically for tumor control, since normal tissue doesn't have this hypoxic-core problem to begin with.

Quick Check!

Which of the four R's specifically helps overcome tumor hypoxia between treatment fractions?

B. Reoxygenation is specifically the process of previously hypoxic tumor regions gaining better blood supply and oxygenation between fractions.

Page 5: Repopulation

Repopulation (regeneration) is the proliferation of surviving cells between fractions to replace those killed by treatment. This happens in both normal tissue and tumor — which is exactly why fractionation is a balancing act: too few fractions over too short a time doesn't give normal tissue enough opportunity to regenerate, while too long a treatment course gives the tumor more opportunity to repopulate and offset the cell killing achieved so far.

Page 6: Fractionation Schedules

These four R's inform how a treatment course is actually scheduled:

SchedulePattern
Conventional fractionation~1.8–2 Gy per day, 5 days a week
HyperfractionationTwo to three smaller fractions per day (more total fractions, often a lower dose per fraction, similar or slightly higher total dose)
HypofractionationFewer, larger fractions than conventional — a shorter overall treatment course

Page 7: Wrap-Up

You've covered Repair, Reassortment, Reoxygenation, and Repopulation — the four biological processes fractionation exploits — and the three main fractionation schedule types built around them.

All four R's connect back to concepts from Cell Survival Curves (repair of sublethal damage, cell-cycle sensitivity) and RBE/OER (the oxygen effect) — this lesson is really about how those cellular-level facts get applied to an actual treatment schedule.
