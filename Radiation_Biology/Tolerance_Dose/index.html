Tolerance Dose

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: Threshold and Tolerance Dose

A threshold dose is the dose below which no biological response is observed; above it, a response appears. Tolerance dose describes the dose at which a normal tissue can be irradiated with an acceptable, defined risk of significant complications — it's inherently a statement about risk, not a hard line between "safe" and "unsafe."

Page 2: TD5/5 and TD50/5

TD5/5 (minimum tolerance dose): the dose expected to result in a 5% risk of severe complications within 5 years of irradiation.

TD50/5 (maximum tolerance dose): the dose expected to result in a 50% risk of severe complications within 5 years.

Example TD5/5 values: whole lens of the eye ~10 Gy, whole brain ~45 Gy, whole lung ~17.5 Gy. Every organ has its own characteristic tolerance, which is exactly why treatment planning constrains dose to specific organs at risk differently depending on which organ is nearby.

A dose expected to cause severe complications in 50% of patients within 5 years is called:

B. TD50/5 is the dose associated with a 50% complication risk within 5 years — the "maximum tolerance dose."

Page 3: The Therapeutic Ratio

The therapeutic ratio compares the dose that controls the tumor against the dose that damages normal tissue:

Therapeutic Ratio = Normal Tissue Tolerance Dose (NTTD) / Tumor Lethal Dose (TLD)

A favorable therapeutic ratio means the tumor lethal dose occurs at a lower dose than the normal tissue tolerance dose — you can deliver a tumor-killing dose without exceeding what nearby normal tissue can tolerate. An unfavorable ratio means the reverse: normal tissue tolerance is reached before the tumor lethal dose, forcing compromises in the treatment plan.

Page 4: Dose-Response Curve Shapes

Three basic dose-response relationship shapes describe how biological effects relate to dose:

TypeShapeMeaning
Linear, nonthresholdStraight line through the originAny dose, however small, carries some risk of effect — this is the basis for radiation protection dose limits
Linear, thresholdStraight line, but starting from a nonzero doseNo response below a certain dose; above it, response rises linearly
Nonlinear, thresholdCurved, starting from a nonzero doseNo response below threshold; above it, response follows a nonlinear (often S-shaped) curve

Radiation protection guides are deliberately based on the most conservative assumption: a linear, nonthreshold relationship for stochastic effects like radiation-induced cancer.

Page 5: Deterministic vs. Stochastic Effects

Deterministic (nonstochastic) effects have a threshold and increase in severity with dose above that threshold. Skin erythema, cataracts, and hair loss are examples — and these follow a threshold dose-response curve.

Stochastic effects have no established threshold, and increasing dose increases the probability of the effect occurring, not its severity. Radiation-induced cancer and genetic effects are the principal examples, and these follow the linear, nonthreshold model.

Quick Check!

Radiation-induced cataracts, which worsen with higher dose above a threshold, are an example of:

B. A threshold effect that increases in severity with dose is deterministic, not stochastic.

Page 6: Normal Organ Tissue Tolerances in Practice

Treatment planning constrains dose to organs at risk based on published tolerance data. This is why a plan for a brain tumor near the optic apparatus, or a lung tumor near the spinal cord, is built around specific numeric dose-volume limits for those structures — not just "as low as possible," but a defined, evidence-based tolerance threshold weighed against the dose needed for tumor control.
