Radioactivity and Isotopes

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: A Quick Review of the Atom

All matter is made of elements and compounds. An atom is the smallest part of an element; a molecule is the smallest part of a compound. Electrons orbit the nucleus in shells; the atomic number is the number of protons in the nucleus, and the mass number is the number of protons plus neutrons.

Atoms with the same atomic number but different mass numbers are isotopes of an element. Radioactive isotopes have unstable nuclei.

Page 2: What Makes Radioactivity Happen

Radioactivity is a phenomenon in which radiation — particles, electromagnetic radiation, or both — is given off by the nuclei of elements. A strong nuclear force holds the nucleons (protons and neutrons) together, but this only works when the nucleus has a correct proton-to-neutron ratio.

Protons repel each other (like charges); neutrons help counteract this. If the neutron:proton ratio is wrong — too many or too few neutrons — the nucleus becomes unstable and eventually disintegrates, a process called radioactive decay. Some high-atomic-number nuclides (like uranium, Z=92) have no stable isotope at all.

Radioactivity is a nuclear process, not an electron/chemical one — it isn't influenced by chemical changes or environmental conditions, and the rate of decay can't be controlled.

Quick Check!

Can chemical reactions or environmental changes affect the rate of radioactive decay?

B. Since radioactivity originates in the nucleus (not the orbital electrons), it's completely independent of chemical bonding or environmental conditions.

Page 3: Measuring Activity

Activity is the rate of decay. The SI unit is the Becquerel (Bq), where 1 Bq = 1 transformation per second. Because a becquerel represents an extremely small activity, the megabecquerel (MBq) — 106 Bq — is more commonly used in practice.

When an unstable nucleus decays, it forms a new nuclide called a daughter product, which may itself be stable or may require a whole chain of further transformations before reaching a stable end product.

Page 4: Modes of Radioactive Decay

Unstable nuclei return toward stability through one of several decay modes:

ModeWhat happensExample
β− (negatron) emissionA neutron converts to a proton, emitting an electron and antineutrino60Co → 60Ni + e−
β+ (positron) emissionA proton converts to a neutron, emitting a positron and neutrino22Na → 22Ne + e+
Electron capture (EC)An atomic electron is captured, converting a proton to a neutron57Co + e− → 57Fe
α emissionThe nucleus emits a helium nucleus (2 protons + 2 neutrons)238U → 231Pa + α

Gamma emission and internal conversion also occur, typically after another decay mode leaves the daughter nucleus in an excited state that then releases energy as a gamma photon.

Page 5: The Decay Curve

As a radioactive sample decays, its activity decreases because progressively less active material remains. Plotting activity against time produces an exponential decay curve — the same mathematical shape used to describe Cobalt-60 source decay and half-life calculations.

Page 6: Isotopes, Isobars, Isotones, and Isomers

These four terms are easy to mix up — each describes a different kind of relationship between nuclides:

TermSame...Different...
IsotopesAtomic number (protons)Mass number (neutrons)
IsobarsMass numberAtomic number
IsotonesNumber of neutronsAtomic number
IsomersBoth protons and neutrons (same nuclide)Energy level/state

Example of isotopes: carbon has isotopes with mass numbers from 12 to 14 (same 6 protons, different neutron counts). Example of isobars: 64Ni (28 protons) and 64Zn (30 protons) share mass number 64 but are different elements.

Quick Check!

Two nuclides have the same mass number but different atomic numbers. What's their relationship?

B. Same mass number, different atomic number defines isobars — the reverse of isotopes.

Page 7: Natural vs. Artificial Radioisotopes

Many unstable isotopes occur naturally — uranium and radium are classic examples. Others are artificially produced in nuclear reactors or particle accelerators, such as Strontium-90 and Cobalt-60, both of which have real clinical applications.

Page 8: Radioactive Isotopes in Radiation Therapy

Several radioisotopes are used directly in treatment, especially in brachytherapy:

  • Iodine-125 (I-125) and Iridium-192 (Ir-192) — prostate seed implants
  • Palladium-103 (Pd-103) — another common seed implant isotope
  • Ir-192 — also used for interstitial implants and breast brachytherapy
  • Strontium-90 (Sr-90) — a pure beta emitter, used in intravascular brachytherapy and some superficial applications

Each isotope is chosen based on its half-life, emission type, and energy — matching the physical properties of the decay to the specific clinical application.
