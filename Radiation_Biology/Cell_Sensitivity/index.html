Cell Sensitivity

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: The Law of Bergonié and Tribondeau

In 1906, scientists Bergonié and Tribondeau performed experiments using rodent testes — chosen because they contain cells ranging from immature, mitotically active spermatogonia to mature, nondividing spermatozoa (sperm).

Their results led to the Law of Bergonié and Tribondeau: ionizing radiation is more effective against cells that (1) are actively mitotic, (2) are undifferentiated, and (3) have a long mitotic future.

Page 2: Maturity, Differentiation, and Radiosensitivity

Differentiation refers to a cell's level of functional/structural specialization. Undifferentiated (immature) cells — whose primary job is to divide and replace lost cells — are called stem or precursor cells, and are extremely radiosensitive. A spermatogonium is a classic example.

A fully differentiated cell (an end cell) has a specialized structure or function, doesn't divide, and is radioresistant. Spermatozoa and circulating erythrocytes are examples.

Quick Check!

According to Bergonié and Tribondeau, which cell type would be MOST radiosensitive?

B. Actively mitotic, undifferentiated cells with a long mitotic future ahead are the most radiosensitive by definition of the law.

Page 3: Conditional Sensitivity

In 1925, Ancel and Vitemberger added an important refinement: the environmental conditions of a cell before, during, or after radiation exposure can also change the extent and appearance of radiation damage — a concept known as conditional sensitivity.

This means a cell's radiosensitivity isn't a fixed, purely intrinsic property — it can be modified when the cell is under stress, especially during irradiation itself (which connects directly to the oxygen effect covered in the RBE and OER lesson).

Page 4: Three Categories of Factors Affecting Response

Cellular radioresponse and the appearance of a survival curve (its n, Dq, and D0 values) can be modified by three groups of external factors:

CategoryExamples
PhysicalLET, dose rate
ChemicalRadiosensitizers, radioprotectors
BiologicalPosition in the cell cycle, ability to repair sublethal damage

A dose-rate effect has been observed for reproductive failure, division delay, chromosome aberrations, and survival time: low dose rates are generally less efficient at producing damage than high dose rates, because cells have more opportunity to repair sublethal damage between hits as the dose rate drops. This dose-rate effect is significant with low-LET radiation but isn't observed with high-LET radiation.
