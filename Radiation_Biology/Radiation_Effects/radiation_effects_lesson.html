Radiation Effects: Total Body Response & Late Effects

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: Two Timeframes of Radiation Response

Radiation effects are generally grouped into two timeframes: early (acute) changes appearing within about 6 months of exposure, and late (chronic) changes appearing more than 6 months later — often as a consequence of irreversible early changes that progress over time. This lesson covers acute whole-body response first, then late/stochastic effects.

Page 2: Requirements for Acute Radiation Syndrome

Acute Radiation Syndrome (ARS) requires three specific exposure conditions to be met: (1) the exposure must be acute (delivered within minutes), (2) it must involve total-body or near-total-body exposure, and (3) it must come from an external, penetrating source — not ingested, inhaled, or implanted radioactive material.

Human data on ARS comes from a genuinely limited set of sources: industrial and laboratory accidents, nuclear weapons testing fallout, therapeutic medical exposures, Hiroshima and Nagasaki, and the Chernobyl reactor accident.

Page 3: The Three Phases of Every Syndrome

Each of the three classic ARS syndromes moves through the same three phases, though timing and severity differ by syndrome and dose:

  1. Prodromal phase — symptoms (gastrointestinal, neurologic, or both) appear, correlating with dose
  2. Latent phase — the person appears symptom-free, even though damage is actively progressing internally
  3. Manifest illness phase — the effects of exposure become clinically evident, correlating with dose

Page 4: Hematopoietic Syndrome

Induced by total-body doses of 100–1000 cGy. The human LD50/60 (dose lethal to 50% of exposed people within 60 days) is estimated between 350–450 cGy, varying with age, health, and sex — females tend to be somewhat more resistant, and the very young and very old somewhat more sensitive.

Prodromal symptoms (nausea, vomiting) appear within hours. The latent phase lasts days to about 3 weeks — the person feels fine, but bone marrow stem cells are actively dying. The manifest illness phase (3–5 weeks post-exposure) brings pancytopenia (depression of all blood cell counts): anemia (fewer red cells), hemorrhage (fewer platelets), and serious infection (fewer white cells). Death, if it occurs, is primarily from infection and hemorrhage.

Page 5: GI and CNS Syndromes

Gastrointestinal (GI) syndrome: induced by doses of roughly 1000–10,000 cGy (as low as 600 cGy in some cases). Above 1000 cGy, severe depopulation of intestinal crypt cells causes partial or complete denudation of the villi lining the small intestine — leading to decreased absorption, fluid leakage into the lumen (dehydration), and bacterial invasion into the bloodstream. Mean survival time is 3–10 days.

Cerebrovascular (CNS) syndrome: occurs at doses greater than 100 Gy (some sources note overlap with GI syndrome symptoms starting around 5000 cGy). Onset is within a few hours; death typically occurs within 24–72 hours. No known treatment exists once the syndrome develops — supportive/comfort care is the only option.

Quick Check!

Which acute radiation syndrome has the shortest survival time and occurs at the highest total-body doses?

B. CNS syndrome occurs at the highest doses (>100 Gy) and has by far the shortest survival time — hours to a few days.

Page 6: Deterministic vs. Stochastic — A Quick Recap

Everything covered so far (hematopoietic, GI, CNS syndromes) is deterministic: produced by high doses, with severity increasing above a threshold. From here, this lesson shifts to stochastic effects — low-dose, long-term (chronic) exposure, where increasing dose increases probability of an effect, not its severity, and no threshold is assumed.

Page 7: Radiation-Induced Malignancy

Much of what's known about radiation-induced cancer in humans comes from a few major population studies: Hiroshima/Nagasaki atomic bomb survivors, American radiologists (early 20th century), ankylosing spondylitis patients treated with therapeutic radiation, and radium watch-dial painters.

Radiation-induced leukemia follows a linear, nonthreshold dose-response relationship, with a latent period of 4–7 years and an at-risk period of about 20 years. Bomb survivor data showed leukemia incidence up to 100 times that of the nonirradiated population at high doses.

Other cancers with well-documented radiation associations include thyroid cancer (from childhood thymic irradiation and fallout exposure, ~20-year latent period), bone cancer (radium watch-dial painters, who ingested radium via paintbrush-tipping habits), skin cancer (a threshold effect, unlike most other radiation-induced cancers), and breast cancer (from repeated fluoroscopic exams during tuberculosis treatment, and among bomb survivors).

Quick Check!

Unlike most radiation-induced cancers, which one follows a threshold dose-response relationship?

B. Radiation-induced skin cancer is specifically noted as following a threshold relationship, unlike leukemia and most other radiation-induced malignancies.

Page 8: Risk Estimates — Relative, Excess, and Absolute Risk

Because it's nearly impossible to link any single cancer case to a specific prior radiation exposure, radiobiologists use population-level risk estimates instead:

TypeFormulaMeaning
Relative riskObserved cases ÷ Expected casesA ratio of 1.0 = no added risk; 1.5 = a 50% higher rate than the unirradiated population
Excess riskObserved cases − Expected casesThe actual number of "extra" cases attributable to radiation
Absolute riskSlope of the dose-response lineCases per population per unit dose; requires at least two known dose levels

Worked example (relative risk): 227 leukemia cases observed per 100,000 irradiated persons; normal incidence is 150/100,000. Relative risk = 227/150 = 1.51.

Page 9: Radiation and Pregnancy

The developing embryo/fetus is considered separately because radiosensitivity changes dramatically across gestation. The first trimester is the most radiosensitive period overall, though the specific type of effect varies by exact timing:

TimingPossible effect
First 2 weeksSpontaneous abortion (all-or-none response — either the pregnancy is lost, or it continues with no observed ill effect)
2nd–12th weeks (organogenesis)Congenital abnormalities, especially CNS; neonatal death if severe enough
2nd trimester onwardDevelopmental delays (reduced behavioral/cognitive performance)

Human data (including atomic bomb survivor offspring) show measurable IQ/scholastic performance differences and a small increase in microcephaly, particularly following doses in excess of 100 mGy during organogenesis.

Quick Check!

Exposure during which period of pregnancy carries the greatest overall radiosensitivity concern?

A. All observations point to the first trimester — particularly the period of organogenesis — as the most radiosensitive window of pregnancy.

Page 10: Genetic Effects

Genetic effects are radiobiology's weakest area of human evidence — essentially all data comes from large-scale animal studies (H.J. Muller's Nobel Prize-winning fruit fly work in 1927; Russell's multi-generation mouse colony studies beginning in 1946). No radiation-induced genetic effects have been observed in atomic bomb survivor descendants, now studied into the third generation.

Key conclusions from the animal data: radiation-induced mutations are usually harmful, any dose to a germ cell carries some genetic risk, the dose-response relationship is linear and nonthreshold, women are less sensitive than men to genetic effects for most of their reproductive life, and most radiation-induced mutations are recessive (requiring the mutant gene in both parents to be expressed).

The doubling dose — the dose that would double the spontaneous mutation rate — is estimated at roughly 0.5–2.5 Gy in humans.
