Page 1: Classifying Cell Populations by Radiosensitivity

In 1968, Rubin and Casarett grouped mammalian cell populations into five categories based on radiosensitivity, using cell death as the endpoint. Increasing radioresistance runs: VIM → DIM → multipotential connective tissue cells → RPM → FPM.

VIM cells are the most radiosensitive cell population in the body — rapidly dividing, undifferentiated cells with short life spans.

Page 4: Applying This to Tissues and Organs

Every tissue and organ contains two compartments: parenchyma (the functional cells characteristic of that tissue — VIM, DIM, RPM, or FPM cells) and stroma (supporting connective tissue and vasculature, generally intermediate in radiosensitivity).

The radiosensitivity of a tissue or organ as a whole is determined by its most sensitive parenchymal cell type. The testes and bone marrow are highly radiosensitive because their parenchyma includes VIM stem cells. Tissues containing only RPM or FPM parenchymal cells — like liver, muscle, brain, and spinal cord — are comparatively radioresistant.

In radioresistant organs, damage often occurs indirectly: stromal (vascular) cells are damaged at lower doses than the parenchymal cells, reducing blood flow and oxygen/nutrient supply, which secondarily damages the parenchyma.

Page 5: Wrap-Up

You've covered the five-category cell population classification (VIM, DIM, connective tissue, RPM, FPM), the mature lymphocyte exception, and how a tissue's overall radiosensitivity traces back to its most sensitive parenchymal cell type.

This directly extends the Law of Bergonié and Tribondeau from the Cell Sensitivity lesson into a practical classification system you can apply to real tissues and organs.
