Lab Worksheet: The Hierarchy of Units

Part 1: Purpose of this Lab

This activity uses an interactive converter to explore the essential units used in radiation sciences. You will practice converting between SI base units and their common prefixes, and learn the clinical context in which each unit is used.

Part 2: Basic & Derived Unit Conversions

Use the Integrated Units Converter to explore foundational clinical units.

Criterion for Success: Your answers should accurately reflect the data displayed by the simulator.

Task 1: Length (Distance)

  1. A patient's SSD is measured as 94.5 cm. Convert this to meters (m).
  2. The multileaf collimator (MLC) leaves have a precision of 1 mm. Convert this to centimeters (cm).

Task 2: Energy

  1. A linear accelerator is set to a 6 MeV beam. Convert this to kilo-electron-Volts (keV).
  2. A diagnostic CT scan uses an X-ray beam with an average energy of about 70 keV. Convert this to mega-electron-Volts (MeV).

Part 3: Special Radiologic Unit Conversions

Explore the special units used for dose, safety, and source strength.

Criterion for Success: Your answers should be accurate and your explanations should correctly describe the clinical roles of each quantity.

Task 3: Absorbed Dose

  1. A patient's prescription is for 60 Gy total. A daily fraction is 2 Gy. Use the converter to express the daily fraction in centigray (cGy).

Task 4: Equivalent Dose

  1. An area survey meter shows a reading of 0.015 mSv per hour near a treatment room door. Convert this to microsieverts (Î¼Sv) per hour.

Task 5: Radioactivity

  1. A PET scanner requires an injection of 370 MBq of a radioactive tracer. Convert this to Gigabecquerels (GBq).

Part 4: Conceptual Understanding

1. The "Special" Units: Read the clinical context for Absorbed Dose. The Gray (Gy) is just a special name for what derived unit?

2. Clinical Convenience: Based on the clinical contexts you've read, why do we use prefixes so often in the clinic (e.g., mSv instead of Sv, or cGy instead of Gy)?
