Page 2: What is Radiation? Ionizing vs. Non-Ionizing

Radiation is energy that travels through space or matter in the form of waves or particles. It exists across a wide spectrum of energies.

A key distinction is made based on the radiation's energy and its ability to interact with atoms:

  • Non-ionizing Radiation: This type of radiation has lower energy levels. It has enough energy to excite atoms (make them vibrate or move electrons to higher energy levels within the atom) but not enough energy to remove electrons completely from an atom or molecule (i.e., it cannot ionize).
    Examples: Radio waves, microwaves, infrared light, visible light, and ultraviolet (UV) light (lower-energy UV).
  • Ionizing Radiation: This type of radiation carries enough energy to remove tightly bound electrons from atoms or molecules, creating charged particles called ions. This ionization process is what can cause damage to living tissue, including DNA, which is why it's used in radiation therapy to target cancer cells, but also why protection is crucial.
    Examples: X-rays, gamma rays, alpha particles, beta particles, neutrons, and high-energy UV light.

The electromagnetic spectrum illustrates this range, with ionizing radiation like X-rays and gamma rays having much shorter wavelengths and higher frequencies (and thus higher energy) than non-ionizing forms like radio waves.

This lesson will focus on the sources of ionizing radiation.

Page 3: Natural Background Radiation - Cosmic Sources

We are all constantly exposed to natural background radiation, which originates from various sources in our environment. One major component is cosmic radiation.

Cosmic Radiation

Cosmic rays are highly energetic particles that originate from the sun (solar cosmic rays) and from outside our solar system, in deep space (galactic cosmic rays). These particles include protons, alpha particles, and heavier atomic nuclei, as well as secondary particles produced when primary cosmic rays interact with the Earth's atmosphere.

  • The Earth's atmosphere and magnetic field act as shields, absorbing or deflecting a significant portion of this incoming radiation.
  • The dose from cosmic radiation increases with altitude. At higher elevations (e.g., in mountainous regions or during air travel), there is less atmospheric shielding, leading to greater exposure. For example, a person living in Denver, Colorado (the "Mile High City") receives about twice the cosmic radiation dose as someone at sea level.
  • Exposure also varies slightly with latitude (higher at poles, lower at equator) due to the Earth's magnetic field.
  • Solar activity, like solar flares, can temporarily increase the intensity of cosmic radiation reaching Earth.

Quick Check: Why does cosmic radiation exposure increase when flying in an airplane at high altitudes?

The correct answer is B. The Earth's atmosphere absorbs a significant amount of cosmic radiation. At higher altitudes, there's less atmosphere above, leading to increased exposure.

Page 4: Natural Background Radiation - Terrestrial Sources

Another significant component of natural background radiation comes from the Earth itself.

Terrestrial Radiation

This type of radiation originates from radioactive materials (radionuclides) that have been present in the Earth's crust since its formation. These radionuclides are found in varying concentrations in soil, rocks, and minerals.

  • The primary radionuclides contributing to terrestrial radiation are isotopes of uranium (U), thorium (Th), and potassium (K), particularly Potassium-40 (40K).
  • These elements undergo radioactive decay, emitting gamma rays, alpha particles, and beta particles. Gamma rays from these sources are the main contributor to external exposure from terrestrial radiation.
  • The concentration of these radioactive elements varies significantly depending on local geology. For example, areas rich in granite or certain shales may have higher background radiation levels.
  • Building materials derived from the earth, such as brick, concrete, stone, and drywall, can also contain these naturally occurring radioactive materials and contribute to indoor radiation exposure.

Quick Check: Which of the following is a common naturally occurring radionuclide found in the Earth's crust that contributes to terrestrial radiation?

The correct answer is C. Potassium-40 is a naturally occurring radioactive isotope of potassium found in soil, rocks, and even our bodies. Cobalt-60 and Iodine-131 are primarily man-made.

Page 5: Natural Background Radiation - Internal Sources & Radon

Internal Radiation Sources

Our bodies naturally contain small amounts of radioactive materials. These radionuclides are taken in through the food we eat, the water we drink, and the air we breathe.

  • The most significant contributor to internal radiation dose is Potassium-40 (40K). Potassium is an essential element for human life, and a small fraction of all potassium is naturally radioactive 40K.
  • Other naturally occurring radionuclides that contribute to internal dose include Carbon-14 (14C) and isotopes from the uranium and thorium decay series, such as Polonium-210 (210Po) and Lead-210 (210Pb).

Radon and Thoron

Radon (specifically Radon-222, 222Rn) is a colorless, odorless, radioactive gas. It is formed from the natural radioactive decay of uranium, which is found in varying amounts in nearly all soils and rocks. Thoron (Radon-220, 220Rn) is a similar radioactive gas produced from the decay of thorium.

  • Radon gas can move from the ground into the air and seep into homes and other buildings through cracks in foundations, walls, or floors.
  • Once inside, radon can accumulate, especially in poorly ventilated areas like basements.
  • When radon and its short-lived decay products (often called radon daughters or progeny) are inhaled, they can deposit in the lungs and irradiate sensitive lung tissue. This exposure increases the risk of developing lung cancer.
  • Radon is typically the largest single source of natural background radiation exposure for the average person.
  • Radon levels can vary greatly from one location to another and even from one house to the next. Testing is the only way to know the radon level in a specific building.

Quick Check: What is the primary health concern associated with exposure to radon gas?

The correct answer is B. Inhaling radon and its decay products irradiates lung tissue, increasing the risk of lung cancer.

Page 6: Man-Made Radiation - Medical Applications (Diagnostic)

Man-made (or artificial) sources of radiation are those created by human activities. Medical procedures are by far the largest contributor to man-made radiation exposure for the general population.

1. Diagnostic X-rays

X-rays are a form of electromagnetic radiation used to create images of the inside of the body.

  • Conventional Radiography: Includes common procedures like chest X-rays, dental X-rays, and bone X-rays to detect fractures or disease. Mammography is a specialized X-ray of the breast.
  • Computed Tomography (CT) Scans: A CT scanner takes multiple X-ray images from different angles around the body. A computer then processes these images to create detailed cross-sectional (slice-like) views. While providing excellent diagnostic information, CT scans typically deliver a higher radiation dose than a single conventional X-ray. The increasing use of CT scans is a major reason for the rise in medical radiation exposure.
  • Fluoroscopy: Produces a continuous, real-time X-ray image, like an X-ray "movie." It's used to observe movement or to guide medical procedures, such as barium studies for the gastrointestinal tract or catheter placement during angiography.

2. Diagnostic Nuclear Medicine

This field uses small amounts of radioactive materials, called radiopharmaceuticals or radiotracers, which are typically injected, swallowed, or inhaled. These substances are designed to accumulate in specific organs or tissues.

  • A special camera (e.g., gamma camera for SPECT scans, or a PET scanner) detects the radiation emitted from the radiopharmaceutical within the body.
  • This information is used to create images that show how organs or tissues are functioning, or to detect disease. Examples include bone scans, thyroid scans, cardiac stress tests, and PET scans (often used in oncology for staging and monitoring treatment response).

Quick Check: Which of these diagnostic medical procedures generally involves administering a radioactive substance to the patient?

The correct answer is C. Nuclear medicine procedures, like PET scans, involve administering radiopharmaceuticals to the patient. X-rays and CT scans use external radiation sources.

Page 7: Man-Made Radiation - Medical Applications (Therapeutic)

In addition to diagnostic uses, radiation is a cornerstone of cancer treatment.

1. Radiation Therapy (Radiotherapy)

This is your field! Radiation therapy uses high doses of ionizing radiation to kill cancer cells and shrink tumors.

  • External Beam Radiation Therapy (EBRT): Radiation is delivered from a machine outside the body (e.g., a Linear Accelerator or Linac). This is the most common form of radiation therapy.
  • Brachytherapy: Radioactive sources are placed directly inside or very close to the tumor.
While the goal is to target the tumor, some radiation dose to surrounding healthy tissues is unavoidable. The planning and delivery processes are designed to maximize the tumor dose while minimizing healthy tissue exposure. Occupational exposure for healthcare workers involved in these procedures is also a key consideration for radiation protection.

2. Therapeutic Nuclear Medicine

As mentioned previously, some nuclear medicine procedures are therapeutic. These involve administering larger doses of specific radiopharmaceuticals that target and destroy diseased cells.

  • Examples include Iodine-131 for thyroid cancer or hyperthyroidism, and newer targeted radionuclide therapies for cancers like prostate cancer or neuroendocrine tumors.
  • Patients receiving these treatments may themselves become temporarily radioactive, requiring specific radiation safety precautions for themselves and those around them.

It's important to remember that while medical uses of radiation contribute to population dose, these procedures are performed because the benefit to the patient (e.g., diagnosis or treatment of disease) outweighs the potential risk from the radiation exposure when used appropriately.

Page 8: Man-Made Radiation - Industrial, Consumer & Other

Beyond medical uses, various other human activities and products involve radiation sources, though their contribution to the general public's average dose is typically much smaller.

1. Industrial Uses

  • Industrial Radiography: X-rays or gamma rays are used for non-destructive testing (NDT) to inspect welds, pipes, and structures for internal flaws.
  • Gauges: Devices containing radioactive sources are used to measure thickness, density, or levels in manufacturing processes (e.g., paper, plastics, beverages).
  • Sterilization: Large irradiators using Cobalt-60 or electron beams sterilize medical supplies, some food products, and mail.
  • Well Logging: Radioactive sources are used in the oil and gas industry to analyze geological formations down boreholes.
Radiation protection for workers in these industries is strictly regulated.

2. Consumer Products

A few consumer products historically or currently contain small amounts of radioactive material, generally posing negligible risk:

  • Ionization Smoke Detectors: Contain a tiny, shielded source of Americium-241.
  • Some Ceramics and Glassware: Older Fiestaware (orange-red) used uranium in its glaze. Some antique glassware used uranium for coloration.
  • Luminous Watches/Dials (Older): Used radium or tritium paint. Modern tritium watches use sealed gas tubes.
  • Building Materials: Some natural stone like granite can emit low levels of radiation.
  • Tobacco Products: Tobacco plants can absorb naturally occurring radioactive elements (Polonium-210, Lead-210) from soil and fertilizers.

Quick Check: Which of the following is an example of an industrial use of radiation?

The correct answer is B. Industrial radiography is a common non-destructive testing method using ionizing radiation. Microwaves and television signals are non-ionizing.

Page 9: Man-Made Radiation - Nuclear Power & Fallout

1. Nuclear Power Generation

Nuclear power plants use controlled nuclear fission (splitting of heavy atoms like uranium) to produce heat, which generates steam to drive turbines and produce electricity.

  • During normal operation, nuclear power plants release very small, controlled amounts of radioactive materials into the environment. These releases are strictly monitored and regulated to ensure they are well below levels that would pose a risk to public health.
  • The average radiation dose to the public from normally operating nuclear power plants is extremely low, contributing a very minor fraction to overall man-made radiation exposure.
  • Accidents at nuclear power plants (e.g., Chernobyl, Fukushima) can release larger amounts of radioactive material, but these are rare events with extensive safety systems designed to prevent them.

2. Atmospheric Fallout

This refers to radioactive particles that were dispersed into the atmosphere from nuclear weapons testing conducted primarily between the 1940s and early 1960s.

  • These tests released various radionuclides (e.g., Strontium-90, Cesium-137) into the upper atmosphere, which then gradually settled back to Earth (fallout), distributing globally.
  • The Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty has significantly reduced atmospheric testing.
  • The contribution of fallout to current background radiation levels has decreased substantially over the decades due to radioactive decay and environmental dispersion. It now represents a very small component of the average person's annual radiation dose.

While these sources are often subjects of public discussion, their contribution to the average individual's annual radiation dose is generally much smaller than that from natural background radiation and medical procedures.

Page 10: Comparing Exposures & The ALARA Principle

Understanding the relative contributions of different radiation sources helps put radiation exposure into perspective. According to organizations like the U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) and the National Council on Radiation Protection and Measurements (NCRP), the average person in the United States receives an annual total effective dose of about 6.2 millisieverts (mSv) or 620 millirem (mrem).

This total is roughly divided as follows:

  • ~50% from Natural Background Radiation (~3.1 mSv):
    • Radon and Thoron: Largest component (~2.28 mSv)
    • Internal (from food/water): (~0.29 mSv)
    • Terrestrial (from ground): (~0.21 mSv)
    • Cosmic (from space): (~0.33 mSv)
  • ~50% from Man-Made Sources (~3.1 mSv):
    • Medical Procedures: Largest component by far (~3.0 mSv, with CT scans being a major part)
    • Consumer Products: (~0.05 mSv)
    • Industrial/Occupational/Nuclear Fuel Cycle: (Very small for general public, ~0.001 mSv)

(Note: These are average values and can vary significantly per individual.)

The ALARA Principle

Given that exposure to ionizing radiation carries some risk (though often very small at low doses), the guiding philosophy in radiation protection is **ALARA**: As Low As Reasonably Achievable.

This means that every reasonable effort should be made to maintain exposures to ionizing radiation as far below the dose limits as is practical, consistent with the purpose for which the activity is undertaken, while taking into account economic and social factors. For medical exposures, this means ensuring that the procedure is justified (benefits outweigh risks) and optimized (lowest dose necessary for diagnostic or therapeutic outcome).

As a Radiation Therapist, you will be a key advocate and practitioner of ALARA principles daily.

Page 11: Conclusion & Key Takeaways

We've journeyed through the various origins of ionizing radiation, from the vastness of space to the ground beneath our feet, and from within our own bodies to the advanced technologies used in medicine and industry.

Key Takeaways from this Lesson:

  • Ionizing radiation, which has enough energy to remove electrons from atoms, comes from both natural and man-made sources.
  • Natural background radiation is unavoidable and includes cosmic rays, terrestrial radiation (from soil/rocks), internal radionuclides (like Potassium-40), and radon gas (typically the largest natural contributor).
  • Man-made radiation exposure for the general public is dominated by medical procedures (especially CT scans and nuclear medicine). Other sources include some consumer products, industrial applications, and negligible amounts from normally operating nuclear power plants.
  • The average annual radiation dose for an individual in the U.S. is roughly split between natural and man-made sources.
  • The **ALARA (As Low As Reasonably Achievable)** principle is fundamental in radiation protection, guiding practices to minimize unnecessary exposure while maximizing the benefits of radiation use, particularly in medicine.

As a radiation therapist, this understanding of radiation sources forms a critical part of your knowledge base, enabling you to work safely, educate patients effectively, and appreciate the context of the powerful therapeutic tool you will be using.
