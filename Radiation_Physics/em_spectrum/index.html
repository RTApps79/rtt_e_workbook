The Electromagnetic Spectrum & X-Ray Production

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: Where X-Rays Sit in the Electromagnetic Spectrum

The electromagnetic spectrum runs from low-energy radio waves up to high-energy gamma rays: radio waves → microwaves → infrared → visible light → ultraviolet → x-rays → gamma rays. X-rays and gamma rays sit at the high-energy end, and they're the two forms of electromagnetic radiation used in radiation therapy.

Page 2: Ionizing vs. Non-Ionizing Radiation

Everything below X-rays on the spectrum — radio waves, microwaves, infrared, visible light, and ultraviolet — does not ionize atoms, even though some of it (microwaves, UV) can still transfer energy and cause real biological effects like heating tissue or skin damage.

Ionization is the removal of an electron from an atom by a photon carrying enough energy to knock it out of orbit. The atom and the ejected electron together are called an ion pair. This is exactly what makes X-rays and gamma rays biologically hazardous if misused — ionized tissue atoms can damage molecules and DNA.

Quick Check!

Ultraviolet light can cause skin damage and is linked to skin cancer. Does it ionize atoms?

B. UV light causes biological damage through a different mechanism (stimulating melanin production, causing cellular damage) without actually ionizing atoms — it sits just below X-rays on the spectrum.

Page 3: Wavelength, Frequency, and the Wave Equation

Wavelength (λ) is the distance between two like points on a wave. Frequency (ν) is how many waves pass a point each second, measured in Hertz. As frequency goes up, wavelength goes down — they're inversely related.

All electromagnetic waves travel at the speed of light, c = 3.0 × 108 m/s, related to frequency and wavelength by: c = ν × λ, so ν = c / λ and λ = c / ν.

Worked example: Find the wavelength of a wave with frequency 4.5 × 1014 Hz.
λ = c / ν = (3.0 × 108 m/s) / (4.5 × 1014 Hz) = 6.67 × 10-7 m

Page 4: The Discovery of X-Rays

Wilhelm Roentgen discovered X-rays on November 8, 1895, and published his findings shortly after. Radiologic Technology Week is celebrated the first full week of November every year in his honor.

Page 5: Producing X-Rays — The X-Ray Tube

Producing X-rays requires a source of accelerated electrons, an appropriate target material, a high voltage, and a vacuum inside a sealed tube. X-rays are generated when high-speed electrons collide with a metal target — the anode.

ComponentFunction
CathodeNegative side; filament + focusing cup. Thermionic emission "boils off" electrons from the heated filament and focuses the stream toward the anode.
AnodePositive side; a rotating tungsten target disk that receives electrons, dissipates heat, and is the source of X-ray photons.
Glass envelopeMaintains the vacuum around cathode and anode so electrons flow uninterrupted.
Protective housingLead-lined housing controlling radiation leakage and electric shock; insulating oil also aids cooling.

Page 6: Target Interactions — Where the Energy Goes

When electrons strike the target, the energy splits three ways: heat (~99%), bremsstrahlung (~0.8%, but 75–80% of the actual X-ray tube output), and characteristic radiation (~0.2%).

Bremsstrahlung ("braking radiation"): X-rays produced when electrons decelerate near atomic nuclei — this is the majority of usable tube output.

Characteristic X-rays: ionization at the target removes an inner-shell electron; an outer-shell electron fills the vacancy and releases its excess energy as a characteristic X-ray, with energy equal to the difference between the two shells' binding energies.

Quick Check!

Which target interaction accounts for the large majority of usable X-ray tube output?

B. Bremsstrahlung accounts for roughly 75-80% of the actual tube output, even though the overwhelming majority of total energy at the target becomes heat.

Page 7: Properties of X-Rays

X-rays are highly penetrating, invisible, electrically and magnetically neutral, heterogeneous (polyenergetic — produced at a range of energies), travel in straight lines at the speed of light while diverging from their source, ionize gases, cause fluorescence in certain materials, cannot be focused by a lens, and exhibit both wave and particle behavior.

Page 8: Quantum Theory in Brief

Quantum theory, alongside relativity, forms the basis of modern physics. Planck (1900) proposed energy exists in discrete units (quanta); Einstein (1905) extended this to radiation itself; de Broglie (1924) proposed wave-particle duality — that on the atomic scale, energy and matter can each behave as either particles or waves. Because ionizing radiation acts more like particles when interacting with matter, its energy-carrying units are called quanta or photons — which still carry wave properties like frequency and wavelength.

Page 9: Beam Quality and Quantity

Two console settings control the X-ray beam:

  • Quality (beam energy) — controlled by kVp (or MeV for electron energy). Affects the beam's penetrating ability.
  • Quantity (number of photons) — controlled by mAs. Directly proportional to the number of electrons liberated by thermionic emission.

These two settings interact: increasing kVp affects both quality and quantity simultaneously, while mAs primarily affects quantity alone — a distinction that matters when troubleshooting image quality or dose.
