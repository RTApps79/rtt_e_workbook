Photon Interactions with Matter

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: What Happens When a Photon Hits Tissue

When an X-ray or gamma ray photon passes through a medium, it interacts with the matter and transfers energy to it. That first energy transfer ejects electrons from atoms in the medium, which then produce further ionization and excitation along their own path.

Four things can happen to a photon in the beam: absorption (energy taken up by tissue), scattering (change in direction), transmission (passes through unaffected), or a combination. Attenuation is the overall removal of photons from the beam by either absorption or scattering combined.

Page 2: Attenuation Coefficients and HVL

The linear attenuation coefficient (μ) expresses the probability of a photon being removed by a given material per unit thickness, for a monoenergetic beam. It's related to the Half-Value Layer (HVL) — the thickness of material needed to cut beam intensity in half — by:

μ = 0.693 / HVL

μ depends on the density of the material, which makes it a less fundamental property than it first appears — compressing a layer of material to half its thickness (doubling its density) won't actually change its attenuation. The mass attenuation coefficient (μ/ρ, linear coefficient divided by density) corrects for this.

Page 4: Coherent Scattering

At very low energy (< 10 keV), an incident photon interacts with an atom's entire electron cloud, causing momentary vibration (excitation) — no electrons are ejected and no ionization occurs. The photon is re-emitted with the same energy but a new direction. Atomic number has no effect on this interaction. It's also called Rayleigh, Thompson, unmodified, or classical scatter, and its main diagnostic effect is blurring shadows on an image.

Quick Check!

Coherent scattering results in:

B. Coherent scattering causes excitation, not ionization — no electron is ejected.

Page 5: The Photoelectric Effect

An incoming photon is completely absorbed by a tightly-bound inner-shell electron, which is ejected as a photoelectron, creating an ion pair. It's most likely when the photon has just slightly more energy than the electron's binding energy.

The photoelectric effect is very dependent on atomic number (Z) — probability increases sharply as Z increases. This is why bone (higher effective Z) produces strong shadows on diagnostic images and is responsible for image contrast; contrast increases as beam energy decreases. Photoelectric absorption is the most common interaction in diagnostic imaging.

Page 6: Compton Scattering

A photon interacts with a loosely-bound outer-shell electron, ejecting it as a Compton (recoil) electron while the photon itself scatters in a new direction with reduced energy.

Compton scattering is independent of atomic number — it depends on electron density instead. It's the most common interaction in radiation therapy (megavoltage range) and is responsible for radiation fog on diagnostic images and for most shielding requirements, since scattered photons travel in unpredictable directions.

Quick Check!

Which interaction dominates at megavoltage treatment energies?

B. Compton scattering is the dominant interaction at the megavoltage energies used in external beam radiation therapy.

Page 7: Pair Production and Photodisintegration

Pair production (threshold ≥ 1.02 MeV) occurs when a photon interacts with the nucleus's electric field and is absorbed, producing a negatron and a positron. The positron quickly annihilates, producing two 0.511 MeV photons traveling in opposite directions. It has no diagnostic imaging importance — it only matters at therapeutic energies.

Photodisintegration (≥ 10 MeV) occurs when a photon is absorbed directly by the nucleus, which then emits a proton or neutron (a nucleon).

Page 8: How Interactions Affect Image Quality

As beam energy (kVp) increases: photoelectric absorption decreases, Compton scattering increases, more photons transmit through without interacting, and image contrast decreases (since less differential absorption occurs).

A decrease in kVp produces higher contrast (more photoelectric absorption) but also increased dose to the patient — a classic imaging trade-off between contrast and dose.
