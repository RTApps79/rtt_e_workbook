Understanding the Linear Accelerator

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: The Role of the Linear Accelerator

The linear accelerator (LINAC) is the workhorse machine of external beam radiation therapy. It accelerates electrons to high energy and uses them to produce either a high-energy photon beam or an electron treatment beam, aimed precisely at a patient's tumor.

This lesson walks through the major components of a LINAC, from electron production to the beam that ultimately reaches the patient, and the systems that keep the whole process safe and accurate.

Page 2: Major Component Overview

At a high level, a LINAC has four functional groups:

  • The gantry — the rotating structure housing the beam-generating and beam-shaping hardware, which rotates around the patient.
  • The stand/drive stand — houses supporting electronics and, on many machines, part of the waveguide and klystron/magnetron.
  • The patient support system — the treatment couch, which moves in multiple axes to position the patient at isocenter.
  • The control console — where the therapist sets up and delivers the treatment, outside the treatment room.

The next several pages go inside the gantry, following the beam's path from creation to delivery.

Page 3: Electron Gun and Waveguide

Electrons originate at the electron gun, a heated cathode that releases electrons via thermionic emission.

These electrons are injected into the waveguide, a precisely machined structure where microwave energy — generated by a klystron (amplifier) or magnetron (self-contained oscillator) — accelerates them to nearly the speed of light.

Lower-energy machines often use a magnetron; higher-energy machines typically use a klystron, which requires a separate low-power microwave source to amplify.

Page 4: The Bending Magnet

On most LINAC designs, the accelerated electron beam travels horizontally through the waveguide but needs to be redirected downward, toward the patient. The bending magnet does this.

An achromatic bending magnet (commonly a 270° or "slalom" design) bends electrons of slightly different energies back to the same focal point, keeping the beam narrow and consistent despite small energy variations picked up in the waveguide.

Quick Check!

What is the main purpose of the bending magnet?

B. The bending magnet redirects the beam (often through 270°) toward the patient and refocuses electrons of slightly different energies to the same point.

Page 5: Treatment Head — Target and Flattening Filter

Once redirected, the electron beam either strikes a target (a high-atomic-number material like tungsten) to produce photons via bremsstrahlung ("braking radiation"), or the target is retracted and the electrons themselves are used directly for electron treatments.

For photon beams, the raw bremsstrahlung output is much more intense along the central axis than at the edges (forward-peaked). A flattening filter — a shaped cone of metal — attenuates the center more than the edges to produce a uniform ("flat") beam profile across the field.

Many modern LINACs also offer a Flattening-Filter-Free (FFF) mode, removing the filter for a much higher dose rate and a forward-peaked profile, commonly used for stereotactic treatments.

Page 6: Ion Chambers — Dose and Symmetry Monitoring

Below the flattening filter sit sealed ion chambers (dose monitor chambers) that continuously measure beam output and symmetry in real time as the beam is delivered.

Machines use at least two independent ion chambers for redundancy — if the primary and secondary readings disagree beyond tolerance, the beam is automatically terminated. This is a core radiation safety interlock, not just a dosimetry convenience.

Page 7: Collimation — Primary and Secondary Collimators

The beam next passes through the primary collimator, a fixed conical opening that defines the maximum possible field size the machine can ever produce.

Beyond that, the secondary collimators — the X and Y jaws covered in the Field Size lesson — define the actual field size for a given treatment, within the limit set by the primary collimator.

Quick Check!

Which collimator sets the absolute maximum field size the machine can physically produce?

A. The primary collimator is a fixed opening that sets the absolute maximum field size; the jaws then define the actual field within that limit.

Page 8: The MLC as Part of the Treatment Head

Sitting below (or sometimes replacing part of) the secondary jaws is the Multi-Leaf Collimator — covered in detail in its own lesson. Mechanically, it's simply the next beam-shaping layer in the head, after the jaws.

Together, the primary collimator, jaws, and MLC form a layered system: fixed maximum size → rectangular field size → conformal shape.

Page 9: Gantry, Couch, and Isocenter

The gantry rotates the entire treatment head around a horizontal axis, allowing the beam to enter the patient from virtually any angle in that plane.

The couch moves independently in vertical, lateral, and longitudinal directions, and on many machines can also rotate. Together, gantry angle and couch position determine where the beam enters and where it's aimed.

The isocenter is the fixed point in space where the central axis of the beam passes regardless of gantry angle — typically 100 cm from the source. Aligning the tumor to isocenter is the entire point of simulation and setup.

Quick Check!

The isocenter is best described as:

B. The isocenter is a fixed point in space — the central axis of the beam passes through it no matter what angle the gantry is rotated to.

Page 10: Imaging Systems — OBI, EPID, and CBCT

Modern LINACs carry onboard imaging to verify patient position before (and sometimes during) treatment:

  • On-Board Imager (OBI): a kV x-ray source and detector mounted on the gantry, perpendicular to the treatment beam, used for 2D kV images or Cone-Beam CT (CBCT).
  • Electronic Portal Imaging Device (EPID): a detector panel opposite the treatment head, used to image with the treatment (MV) beam itself.
  • CBCT: a full 3D volumetric image acquired by rotating the OBI around the patient, used for image-guided setup verification — this is exactly what the 3D Image Alignment Module simulates.

Page 11: Safety Interlocks and Transition to Activity

A LINAC has many layered safety interlocks that stop the beam or prevent it from starting if a condition isn't met — door interlocks, collision-avoidance sensors, dose-rate and symmetry checks from the ion chambers, and more. Any one of these tripping halts treatment immediately.

You've now covered the full beam path: electron gun → waveguide → bending magnet → target/flattening filter → ion chambers → collimation (primary, jaws, MLC) → gantry/couch/isocenter → imaging → safety interlocks.
