Console Operations: Understanding & Setting Field Size

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: What "Field Size" Means at the Console

The field size is the dimension of the radiation beam at isocenter, defined by the collimator jaws. It's usually described as X × Y in centimeters — for example, a "10 × 10" field is 10 cm wide by 10 cm tall at isocenter.

Getting field size right matters for two reasons that pull in opposite directions:

  • Coverage: the field must be large enough to cover the target volume with an adequate margin.
  • Sparing: the field must be no larger than necessary, or healthy tissue outside the target gets irradiated for no benefit.

This lesson covers how field size is set and read at the console, before you practice it yourself in the simulator.

Page 2: Jaws — Symmetric vs. Asymmetric

Field size is created by two pairs of collimator jaws: the X jaws (X1/X2) and the Y jaws (Y1/Y2). Each jaw in a pair can typically move independently.

Symmetric field: both jaws in a pair are equidistant from central axis (e.g., X1 = X2 = 5 cm gives a 10 cm wide field centered on the beam axis).

Asymmetric (independent) field: the jaws are set to different distances from central axis, shifting the field off-center without moving the isocenter itself — useful for half-beam blocks or matching adjacent fields.

Quick Check!

A field defined by X1 = 4 cm and X2 = 8 cm (measured from central axis) is:

B. Since X1 and X2 are different distances from central axis, the field is shifted off-center — this is an asymmetric (independent jaw) field.

Page 3: Field Size Is Defined at Isocenter

A key point that trips up new students: the field size number displayed at the console is always defined at isocenter (typically 100 cm from the source), not at the patient's skin surface.

Because the beam diverges from the source, the actual irradiated area is smaller than the stated field size at any distance closer to the source than isocenter, and larger at any distance beyond it.

This is why field size and SSD/SAD are always considered together — the same jaw settings produce a different footprint on the patient depending on how far the patient's surface or the target is from the source.

Quick Check!

A 10 × 10 cm field is defined at isocenter (100 cm). At a distance closer to the source than isocenter, the actual beam footprint is:

B. Because the beam diverges outward from the source, the footprint is smaller than the stated field size at any distance closer than isocenter.

Page 4: Collimator Rotation vs. Field Size

The collimator can also rotate around the central axis, independent of the jaw settings. Rotating the collimator rotates the orientation of the rectangular field — it does not change the field's dimensions.

Field size (how big) and collimator angle (which way it's oriented) are two separate console settings. Students sometimes confuse changing collimator angle with changing field shape — it only changes orientation.

For irregularly shaped targets, jaws alone only produce a rectangle. Shaping within that rectangle — blocking corners, following an organ's contour — is done with a Multi-Leaf Collimator (MLC), which is its own lesson.

  3. Why field size is always defined at isocenter, and how it changes with distance
