Page 1: Introduction to Multi-Leaf Collimators

A Multi-Leaf Collimator (MLC) is a set of many thin, motorized tungsten leaves arranged in two opposing banks inside the treatment head. Each leaf can move independently in and out of the beam.

MLCs replaced custom cerrobend (low-melting-point alloy) blocks, which had to be hand-cast for every patient and every field. An MLC can form a new field shape in seconds, entirely from the console.

Because the two banks of leaves face each other across the field, the pattern they form together defines the beam's aperture — the shape actually irradiating the patient, within the rectangle set by the jaws.

Page 2: Leaf Width and Spatial Resolution

Each MLC leaf has a fixed width projected at isocenter — commonly 5 mm or 10 mm, though high-resolution MLCs use narrower leaves (sometimes down to 2.5–5 mm) near the central axis.

Narrower leaves = finer spatial resolution = a smoother, more conformal field edge, at the cost of more leaves (and more mechanical complexity) to cover the same field.

Wider leaves produce a more visible "staircase" pattern along curved or diagonal target edges, since each leaf can only move in a straight line.

Quick Check!

A target has a smoothly curved edge. Compared to a 10 mm leaf-width MLC, a 5 mm leaf-width MLC will produce:

B. Narrower leaves can approximate a curve more finely, producing less of a staircase pattern and better conformality.

Page 3: MLC Field Shaping for Conformal Blocking

For a static field, the MLC leaves move once to form a shape that stays fixed for the entire beam-on time — conforming the field to the target's beam's-eye-view outline while blocking nearby organs at risk (OARs).

This is the modern replacement for custom blocking: instead of casting a block for every unique field shape, the planning system commands the MLC leaves directly, and the shape is reproducible and instantly adjustable.

Static MLC shaping is used for conventional 3D conformal fields. Dynamic MLC motion — where the leaves keep moving during beam-on — is used for intensity modulation, covered next.

Page 4: MLC in IMRT and VMAT

In Intensity-Modulated Radiation Therapy (IMRT) and Volumetric Modulated Arc Therapy (VMAT), the MLC leaves move while the beam is on, varying the shape (and effectively the intensity pattern) delivered across the field.

  • Step-and-shoot: the beam turns off, the leaves move to a new shape, the beam turns back on — repeated for many segments.
  • Sliding window (dynamic MLC): the beam stays on continuously while leaves sweep across the field at varying speeds, modulating dose by how long each point is exposed.
  • VMAT: combines continuous gantry rotation with simultaneous dynamic MLC motion and variable dose rate.

Quick Check!

Which technique keeps the beam on continuously while sweeping the leaves across the field?

B. Sliding window (dynamic MLC) keeps the beam on continuously while leaves move at varying speeds.
