Modern LINACs didn't appear out of nowhere — treatment equipment evolved in stages, each one pushing toward deeper penetration, better skin sparing, and more clinical efficiency.

Quick Check!

Which of these historical devices is the direct ancestor of modern proton therapy?

B. The cyclotron's dual-magnet, spiral-acceleration design is the foundation proton therapy machines still build on.

Page 3: Introduction to the Linear Accelerator

The linear accelerator (LINAC) is the workhorse machine of external beam radiation therapy. It accelerates electrons to high energy and uses them to produce either a high-energy photon beam or an electron treatment beam, aimed precisely at a patient's tumor.

Early linear accelerators were large and bulky compared to today's machines, which are compact and fully computer-driven.

Page 4: Major Component Overview

At a high level, a LINAC has four functional groups:

  • The gantry — the rotating structure housing the beam-generating and beam-shaping hardware, which rotates around the patient.
  • The drive stand — houses supporting electronics and, on many machines, part of the waveguide and klystron/magnetron. The gantry rotates on bearings within the drive stand.
  • The patient support system — the treatment couch (also called the "tabletop" or "patient support assembly"), which moves in multiple axes to position the patient at isocenter.
  • The control console — where the therapist sets up and delivers the treatment, outside the treatment room.

Page 5: Electron Gun and Waveguide

Electrons originate at the electron gun, which functions as the cathode — a heated cathode that releases electrons via thermionic emission.

These electrons are injected into the waveguide, a hollow, tube-like structure using radiofrequency electromagnetic radiation (microwaves) to accelerate them to nearly the speed of light. A circulator directs energy into the waveguide and prevents reflected microwaves from traveling backward.

Lower-energy machines (up to about 12 MeV) typically use a magnetron (self-contained oscillator); higher-energy machines use a klystron (amplifier), which requires a separate low-power microwave source to amplify.

Page 6: The Bending Magnet

On most LINAC designs, the accelerated electron beam travels through the waveguide but needs to be redirected toward the patient. The bending magnet does this — redirecting electrons toward the exit window of the treatment head.

An achromatic bending magnet (commonly a 270° or "slalom" design) bends electrons of slightly different energies back to the same focal point, keeping the beam narrow and consistent despite small energy variations picked up in the waveguide.

Quick Check!

What is the main purpose of the bending magnet?

B. The bending magnet redirects the beam (often through 270°) toward the patient and refocuses electrons of slightly different energies to the same point.

Page 7: Treatment Head — Target and Flattening Filter

Once redirected, the electron beam either strikes a target (typically tungsten, a high-atomic-number material) to produce photons via bremsstrahlung ("braking radiation"), or the target is retracted and the electrons themselves are used directly for electron treatments.

For photon beams, the raw bremsstrahlung output is much more intense along the central axis than at the edges (forward-peaked). A flattening filter attenuates the center more than the edges to produce a uniform ("flat") beam profile across the field.

For electron treatments, both the target and flattening filter are moved out of the beam path, and a scattering foil is moved in to spread the narrow electron beam into a usable field.

Page 8: Ion Chambers — Dose and Symmetry Monitoring

Below the flattening filter sit sealed dual ion chambers (dose monitor chambers) that continuously measure beam output and symmetry in real time as the beam is delivered.

Two independent ion chambers provide redundancy — if the primary and secondary readings disagree beyond tolerance, the beam is automatically terminated. The ion chamber is what signals the machine to shut off once the requested dose (monitor units) has been delivered — a core radiation safety interlock, not just a dosimetry convenience.

Page 9: Collimation — Primary and Secondary Collimators

The beam next passes through the primary collimator, a fixed conical opening that sets the maximum possible field size the machine can ever produce.

Beyond that, the secondary collimators (the X and Y jaws) define the actual field size for a given treatment, within the limit set by the primary collimator. The jaws move to generate a customized field shape — this is covered in depth in the Field Size lesson.

Quick Check!

Which collimator sets the absolute maximum field size the machine can physically produce?

A. The primary collimator is a fixed opening that sets the absolute maximum field size; the jaws then define the actual field within that limit.

Page 10: The MLC and Field Light

Sitting below the secondary jaws is the Multi-Leaf Collimator — covered in detail in its own lesson. Mechanically, it's simply the next beam-shaping layer in the head, after the jaws.

The field light simulates the radiation field by illuminating the secondary collimator and MLC area, letting staff visually confirm field shape and position without turning on the actual beam.

Page 11: Gantry, Couch, and Isocenter

The gantry rotates the entire treatment head around a horizontal axis, allowing the beam to enter the patient from virtually any angle in that plane.

The couch (tabletop) moves independently in vertical, lateral, and longitudinal directions, and on many machines can also rotate. Together, gantry angle and couch position determine where the beam enters and where it's aimed.

The isocenter is the fixed point in space where the central axis of the beam passes regardless of gantry angle — typically 100 cm from the source. Aligning the tumor to isocenter is the entire point of simulation and setup.

Page 12: Imaging Systems — OBI, EPID, and CBCT

Modern LINACs carry onboard imaging to verify patient position before (and sometimes during) treatment: the kV imaging device (On-Board Imager) and the Electronic Portal Imaging Device (EPID), which replaced film with silicon imaging technology reviewed immediately online. Both are covered in depth in the Image Production lesson.

Page 13: The Control Console and Modulator Cabinet

The control console was developed specifically to reduce the risk of treatment errors, allowing verification of setup parameters before beam-on. The modulator cabinet houses the pulsed power supply and supporting electronics that drive the whole accelerator.

Safety systems built around the console and treatment room — interlocks, emergency-off controls, warning lights — are covered in depth in the Console Operations & Interlocks lesson.

Page 14: Medical Accelerator Hazards and Safety Considerations

A LINAC has many layered safety interlocks that stop the beam or prevent it from starting if a condition isn't met — door interlocks, collision-avoidance sensors, dose-rate and symmetry checks from the ion chambers, and more. Any one of these tripping halts treatment immediately.

You've now covered the full beam path: electron gun → waveguide → bending magnet → target/flattening filter → ion chambers → collimation (primary, jaws, MLC) → gantry/couch/isocenter → imaging → safety interlocks — plus the equipment history leading up to it.
