Module 4: Anatomy, Landmarks & Critical Structures

Focusing on Field Borders, Lymphatic Drainage, and Dose Limits (TD 5/5).

1

Dose Limiting Structures (TD 5/5)

You must memorize these values. Exceeding them causes irreversible damage.

1.1 The "Do Not Exceed" List

Organ TD 5/5 (Gy) Endpoint (Effect)
Spinal Cord 45 - 50 Gy Myelitis / Necrosis (Paralysis)
Brain Stem 50 Gy Necrosis / Nerve Palsy
Optic Chiasm 50 Gy Blindness
Lens of Eye 10 Gy Cataracts
Lung 17.5 Gy Pneumonitis
Parotid Gland 32 Gy Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)
Kidney 23 Gy Nephritis
Liver 30 Gy Liver Failure
Small Bowel 40 Gy Obstruction / Perforation
Heart 40 Gy Pericarditis
Exam Strategy: The exam often asks about "Parallel" vs. "Serial" organs.
  • Serial (Spine): Damage to ONE part disables the WHOLE organ. (Max dose matters).
  • Parallel (Lung, Liver): Damage to one part is okay; the rest can function. (Mean dose / Volume matters).
2

Classic Field Borders

Localization scores dropped (8.2 -> 6.5). Recover points here by knowing exactly where the field edges land.

2.1 Whole Brain (WB) Borders

  • Superior: Clear of cranium (1 cm "flash" or shine-over).
  • Inferior: From the Supra-Orbital Ridge (Intersection of outer canthus) to the Mastoid Tip (or EAM).
  • Anterior: Clear of cranium (flash).
  • Posterior: Clear of cranium (flash).
Block Checklist: You MUST block the Lens of the Eye. You usually block the anterior face (sinuses) to spare healthy tissue.

2.2 Spine Fields

  • Width: Typically 6-8 cm wide (enough to cover vertebral body + transverse processes).
  • Length: Must cover the lesion + 1 vertebral body above and 1 below (margin).

Landmarks for Spine Level:

Vertebra Anatomical Landmark
C1 Mastoid Tip
C4 Hyoid Bone
T2 SSN (Suprasternal Notch)
T4 Angle of Louis (Carina)
T10 Xiphoid Process
L4 Iliac Crest
3

Treatment Sites & Lymphatics

3.1 Breast Cancer

Standard Fields: Tangents (Medial & Lateral). Occasionally Supraclavicular (SCV) and Posterior Axillary Boost (PAB).

  • Borders:
    • Medial: Midline (SSN to Xiphoid).
    • Lateral: Mid-Axillary Line (2cm beyond breast tissue).
    • Superior: First intercostal space (Head of Clavicle).
    • Inferior: 1.5 cm below inframammary fold.
  • Lymphatics: Primary drainage is Axillary Level I, II, III. Inner quadrant tumors may drain to Internal Mammary nodes.

3.2 Prostate Cancer

Simulation Instructions:

  • Full Bladder: Pushes the small bowel up and out of the treatment field (spares bowel).
  • Empty Rectum: Maintains consistent prostate position (reduces motion).

Lymphatics: Obturator nodes, Internal Iliac nodes, External Iliac nodes.

4

Oncologic Emergencies

These patients are treated STAT. You must know the symptoms and typical fractionation.

4.1 SVC Syndrome (Superior Vena Cava)

  • Cause: Lung mass compressing the SVC.
  • Symptoms: Facial edema (swelling), Dyspnea (trouble breathing), Venous distention in neck.
  • Treatment: Radiation immediately. Usually high dose per fraction initially (e.g., 300-400 cGy) to shrink tumor fast.

4.2 Spinal Cord Compression

  • Cause: Tumor pressing on the cord.
  • Symptoms: Pain, weakness, loss of bladder/bowel control.
  • Treatment: Steroids + Radiation immediately to prevent permanent paralysis.

Module 4: Master Site-Specific Oncology Review

1. Central Nervous System (Brain & Spinal Cord)

Anatomy & Lymphatics
  • Anatomy: Cerebrum (adult tumors), Cerebellum/Brainstem (pediatric tumors). No lymphatic drainage in the brain (Blood-Brain Barrier).
  • CSF Flow: Ventricles â†’ Spinal Cord. Important for Medulloblastoma spread (Drop Mets).
Epidemiology & Etiology
  • Most Common (Adult): Metastatic lesions (from Lung/Breast).
  • Most Common (Primary Adult): Glioblastoma Multiforme (GBM).
  • Most Common (Child): Medulloblastoma / Astrocytoma.
Pathology (Histology & Grading)
  • Type: Gliomas (Astrocytes), Medulloblastoma (PNET), Meningioma (Benign).
  • Grading: G1 (Benign) to G4 (GBM - highly necrotic/aggressive).
Clinical Presentation Headache (worse in morning), Seizures, Nausea/Vomiting (ICP), Personality changes (Frontal lobe), Vision changes.
Radiation Specifics
(Borders & Techniques)		 Whole Brain Fields (Metastasis):
  • Inferior: Supra-orbital ridge to Mastoid Tip (or EAM).
  • Flash: 1-2 cm flash on Anterior, Superior, Posterior.
  • Blocks: Eye Blocks (Lens).
Craniospinal (CSI) for Medulloblastoma:
  • Must treat entire brain and spine. Junction shifts (feathering) required to prevent hot/cold spots at cord.
Doses & OARs
  • Whole Brain: 30 Gy / 10 fx OR 37.5 Gy / 15 fx.
  • Primary GBM: 60 Gy Total (45-50 Gy Initial + Boost).
  • OARs: Lens: 10 Gy (Cataracts), Optic Chiasm: 50 Gy (Blindness), Brainstem: 50 Gy.

2. Head & Neck (Larynx, Oral Cavity, Pharynx)

Anatomy & Lymphatics
  • Lymph Nodes: Levels I-V (Submental to Supraclav). Rouviere's Node (Retropharyngeal) is crucial for Nasopharynx.
Epidemiology & Etiology
  • Risk Factors: Smoking + Alcohol (Synergistic effect). EBV (Nasopharynx). HPV (Oropharynx/Base of Tongue).
  • Gender: More common in males (historically).
Pathology (Histology)
  • Majority (80%): Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC).
  • Exception: Salivary Glands = Adenocarcinoma.
Radiation Specifics
(Borders & Techniques)		 True Vocal Cord (Glottic Larynx): Small fields (5x5 or 6x6).
  • Superior: Top of Thyroid Cartilage.
  • Inferior: Cricoid Cartilage.
  • Anterior: 1.5 cm Flash ("Shine over").
  • Posterior: Anterior to Vertebral Body (exclude spine).
Whole Neck (IMRT/VMAT): Treats primary + bilateral nodes.
Doses & OARs
  • Curative Dose: 70 Gy (2 Gy/fx).
  • Lymphoma (Waldeyer's Ring): Lower dose (30-40 Gy).
  • OARs: Spinal Cord: 45 Gy, Parotid: 32 Gy (Xerostomia/Dry Mouth).

3. Breast Cancer

Anatomy & Lymphatics
  • Nodes: Axillary Levels I, II, III. Internal Mammary Chain (medial tumors). Supraclavicular nodes.
  • Location: Upper Outer Quadrant (UOQ) / Tail of Spence is most common (50%).
Pathology (Histology)
  • Most Common: Infiltrating Ductal Carcinoma (IDC) (~80%).
  • Others: Lobular Carcinoma (LCIS/ILC), Inflammatory (Aggressive/Peau d'orange).
Radiation Specifics
(Borders & Techniques)		 Tangential Fields (Chest Wall/Breast):
  • Medial: Midline (SSN to Xiphoid).
  • Lateral: Mid-Axillary Line (2 cm beyond breast tissue).
  • Superior: First Intercostal Space / Head of Clavicle.
  • Inferior: 1.5 cm below Inframammary Fold.
Technique: Deep Inspiration Breath Hold (DIBH) used for Left Breast to spare Heart.
Doses & OARs
  • Whole Breast: 45-50 Gy (or Hypofractionated 42.5 Gy / 16 fx).
  • Boost (Lumpectomy Bed): +10-16 Gy electrons/photons. Total ~60-66 Gy.
  • OARs: Lung: 17.5 Gy (Pneumonitis), Heart: 40 Gy (Pericarditis/Late Cardiac).

4. Lung / Thorax

Epidemiology & Pathology
  • Non-Small Cell (NSCLC): 80%. Includes Adenocarcinoma (Periphery, Non-smokers) and Squamous Cell (Central, Smokers).
  • Small Cell (SCLC): 20%. Central. Very aggressive. High met rate to Brain. "Oat Cell".
  • Mesothelioma: Asbestos exposure. Pleura lining.
Clinical Presentation Cough, Hemoptysis (bloody sputum), Dyspnea.
Pancoast Tumor: Apex of lung. Causes Horner's Syndrome (Ptosis, Miosis, Anhidrosis) and Arm Pain.
SVC Syndrome: Facial swelling, distended neck veins (Emergency).
Radiation Specifics
(Techniques)
  • Motion Management: 4D CT (Respiratory Gating) or Compression required due to breathing motion.
  • SBRT: Used for early stage/small lesions. Very high dose, few fractions (e.g., 54 Gy / 3 fx).
Doses & OARs
  • Definitive Dose: 60-66 Gy.
  • Palliative: 30 Gy / 10 fx.
  • OARs: Spinal Cord: 45 Gy, Heart: 40 Gy, Lung (V20): Minimize volume receiving 20 Gy.

5. Gastrointestinal (Esophagus, Pancreas, Rectum)

Pathology (Histology)
  • Esophagus: Upper = Squamous. Lower/Distal = Adenocarcinoma (Barrett's Esophagus/GERD).
  • Pancreas: Adenocarcinoma (Head of Pancreas). Jaundice is key symptom.
  • Rectum/Colon: Adenocarcinoma.
  • Anal Canal: Squamous Cell (HPV linked).
Radiation Specifics
(Techniques)
  • Rectum: Prone position often used with "Belly Board" to displace small bowel out of field. Full bladder also pushes bowel up.
  • Anal Canal: "Nigro Protocol" (Chemo + Rad). Sphincter preservation is the goal. Inguinal nodes must be treated.
Doses & OARs
  • GI Standard Dose: 45-50.4 Gy. (Limited by small bowel tolerance).
  • OARs: Small Bowel: 40-45 Gy (Obstruction/Perforation), Liver: 30 Gy, Kidneys: 23 Gy.

6. Genitourinary (Prostate, Bladder, Testis)

Pathology (Histology)
  • Prostate: Adenocarcinoma. Gleason Score used for Grading.
  • Bladder: Transitional Cell Carcinoma (TCC). Linked to smoking/industrial chemicals.
  • Testicular: Seminoma (Germ Cell). Radiosensitive.
Radiation Specifics
(Techniques)		 Prostate Sim:
  • Full Bladder: Pushes small bowel up/out.
  • Empty Rectum: Reproducibility.
Testicular (Seminoma): Treat "Hockey Stick" or "Dog Leg" field (Para-aortic + Ipsilateral Iliac nodes).
Doses & OARs
  • Prostate: Escalated dose 75-80 Gy (IMRT/VMAT).
  • Seminoma: Low dose ~25-30 Gy.
  • OARs: Rectum: 60 Gy (Bleeding/Fistula), Bladder: 65 Gy, Femoral Heads: 52 Gy.

7. Gynecological (Cervix, Endometrium)

Pathology (Histology)
  • Cervix: Squamous Cell Carcinoma. HPV association.
  • Endometrium (Uterus): Adenocarcinoma. Hormonal/Obesity link. Most common GYN cancer.
  • Ovarian: Epithelial. Spreads via seeding (peritoneal fluid). "Silent Killer".
Radiation Specifics
(Techniques)		 Brachytherapy (Internal): Critical for Cervix.
  • Point A: 2 cm superior to cervical os, 2 cm lateral. Represents medial parametrium/ureter crossing.
  • Point B: 3 cm lateral to Point A (Pelvic wall nodes).
  • Tandem & Ovoids: Common applicators.
Doses
  • External Beam: 45 Gy to Whole Pelvis ("4-Field Box").
  • Brachytherapy Boost: brings total dose to cervix >80 Gy.

8. Lymphoma & Sarcoma

Pathology (Histology)
  • Hodgkin's (HL): Presence of Reed-Sternberg Cell. Contiguous spread (predictable). Bimodal age peaks.
  • Non-Hodgkin's (NHL): Diffuse spread. No Reed-Sternberg. B-Cell or T-Cell.
Radiation Specifics
(Fields)
  • Mantle Field (Historical): Large field covering neck, axilla, mediastinum. Blocked lungs/humeral heads.
  • Inverted Y: Covers para-aortic and pelvic nodes.
  • Current Standard: Involved Site Radiation Therapy (ISRT) - smaller, tighter margins.
Doses
  • Lymphoma: Very radiosensitive. 30-36 Gy.
  • Sarcoma (Soft Tissue): Radioresistant. Requires 60-66 Gy post-op. Strip of tissue spared to prevent lymphedema.
