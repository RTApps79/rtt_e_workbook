Module 5: Comprehensive Site-Specific Pathology & Treatment

Detailed breakdown of Histopathology, Tumor Location, and Treatment specifics for all major sites.

CNS
Central Nervous System (Brain & Spinal Cord)
Most Common Histopathology Gliomas (Astrocytoma / Glioblastoma Multiforme)

Specifically, Glioblastoma Multiforme (GBM) is the most common and most aggressive primary adult brain tumor.

Note: Metastatic disease (from Lung/Breast) is more common than primary brain tumors.

Specific Tumor Locations Cerebrum (Adults) vs. Cerebellum (Children)
  • Adults: Supratentorial (Upper brain).
  • Children: Infratentorial (Posterior Fossa/Cerebellum) - e.g., Medulloblastoma.
Treatment Pearl (CSI): For Medulloblastoma, the entire subarachnoid space (Brain + Spine) must be treated because the tumor seeds via CSF (drop metastases).
H&N
Head & Neck
Most Common Histopathology Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)

~80-90% of all H&N cancers are Squamous Cell.

Exceptions (The "Weird" Ones) Adenocarcinoma (Salivary Glands)

Parotid and minor salivary glands typically present as Adenocarcinoma, not Squamous.

Site Specific Note / Etiology
Nasopharynx Linked to Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV). Not typically linked to smoking/alcohol like other H&N cancers.
Oral Cavity Floor of Mouth & Tongue. Strongly linked to Smoking + Alcohol.
Larynx Glottic: (True vocal cords) - Presents early due to hoarseness. Very rarely spreads to lymph nodes (sparse lymphatics).
Supraglottic: High rate of lymph node spread.
Maxillary Sinus Most common paranasal sinus cancer. 80% Squamous Cell.
THX
Thorax (Lung & Breast)

Lung Cancer

Histopathology Breakdown
  • Non-Small Cell (NSCLC) - 80%:
    • Adenocarcinoma: Most common overall. Not as strongly linked to smoking. Periphery of lung.
    • Squamous Cell: Linked to smoking. Central location (near bronchi).
  • Small Cell (SCLC) - 20%: Very aggressive. High met rate to brain. Almost always smokers. Central location.
Special Tumor: Pancoast Apex of the Lung

Tumor in the superior sulcus. Causes arm pain and Horner's Syndrome (Ptosis, Miosis, Anhidrosis).

Breast Cancer

Most Common Histopathology Infiltrating Ductal Carcinoma (IDC)

Accounts for ~80% of cases. (Lobular carcinoma is ~10%).

Most Common Location Upper Outer Quadrant (UOQ)

~50% of breast tumors occur in the tail of Spence (UOQ).

ABD
Abdomen & GI
Site Histopathology Location Specifics
Esophagus Upper 2/3: Squamous Cell.
Lower 1/3: Adenocarcinoma (linked to GERD/Barrett's).		 Distal esophagus is most common site for Adeno.
Pancreas Adenocarcinoma (Ductal) Head of Pancreas (most common). Often presents with jaundice (blocks bile duct).
Colorectal Adenocarcinoma Rectosigmoid region. Spread via direct extension and lymphatics.
Anal Canal Squamous Cell Carcinoma Linked to HPV. Treated with Chemo/Rad (Nigro Protocol).
Rule of Thumb: "Upper" GI (oral/upper esophagus) tends to be Squamous. "Lower" GI (stomach/colon/rectum) tends to be Adenocarcinoma. The Anus (skin/mucosa) reverts to Squamous.
PEL
Pelvis (Genitourinary & Gynecological)
Genitourinary (GU)
Prostate Adenocarcinoma

Most common location: Peripheral Zone.

Bladder Transitional Cell Carcinoma (TCC)

Most common location: Trigone (posterior floor). Linked to smoking/chemicals.

Testicular Seminoma (Germ Cell)

Very radiosensitive. Most common cancer in men ages 15-35.

Gynecological (GYN)
Site Pathology Key Fact
Cervix Squamous Cell Carcinoma Strongly linked to HPV.
Endometrium (Uterus) Adenocarcinoma Linked to estrogen exposure / obesity. Most common GYN cancer in US.
Ovary Epithelial "Silent Killer" (presents late). Spreads via peritoneal seeding.
Vagina / Vulva Squamous Cell Carcinoma Rare compared to Cervix/Endometrium.
SYS
Systemic (Lymphoma & Sarcoma)

Hodgkin's vs. Non-Hodgkin's

The distinction is vital for treatment planning.

Hodgkin's Lymphoma (HL)
  • Cell: Presence of Reed-Sternberg Cell (Giant multi-nucleated cell).
  • Spread: Contiguous (predictable, moves to next node group).
  • Age: Bimodal (peaks at 25 and 55).
Non-Hodgkin's Lymphoma (NHL)
  • Cell: B-Cell or T-Cell origin. No Reed-Sternberg.
  • Spread: Non-contiguous (random/diffuse spread).
  • Age: Risk increases with age.
Bone Tumors
  • Osteosarcoma: Most common bone primary. Radioresistant. Metaphysis of long bones (knee).
  • Ewing's Sarcoma: Radiosensitive. Diaphysis (shaft) of long bones. "Onion-skin" appearance on X-ray.
  • Multiple Myeloma: Plasma cell tumor. "Punched out" lytic lesions.
