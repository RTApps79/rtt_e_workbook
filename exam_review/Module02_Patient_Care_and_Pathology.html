Ethical, Legal, and Communication Essentials

0.1 Patients’ Rights, Consent, and Goals of Care

Patient care starts with respect for autonomy, clear communication, and safe verification. In radiation therapy, you’ll see these themes every day: consent, privacy, access to information, and aligning care with the patient’s goals.

  • Consent — recognize common forms:
    • Informed: patient understands the nature, benefits, risks, and alternatives.
    • Oral: spoken permission (often documented in the record).
    • Implied: patient’s actions indicate agreement (e.g., presenting for treatment after education).
  • Confidentiality (HIPAA) — protect patient health information: discuss only with those involved in care, in appropriate settings.
  • Privacy — preserve dignity during positioning, gowning, and when dealing with skin marks/tattoos and sensitive sites.
  • Access to information — patients can request understandable explanations of their plan and what to expect.
  • Extent of care — understand facility policy for DNR and escalation, and the difference between treatment immobilization vs patient restraints.
  • Advanced directives — be aware of living wills, health care proxy, and other directives that guide decision-making.
  • Research participation — patients may be enrolled in clinical trials; follow protocol and documentation requirements.
  • Goal of care — examples include definitive (curative intent) and palliative (symptom control/quality of life).
Standards of practice: Professional conduct expectations (including ethical standards for patient care, confidentiality, and accountability) are part of what’s assessed on the exam and in clinical practice.
Daily translation: A rights-based approach includes the American Hospital Association’s Patient Care Partnership themes—respect, communication, involvement in decisions, and safety. Translate this into everyday actions: protect privacy, use plain language, and verify correctly every time.

0.2 Legal Concepts You Should Recognize (High Yield)

You’re not expected to be an attorney, but you are expected to recognize common legal language and how it shows up in safe practice.

Common terminology

  • Battery: unauthorized touching/procedure.
  • Negligence: failure to act as a reasonably prudent professional would.
  • Malpractice: professional negligence resulting in harm.
  • Beneficence: ethical duty to act for the patient’s benefit.

Legal doctrines

  • Respondeat superior: employers may be liable for employees’ actions within scope of employment.
  • Res ipsa loquitur: “the thing speaks for itself” (negligence inferred when harm wouldn’t normally occur without it).
Restraints vs immobilization: Treatment immobilization devices are used to prevent motion for accuracy; restraints are used to restrict patient movement for safety/behavioral control and have different legal/ethical requirements.

0.3 Verification and “Pause Points” (Patient ID, Site, Prescription)

Verification is a legal and safety cornerstone. Build a habit of brief, consistent pause points before simulation, imaging, and every treatment fraction.

Minimum verification checklist (every encounter):
  • Two patient identifiers (e.g., full name + date of birth) — match armband and record-and-verify system.
  • Treatment site / laterality — confirm correct site and that immobilization, marks, and images match.
  • Prescription & plan consistency — correct dose/fractionation, energy/modality, accessories (bolus/wedge), and imaging orders.
Common verification pitfalls to avoid
  • “Nickname” identification, room-number identification, or relying on staff recognition.
  • Assuming laterality from diagnosis without confirming the ordered site.
  • Using an old plan (re-sim/new CT) without confirming which dataset is active.
  • Accessory mismatch (bolus on/off, wrong thickness, wrong energy for field).

0.4 Interpersonal Communication and Patient Education

Modes of communication

  • Verbal/written (teach-back, written instructions, simple diagrams).
  • Nonverbal (eye contact, posture, appropriate touch, tone, pacing).

Explain medical terms clearly

Replace jargon with “everyday language” (e.g., “treatment position” instead of “immobilization,” “daily image check” instead of “CBCT verification” when appropriate).

Challenges you’ll see often

  • Language barriers → use qualified interpreters (not family/children when avoidable).
  • Cultural/social factors → ask preference-based questions; avoid assumptions.
  • Physical/sensory/cognitive impairments (hearing loss, dementia, aphasia).
  • Age-related needs (pediatric vs geriatric communication).
  • Emotional status (shock, denial, anger, anxiety; stages of grief).

Patient education you should be able to deliver

  • What will happen: purpose, expected duration on the table, and what the patient will feel (often nothing).
  • Radiation basics: external beam treatment does not make the patient radioactive; contrast this with some nuclear medicine procedures.
  • Pre-/post-treatment instructions: preparation steps, diet guidance, and medication timing as ordered.
  • Skin care and marks: how to protect skin marks/tattoos and why they matter for reproducibility.

Answering common “other modality” questions

Be prepared to compare, at a high level, dose differences, types of radiation, and typical patient preparation across modalities (e.g., diagnostic x-ray/CT vs radiation therapy; MRI uses magnetic fields and radiofrequency, not ionizing radiation).

Treatment compliance and support services

  • Compliance drivers: predictable routines, consistent education, symptom control, and respectful privacy.
  • Support services: connect patients to hospice (when appropriate), dietitians, clergy/spiritual care, social services, financial counselors, and other professionals.

The Cycle of Infection & Asepsis

1.1 The Chain of Infection

For an infection to spread, it must complete a specific cycle. If you break ANY link in the chain, the infection stops.

Link Definition & Example
Reservoir Where the pathogen lives and multiplies (e.g., A patient with Flu, contaminated water).
Mode of Transmission Direct: Touching, Kissing, Sexual contact.
Indirect:
  • Fomite: Inanimate object (Table, Doorknob).
  • Vector: Insect/Animal (Tick, Mosquito).
  • Vehicle: Food, Water, Blood.
  • Airborne: Droplet nuclei remaining in air (TB, Varicella).
Exam Trap: Know the difference between Droplet (large, travels 3ft, Flu) and Airborne (tiny, travels far, TB). Airborne requires a Negative Pressure Room and N95 mask.

1.2 Cleaning Levels

  • Medical Asepsis: Reduction in numbers of infectious agents (Hand washing, wiping table). *Does not kill spores.*
  • Surgical Asepsis (Sterilization): Complete destruction of all microbial life, including spores (Autoclave).
Autoclave Settings: 121°C (250°F) at 15 lbs pressure for 15 minutes.

1.3 CDC Standard Precautions (Your Daily Default)

Standard Precautions apply to every patient because blood and body fluids may carry pathogens even when infection is not known.

Core actions

  • Hand hygiene before/after patient contact and after removing gloves.
  • PPE based on exposure risk: gloves, gown, mask/eye protection.
  • Safe surface/equipment handling: disinfect high-touch surfaces and shared devices.

Safe disposal and handling

  • Sharps: immediate disposal in puncture-resistant sharps containers; never recap unless required by policy.
  • Linens & supplies: treat as contaminated when soiled; follow facility bagging/transport rules.
  • Blood/body fluids: clean using approved disinfectants; document/report exposures per policy.
Needlestick & exposure response (high level): wash the area, report immediately, follow employee health protocol, and document per facility policy.

1.4 Transmission-Based Precautions (Contact, Droplet, Airborne)

When a patient has a known/suspected infectious organism, precautions are added to Standard Precautions.

TypeHow it spreadsCommon controls
ContactDirect touch or contaminated surfaces (fomites)Gloves/gown; dedicate equipment when possible; environmental cleaning
DropletLarge respiratory dropletsSurgical mask; eye protection when indicated; patient masking during transport
AirborneSmall particles that remain suspendedRespirator as required; negative pressure room when applicable; limit transport
Two special situations you should recognize
  • Neutropenic precautions (reverse isolation): protect an immunocompromised patient from you/others (strict hand hygiene, visitor controls, masking as directed).
  • Healthcare-associated (nosocomial) infections: infections acquired in a healthcare environment—prevention relies heavily on hand hygiene, equipment cleaning, and adherence to precautions.

2.3 Treatment Side Effects: Recognize, Explain, and Escalate

Many side effects are site- and dose-dependent, but you should always be able to: (1) recognize common signs/symptoms, (2) describe likely causes, (3) offer basic management guidance within your scope, and (4) escalate appropriately.

CategoryCommon acute effectsCommon management themesEscalate urgently if…
Skin Erythema, dry/moist desquamation, itching Gentle cleansing, avoid irritants, protect from friction/heat, topical care per clinic Rapidly worsening pain, open weeping areas, fever
GI Nausea, diarrhea, cramping, poor appetite Hydration, symptom-driven diet (low-residue when indicated), antiemetics/antidiarrheals as ordered Severe dehydration, uncontrolled vomiting/diarrhea, bloody stool
GU Frequency/urgency, dysuria Hydration, bladder guidance per protocol, symptom reporting Unable to void, hematuria, severe pain/fever
H&N Mucositis, xerostomia, taste changes, dysphagia Oral care regimen, nutrition support, swallow guidance, pain management coordination Airway concerns, inability to swallow fluids, significant weight loss
General Fatigue, anxiety, sleep disturbance Energy conservation, light activity when appropriate, psychosocial support New confusion, severe weakness, falls
Language to remember: Acute/early effects tend to occur during or shortly after treatment; late effects can develop months to years later. Your job is to identify patterns and report changes early.

2.4 Dietary Counseling: Symptom → Strategy

Diet counseling in radiation therapy is practical: reduce irritation, maintain hydration, and protect weight and protein status. When in doubt, involve a dietitian.

Common nutrition problems and “first-pass” strategies
  • Nausea: small frequent meals, bland foods, avoid strong odors; coordinate antiemetics.
  • Diarrhea: hydration + electrolytes, lower fiber/low-residue approach if advised, avoid lactose if sensitive.
  • Mucositis/sore throat: soft foods, avoid acidic/spicy foods, emphasize calorie/protein density.
  • Weight loss: calorie/protein boosting, oral supplements, early referral for nutrition support.

3.2 Critical Blood Counts (CBC)

Radiation suppresses bone marrow. You must know when counts are too low to treat.

Component Normal Range Function / Critical Low
WBC (Leukocytes) 5,000 - 10,000 /mm³ Fights infection. < 2,000 (Neutropenia)
RBC (Erythrocytes) 4 - 5 million /mm³ Carries oxygen.
Platelets (Thrombocytes) 150,000 - 400,000 /mm³ Clotting. Start watching <50,000.
Hemoglobin 12 - 16 g/dL Oxygen carrying capacity. < 10 (Anemia)

3.3 Kidney Function (Contrast Safety)

Before giving IV contrast, check kidney function. Contrast can cause renal failure.

  • BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen): 7 - 20 mg/dL
  • Creatinine: 0.6 - 1.2 mg/dL
  • eGFR: > 60 is typically safe.
Diabetes: Patients taking Metformin (Glucophage) must stop the drug before contrast administration to prevent lactic acidosis.

3.4 Common Blood Studies in Oncology (Know the Names + Why They Matter)

Beyond CBC and kidney function, you’ll encounter site- and therapy-specific labs. You don’t need to diagnose, but you should know what the tests represent and when abnormal values may affect care pathways.

  • PSA (prostate): used for screening, risk stratification, and follow-up trend monitoring.
  • BUN/Creatinine/eGFR: guide contrast safety and reflect renal function.
  • General themes that shift labs: chemotherapy timing, dehydration, infection/inflammation, marrow involvement, medications (e.g., steroids), recent surgery.
Clinical workflow tie-in: Abnormal labs can influence contrast decisions, supportive care urgency, and coordination with the oncology team.

3.5 Weight and Fall Risk Monitoring

Radiation therapy is reproducibility-heavy. Changes in weight and mobility can affect immobilization fit, setup accuracy, and patient safety.

Weight

  • Track consistently (often weekly) and compare trends.
  • Large changes may trigger mask/immobilization fit issues or skin-fold changes that affect setup.
  • Escalate for dietitian evaluation when weight loss is clinically significant or rapid.

Fall prevention

  • Assess dizziness/orthostasis, sedation, neuropathy, weakness.
  • Use assistive devices; keep environment clear; ensure proper footwear.
  • Never rush transfers—slow is safe.

3.6 Contrast Media Basics (Types, Screening, and “Is It Appropriate?”)

Contrast may be used during simulation or related imaging. You should recognize common contrast types, what they do, and the key screening considerations that make a contrast choice appropriate for the procedure.

TypeCommon useKey properties / notes
Iodinated (water-soluble) CT contrast enhancement Can cause allergic-like reactions; assess renal function; follow screening protocol
Barium GI tract opacification Used for certain GI studies; follow site protocol for aspiration/perforation risk screening
Ionic vs non-ionic Contrast formulation Non-ionic agents are generally lower osmolality and tend to have fewer reactions

Appropriateness screening themes

  • Patient condition: history of contrast reaction, asthma/atopy, hemodynamic stability.
  • Age & weight: dosing and physiologic reserve considerations.
  • Laboratory values: BUN/creatinine/eGFR are used to evaluate renal risk.

Emergency readiness (departmental)

  • Know where emergency supplies are and how to activate the response team.
  • Common emergency meds you may see in protocols: epinephrine, antihistamines, bronchodilators, steroids.
  • Document screening, reaction details, and response per policy.
Test point: If a patient has reduced renal function or a prior contrast reaction history, the “correct” answer is typically to follow screening protocols, notify the appropriate provider, and use alternative strategies as ordered—not to proceed automatically.

Pathology: Staging & Grading

4.0 Patient Evaluation: Epidemiology, Etiology, Screening, and Workup

In patient care, “evaluation” means you can interpret the big clinical picture using the chart: risk factors, how the disease was found, what tests confirm it, and what issues might affect tolerance or positioning.

Epidemiology & etiology (high yield)

  • Risk factors (examples): tobacco, alcohol, HPV/hepatitis, obesity, UV exposure, occupational exposures, family history/genetics.
  • Prevalence vs incidence: prevalence = how many people have it; incidence = new cases over time.

Screening (recognize common examples)

  • Breast (mammography), cervical (Pap/HPV testing), colorectal (stool testing/colonoscopy).
  • Prostate (PSA in appropriate contexts), lung (low-dose CT in high-risk populations).

History & physical (H&P): what you look for

  • Symptoms that affect positioning (pain, dyspnea, claustrophobia).
  • Functional status (KPS/ECOG), fall risk, cognitive status.
  • Implants/medical devices (ports, pumps, pacemakers), pregnancy status when applicable.
  • Baseline side effects and medications that affect tolerance.

Imaging and diagnostic studies

  • CT/MRI/PET-CT help define disease extent and guide planning.
  • Lab results (CBC, renal function) support safe procedures and supportive care.
  • Surgical & pathology reports confirm diagnosis, margins, grade, and staging inputs.

4.1 Grading vs. Staging

Concept Determined By Question Asked
GRADING (G1-G4) Pathologist (Microscope) "How aggressive does this cell look?" (Differentiation)

Patient Transfer & Body Mechanics

5.1 Wheelchair Transfers

  • Position: Place wheelchair parallel or at a 45° angle to the table. Lock the wheels.
  • Strong Side: Always position the patient so they move toward their strong side.
  • Lifting: Lift with your knees (legs), not your back. Keep the center of gravity low. Keep the load (patient) close to your body.

5.2 Ergonomic Devices and Safe-Assist Culture

Good ergonomics protects both patient and staff. Use devices proactively—especially for deconditioned, bariatric, or neurologically impaired patients.

DeviceBest useKey safety points
Transfer board Bed ↔ wheelchair lateral transfers when patient can assist Lock wheels; protect skin; communicate each step
Gait belt Assisted standing/walking transfers Use proper placement; do not pull on clothing/arms
Mechanical lift (e.g., Hoyer) Non-weight-bearing or high-fall-risk transfers Correct sling size; check straps; clear the path; team communication
Rule of thumb: If a transfer feels unsafe, stop and get help. Avoid “hero transfers.”

5.3 Fall Prevention: Practical Checklist

  • Assess dizziness, new weakness, neuropathy, confusion, and sedating meds.
  • Confirm locked wheels (wheelchair and treatment couch) before standing or pivoting.
  • Keep lines/tubes from tangling; manage IV poles and oxygen tubing.
  • Use non-slip footwear and clear the floor of obstacles.
  • After treatment, don’t “rush to the door”—stand, pause, then walk.

Medical Equipment: Tubes & Lines

6.1 Managing Lines

DevicePlacement RuleRisk
IV Bag Must be 18-24 inches ABOVE the vein. If too low, blood backflows into the line (clotting).
Urinary Catheter (Foley) Must be BELOW the level of the bladder. If too high, urine backflows (Infection/UTI).
Chest Tube Must be BELOW the level of the chest. If raised/tipped, fluid flows back into pleural space (Lung collapse).
Oxygen Prescribed drug (L/min). Use nasal cannula for low flow (1-4 L). Combustion. No smoking/sparks.

6.2 Common “Other” Tubes and Devices You’ll See

In addition to IVs, Foley catheters, chest tubes, and oxygen, be ready for these frequent devices. Your priorities: avoid dislodgement, avoid occlusion, and maintain function during positioning.

DeviceWhat it doesPositioning and safety notes
PICC/Port (infusion catheter) Long-term venous access (chemo, hydration) Avoid pressure points; protect dressing; do not tug; coordinate with nursing if accessed
Infusion pump Controls medication/fluids delivery Secure tubing; keep pump stable; check battery/alarms per facility protocol
Nasogastric (NG) tube Gastric decompression/feeding Avoid pulling during mask placement; confirm tubing route; watch for aspiration risk cues
Tracheostomy tube Airway support Keep airway unobstructed; avoid kinking; consider humidification/oxygen needs; communicate if suctioning is required (nursing/RT)
Urinary catheter variants Urinary drainage/monitoring Keep bag below bladder; avoid tension; confirm tubing isn’t caught in table movement
Always ask: “Is anything attached to you today?” before moving the patient. Tubes get forgotten—especially under blankets.

7.4 Other Medical Emergencies (Know the Patterns)

Radiation therapists must recognize common emergencies and activate the correct response pathway. Your actions should follow facility policy—when in doubt, call for help early.

EmergencyWhat it may look likeFirst priorities (high level)
Latex or non-contrast allergic reaction Itching, hives, swelling, wheezing, hypotension Stop exposure, call for assistance, support airway/breathing, follow emergency protocol
Seizure Rhythmic jerking, altered awareness, post-ictal confusion Protect from injury, maintain airway, time the event, call for help
Diabetic reaction Hypoglycemia: sweating, confusion, tremor; Hyperglycemia: thirst, polyuria, weakness Assess, call for assistance, follow protocol for glucose assessment/intervention

Scope reminder: Do not administer medications or perform interventions outside your scope/authorization. Prioritize calling appropriate support and maintaining patient safety.

7.5 IV/Contrast Complications: Local vs Systemic

During simulation or other imaging, complications may be local (at the IV site) or systemic (body-wide). Recognize patterns and respond quickly.

Local effects

  • Extravasation / infiltration: swelling, pain, tightness, coolness at site.
  • Phlebitis: redness, warmth, tenderness along the vein.

Response themes: stop the infusion/injection, assess, follow site care protocol, notify appropriate staff, and document.

Systemic effects

  • Mild: nausea, limited hives, mild itching.
  • Moderate: diffuse hives, bronchospasm, marked vomiting.
  • Severe: hypotension, airway compromise, anaphylaxis.

Response themes: call for help early, support ABCs, prepare for emergency medications per authorized protocol, and monitor/document.

Handling and Disposal of Toxic or Hazardous Materials

8.1 Types of Hazardous Materials You May Encounter

  • Chemicals: cleaning agents, disinfectants, solvents.
  • Chemotherapy/biologic agents: may be present in infusion areas or in patient waste.
  • Metals (e.g., block alloy used for custom shielding/blocks): heavy-metal exposure risk if mishandled.
Key mindset: Know what you’re handling, use the right PPE, and dispose of materials exactly as your facility requires.

8.2 Safety Data Sheets (SDS) and Spill Response Basics

An SDS (Safety Data Sheet) summarizes hazards, PPE, first aid, and disposal requirements for a substance. You should be able to locate SDS resources quickly in your department.

Hazardous material handling basics (high yield)
  • Identify the material and consult the SDS.
  • Use correct PPE (gloves, gown, eye/face protection as indicated).
  • Use designated containers for disposal (sharps, chemo waste, contaminated linens, etc.).
  • Report and document spills/exposures per institutional policy.

  • Accountability: signatures/initials and clear responsibility for actions.

  • Variance from prescription: document errors, prescription changes, and plan modifications according to policy.
  • Medical events: know your facility’s definition and required documentation/reporting pathway.
Practice question hook: If a delivered treatment deviates from the prescription, the exam expects you to know that it requires prompt recognition, appropriate notification, and documentation.
