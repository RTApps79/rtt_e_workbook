From CT Simulation to Daily Treatment

A big portion of “Procedures” questions test whether you understand how the whole process fits together: what gets done at CT simulation, what gets verified before the first fraction, and what gets checked every day at the console.

0.1 CT Simulation Procedure (what must be done and documented)

Goal Create a reproducible setup + accurate CT dataset so the plan can be built and delivered safely.

Think “workflow”: order → sim → planning → QA → R&V → daily IGRT → delivery/documentation.

  • Implement per physician order: correct site, laterality, intent, immobilization needs, contrast needs, and scanning extent.
  • Reproducible anatomic position: head/arms/leg supports, indexing, consistent bladder/rectum instructions when applicable.
  • Equipment orientation & accessories: immobilization device type, couch insert, knee sponge, belly board, breast board, etc.
  • Field parameters captured for planning: isocenter approach (planned isocenter vs reference point), scan range, scan thickness/slice spacing.
  • Reference marks: temporary marks and/or permanent tattoos; clearly labeled and verified.
  • Documentation that supports safe treatment: set-up instructions, set-up photos, notes about patient limitations, and any special monitoring needs.
High-frequency exam pitfall: “A great plan” can still fail if the setup is not reproducible. Consistency beats perfection.
Quick drill: What would you document at CT sim?
  • Immobilization device type and index position
  • Patient position (supine/prone), arm position, head tilt, knee support
  • Any special instructions (full bladder/empty rectum, fasting, pain meds timing)
  • Scan range (superior/inferior limits) and contrast details (if used)
  • Reference marks/tattoo locations and how to align
  • Set-up photo(s) showing arm/leg position and immobilization placement

0.2 Daily Treatment Procedure (what happens every fraction)

Goal Confirm you have the right patient + right plan + right geometry before the beam is on.

  1. Verify patient identity (use two identifiers) and confirm treatment site/laterality.
  2. Recreate the setup using reference marks + immobilization + indexing.
  3. Acquire IGRT imaging (kV/MV/CBCT as ordered) and perform registration to the reference dataset.
  4. Apply couch shifts (translations and, if available/approved, rotations) within institutional tolerances.
  5. Confirm treatment parameters via record & verify: energy, MUs, gantry/collimator angles, field sizes, accessories, gating status.
  6. Deliver treatment + monitor (visual/audio, motion management), then document what was done.

Dose verification In vivo dosimetry (e.g., diodes) may be used to confirm delivered dose at the patient’s surface/entrance for selected fields. If a reading is out of tolerance, think: setup/SSD, accessory placement (bolus), energy/field parameters, detector placement, and chart/plan selection before treating again.

Console safety checklist (use this logic on exam questions)
  • Stop & clarify if identity, site, or plan version is uncertain.
  • Confirm immobilization and indexing match the sim documentation.
  • Verify imaging match quality before shifting (don’t “shift first, think later”).
  • Re-check shifts after a major patient adjustment (arms moved, coughing fit, reposition).
  • Make sure accessories are correct (bolus, blocks, wedge mode, immobilization attachments).
  • Document anything outside the routine workflow (re-imaging, large shifts, interruptions).
Exam framing: If a question asks “what should you do FIRST?”, the safest answer is often verify patient/plan or repeat imaging/reassess setup before proceeding.

IGRT (Image Guided Radiation Therapy)

You must distinguish between the imaging beams used at the console.

1.1 kV vs. MV Imaging

Modality Source Contrast/Quality Dose to Patient
On-Board Imager (OBI) - kV Diagnostic X-ray tube mounted 90° from treatment head. High Contrast: Excellent for bone and soft tissue markers. Low (mGy range).
Portal Imaging (EPID) - MV The Treatment Beam (Megavoltage). Low Contrast: "Compton Scattering" dominates. Good for verifying field shape (port film) but poor for soft tissue. High (cGy range).
CBCT (Cone Beam CT) kV source rotates 360° around patient. 3D Volumetric: Allows visualization of soft tissue (prostate, bladder fill) before treatment. Higher than single kV shot, but critical for soft tissue alignment.
Exam Tip: If the question asks about aligning soft tissue (prostate, head & neck tumor) and not bony anatomy, the answer is often CBCT (3D alignment).

Quick translation:
  • kV (2D) = great contrast, low dose → best for bones, fiducials, quick daily checks.
  • MV (2D) = uses treatment beam, poorer contrast → best for field shape verification (ports), not soft tissue.
  • CBCT (3D) = volumetric anatomy → best for soft tissue alignment + anatomy-of-the-day changes.

1.2 How the images line up (registration) — what you’re actually comparing

The core idea of IGRT is comparison to a reference: you compare today’s image to the reference dataset (planning CT and/or digitally reconstructed radiographs).

kV imaging is typically orthogonal to MV delivery hardware; CBCT is a kV rotation producing a 3D dataset.

  • 2D-2D match (kV-kV or kV-MV): compare bony anatomy and/or fiducials to reference images/DRRs.
  • 3D match (CBCT): compare soft tissue and “anatomy-of-the-day” (bladder/rectum, tumor shrinkage, weight loss).
  • Registration output is a couch shift: translation (x/y/z) and sometimes rotation (pitch/roll/yaw).
Common trap: If the question emphasizes soft tissue (prostate/bladder filling, head & neck weight loss), the most appropriate imaging answer is often CBCT rather than a single 2D kV image.

Motion Management

Tumors in the thorax and abdomen move. If we don't manage this, we miss the target.

2.1 Managing the Moving Target

Technique Description Best Use Case
4D CT CT scan that captures "Time" (breathing cycle). Sorts images into "bins" (0% Inhale to 100% Exhale). Lung tumors (NSCLC) to determine the full extent of motion (ITV).
Respiratory Gating The beam turns ON only when the tumor is in a specific "window" (usually Exhale). Left breast (DIBH) or Lung. Spares heart/normal lung.
Abdominal Compression A paddle/belt forces the patient to breathe shallowly. SBRT (Liver/Lung) to physically limit tumor excursion.
SGRT (Surface Guided) Cameras monitor skin surface (e.g., VisionRT). No radiation dose. Breast DIBH (tracks chest rise) or SRS (detects head motion).
Mini-case logic: picking a motion management strategy
  • Lung SBRT + large excursion: 4DCT to quantify motion + consider gating or compression.
  • Left breast heart sparing: DIBH (often with SGRT) + kV imaging as needed.
  • Patient can’t hold breath: consider compression or an ITV strategy depending on site.

IMRT vs. VMAT

How does the machine deliver complex dose distributions?

3.1 Shaping the Dose

  • IMRT (Intensity Modulated Radiation Therapy):
    • "Step and Shoot": Gantry stops → MLCs move → Beam ON → Beam OFF → Gantry moves.
    • "Sliding Window": MLCs move while the beam is ON. (Dynamic MLC).
    • Goal: Creates concave dose distributions (carves out OARs like the spinal cord).
  • VMAT (Volumetric Modulated Arc Therapy):
    • The Gantry rotates while the Beam is ON and MLCs move and Dose Rate changes.
    • Advantage: Much faster treatment time (better for patient comfort/less intrafraction motion).

Setup Errors: Random vs. Systematic

This is a classic registry concept. You must know the difference.

4.1 Classifying Errors

Error Type Definition Example Fix
Systematic Error An error that happens in the same direction/magnitude every day. The lasers were aligned incorrectly at Sim. The isocenter was shifted 1cm right in the plan but not on the patient. Requires replanning or shifting the reference marks. (Hard to detect without imaging).
Random Error An error that changes unpredictably day-to-day. Patient bloat, muscle tension, slightly different arm position, coughing. Managed by PTV margins and daily IGRT. (Cannot be "fixed," only minimized).
Exam Tip: If the error is inherent to the process (Sim/Planning), it is Systematic. If it is inherent to the patient's state on a specific day, it is Random.
Safety tie-in: what prevents errors?
  • Systematic → caught by simulation documentation, plan checks, and first-day imaging/verification.
  • Random → reduced by daily imaging, good immobilization, and motion management.

Record & Verify Systems

Record & Verify (R&V) systems are a major safety layer in daily treatment delivery. They store and enforce the planned parameters at the treatment console, but they are not a replacement for therapist verification.

5.1 What an R&V system does (and does not do)

  • Does: link patient → course → plan/fields; load parameters (energy, MUs, gantry/collimator angles, field size/MLC pattern, imaging instructions); record delivered fractions.
  • Does: enforce interlocks/tolerances (e.g., prevents beam-on if wrong energy/angle beyond tolerance, depending on configuration).
  • Does: support documentation (treatment record, imaging record, notes, approvals).
  • Does NOT: confirm you positioned the patient correctly or that the immobilization is correct.
  • Does NOT: guarantee the correct patient if the wrong chart is opened—therapist ID checks still matter.
Exam concept: When something “doesn’t match,” the safest action is to stop, re-verify identity/plan, and follow department policy (re-image, contact physics/dosimetry, document).

5.2 Common R&V safety checks (practical console logic)

Check What you’re confirming Why it matters
Patient/Plan Verification Correct patient, correct course, correct plan version, correct site/laterality. Prevents wrong-patient or wrong-course treatments.
Setup Instructions Immobilization, indexing, bolus, accessories, special instructions. Reproducibility + dose accuracy depends on geometry.
Parameter Match Energy, MUs, gantry/collimator angles, jaw settings/MLC, wedges/FFF, couch parameters. Incorrect parameters can cause large dose errors quickly.
Imaging Order kV/MV/CBCT per fraction schedule; match + shift tolerances. Ensures IGRT is performed as prescribed and documented.
Overrides & Interruptions Any overrides are justified, authorized, and documented; beam interruptions are handled per policy. Overrides are high-risk and must be traceable.
Mini-case: R&V shows the wrong energy (what do you do?)
  • Do not treat. Stop and verify you have the correct patient and correct plan opened.
  • Verify the prescription and the plan approval status.
  • Check whether the displayed parameters are from a different field or plan version.
  • Escalate per policy (lead therapist/physicist/dosimetry) and document the issue.

Off-Axis Calculation

Off-axis calculations appear when the point of interest is not on the central axis of the beam. Because beam intensity changes across the field (beam profile), you may need an off-axis factor/ratio.

6.1 The concept (what the exam is testing)

  • Central axis is usually the reference for depth dose and output data.
  • Off-axis points may receive slightly different dose because of the beam profile (flatness/symmetry limits).
  • When the exam gives an off-axis ratio (OAR/OAF) table/graph, you use it like any other factor: multiply dose by the factor (or divide MUs by the factor), depending on how the question is framed.
  • Key rule: Use the values provided in the question (off-axis factors are machine/energy/depth specific).
Exam wording tip:
  • If you need the dose at an off-axis point → central-axis dose × off-axis factor.
  • If you need the MUs to deliver a fixed dose at an off-axis point → central-axis MU ÷ off-axis factor.

6.2 One worked example (MU adjustment using an off-axis factor)

Scenario: Your independent check calculates 180 MU to deliver 200 cGy on central axis. The calculation point is actually 4 cm off-axis, and the question provides an off-axis factor = 0.98 for that location/depth/energy.

MUoff-axis = MUcentral ÷ OAF

Step-by-step:

  1. Write what you have: MUcentral = 180; OAF = 0.98
  2. Compute: 180 ÷ 0.98 = 183.673...
  3. Round appropriately (per typical clinical rounding): 184 MU
Reasonableness check: If OAF is < 1.0, the off-axis point receives less dose per MU, so you need more MU to deliver the same dose at that point.
Extra practice prompt

A point 6 cm off-axis has OAF = 0.95. Your central-axis MU is 220. What MU is needed to deliver the same dose at that point? (Use: MU = 220 ÷ 0.95.)
