Inside the Linear Accelerator

1.1 The Path of the Electron

To create a treatment beam, electrons must be produced, accelerated, and steered. You must know the order of components.

Component Function
Klystron / Magnetron Provides the Microwave Power to accelerate electrons.
Mnemonic: Magnetron = Microwave (Lower energy units). Klystron = High Energy (Linear accelerators > 10MV).
Circulator Prevents microwaves from bouncing back and damaging the Klystron (One-way valve).
Waveguide Copper pipe where electrons surf on microwaves to gain speed.
Bending Magnet Turns the electron beam 90° or 270° to point down at the patient. Also filters energy spectrum.
Target Tungsten target. Electrons hit it →Bremsstrahlung interactions →Photons (X-rays) created.
Note: Retracted for Electron therapy mode.
Flattening Filter Cone-shaped metal. Makes the forward-peaked beam uniform (flat) across the field.
Note: Retracted for "Port Films" or FFF (Flattening Filter Free) modes.

Dosimetry & Calibration Physics

2.1 Temperature & Pressure Correction (Ctp)

The Context: Ionization chambers used for calibration (like Farmer chambers) are usually vented to the outside air. This means the mass of air inside the chamber changes with the weather.

  • Standard Conditions: Calibration labs use 22°C (295.2 K) and 760 mmHg (1 atm).
  • The Problem: If the air in the room is hotter or has lower pressure than standard, the number of air molecules in the chamber changes, affecting the reading. We must mathematically "correct" the reading back to standard conditions.
Ctp = [(273.2 + T) / 295.2] × [760 / P]
Scenario Physical Effect on Air Meter Reading Action Items (Logic)
Temperature INCREASES (Hotter) Air Expands (Less air molecules in chamber). Decreases (Reads Low) Ctp > 1.0. You must multiply by a factor greater than 1 to boost the result up to the correct value.
Pressure INCREASES (High Pressure) Air Compresses (More air molecules in chamber). Increases (Reads High) Ctp < 1.0. You must multiply by a factor less than 1 to scale the result down.
Calculation Rule: Always convert Temperature to Kelvin (C + 273.2) for the ratio. Pressure remains in mmHg.

Quality Assurance (QA) Tolerances

You must know the difference between Daily and Monthly checks, and the "2 vs 3" rule.

3.1 Daily QA (The Therapist's Job)

These checks affect patient safety immediately.

Test Tolerance
Audio / Visual Monitors Functional (Pass/Fail)
Door Interlock Functional (Beam must stop)
X-Ray Output Constancy 3%
Lasers 2 mm
ODI (Optical Distance Indicator) 2 mm
Rule of Thumb: Mechanical checks (Lasers, ODI) are usually 2 mm. Dosimetric checks (Output) are usually 3% daily (and 2% monthly).

3.2 Linac Warm-Up & Safety Inspection (Daily Start-Up)

Before a single patient is treated, the therapist must confirm the system is safe to operate and that the machine is behaving normally. Think of this as a “safety + readiness + alignment” checklist.

Start-Up Check What you are verifying Why it matters
Interlock systems (door, beam-on, motion, imaging) Interlocks stop beam when a safety condition is violated. Prevents unintended irradiation when the room is not secured.
Safety lights / status indicators Correct “beam-on / ready / in-use” indications. Provides clear visual cues for staff inside/outside the vault.
Emergency switches (E‑stop, beam hold/off) Emergency controls function and are accessible. Critical for immediate response to patient distress or collision risk.
Laser alignment (including isocenter lasers/sidelights) Lasers coincide with expected reference positions. Laser drift can create systematic set‑up errors for every patient.
Critical machine parameters (e.g., pressure, temperature) System values are within normal operating ranges. Abnormal parameters can indicate instability affecting output or safety.
Electrical / mechanical hazards No unusual noises/odors, loose panels, obstruction on rails, collision concerns. Protects patients/staff and prevents treatment interruption.
Imaging systems (kV/MV/CBCT as applicable) Imaging arms move correctly, panels respond, image acquisition works. Image guidance is part of safe delivery and verification.
Audio/visual communication Cameras and microphones provide reliable monitoring. Patient monitoring is required during beam delivery.
Clinical decision point: If a required safety check fails, do not treat. Escalate per department policy (physics/service) and document the issue and your actions.

3.3 CT Simulator Warm-Up & QC Water Phantom (Accuracy Foundations)

CT simulation quality control supports accurate contours, isocenter placement, and density (HU) integrity used by the treatment planning system. The ARRT outline specifically calls out a QC water phantom check.

CT Sim Daily/Start-Up Focus Examples What it protects
Safety readiness Safety lights, emergency switches, room clear, table motion verified. Prevents injury/collisions and ensures safe patient scanning.
Laser alignment Wall/ceiling lasers match the CT reference position. Prevents systematic isocenter/marking errors.
Tube warm-up Complete manufacturer warm‑up sequence before scanning. Improves tube stability and image consistency.
QC water phantom CT number (HU) for water, noise, basic uniformity/artifacts. Protects HU accuracy and image quality used for planning.
Exam focus: Know what the water phantom checks (CT number and noise) and why that matters (density + image quality for planning).

3.4 Radiation Output Verification: Methods, Frequency, and Humidity Effects

Output constancy checks confirm that the machine is delivering dose consistently. Output verification can range from daily constancy checks to periodic reference calibrations. The content outline also highlights that environment (including humidity) can affect measurements.

  • Common measurement approach: Ion chamber in a phantom (water or solid water) at a defined geometry and depth.
  • Frequency concept: Constancy checks are more frequent (e.g., daily) than full calibration checks (performed by physics per policy).
  • Environmental effects: Temperature/pressure changes affect vented chambers (connect to Ctp above). Humidity can influence electronics/leakage, detector response, and phantom conditions—so trends should be evaluated with awareness of the environment.
Quick recall: Think T‑P‑H (Temperature, Pressure, Humidity) whenever you interpret constancy data.

3.5 Light & Treatment Field Agreement, Collimator Indicators, and MLC Performance

Field agreement checks confirm that what you set (numbers and light field) is what the machine delivers (radiation field). The outline calls out light/radiation agreement, collimator indicators, MLC performance, and laser/isocenter accuracy.

Check How it is commonly verified What a problem looks like
Light vs. radiation field agreement Compare light field edges and radiation field edges (e.g., EPID/film). Radiation field shifted relative to light field → systematic geometric error.
Collimator indicator agreement (jaw readouts / angle indicators) Set a known field/angle and confirm the indicator/reading matches the delivered geometry. Mismatch between digital readout and delivered field size/angle.
MLC performance Picket‑fence or leaf position tests to confirm leaf positioning repeatability. Leaf offset, sticking leaves, or pattern misalignment.
Sidelight/laser isocenter accuracy Verify lasers intersect at isocenter and remain stable through couch/gantry motions as applicable. Laser drift or non‑coincidence → set‑up shift risk.
Practical takeaway: The exam often targets the concept: you are ensuring the machine’s geometry (light field, indicators, MLC, lasers) matches the radiation that will actually be delivered.

3.6 Rotation Checks: Gantry / Collimator / Couch

Rotation checks confirm safe motion and accurate angles. They also reduce collision risk and support treatments that rely on multiple angles or arcs.

  • Safety procedures: Ensure the room is clear, verify collision zones, and confirm emergency stops work before sweeping rotations.
  • Operation (gantry/console): Confirm smooth motion, correct directionality, accurate readouts, and that the system responds appropriately to beam‑hold and stop commands.
  • Angle accuracy concept: If an angle indicator is off, then every planned beam angle can be systematically wrong.

3.7 What to Document for QA (And What to Do When It Fails)

QA is not only performing checks—it’s also proving they were done and recording the response when something is out of tolerance.

Documentation Element Examples
Who / When / Which unit Date/time, machine ID, therapist initials/signature.
What test Output constancy, laser alignment, ODI, door interlock, imaging check.
Expected tolerance The pass/fail criterion used by your department policy.
Measured value + pass/fail Numeric result when applicable + whether it met tolerance.
Action taken Repeat measurement, remove from clinical use, notify physics/service, corrective action note.
Follow‑up Post‑fix recheck results, clearance to treat, incident/medical‑event documentation if required.
Pattern to remember: Measure → Compare → Decide → Document → Escalate (when needed).

Radiation Protection & Limits

4.1 Detectors: Which tool for which job?

  • Ionization Chamber (Cutie Pie): High accuracy. Used for measuring machine output (calibration) or assessing exposure rates outside a treatment room.
  • Geiger-Muller (GM) Counter: High sensitivity. Used for "finding" radiation (e.g., locating a lost seed, finding a spill). NOT accurate for measuring exact dose.
  • OSL / TLD: Personnel monitoring. OSL (Aluminum Oxide) is more common now than TLD (Lithium Fluoride).
  • Diodes: Semiconductor detectors used for in‑vivo dosimetry and constancy checks (e.g., verifying entrance dose or day‑to‑day output trends). Very sensitive and fast, but require appropriate setup and awareness of energy/angle dependence.
  • Neutron Detectors: Specialized survey instruments used to detect neutrons (e.g., photoneutrons from high‑energy linacs). Important for radiation protection assessments because neutron dose equivalent and shielding considerations differ from photons.

4.2 NCRP Report #116 Dose Limits

Memorize these numbers. They are guaranteed test questions.

Group Annual Limit (mSv) Cumulative Limit
Occupational (Worker) 50 mSv (5 rem) 10 mSv × Age
Public (Continuous) 1 mSv (0.1 rem) -
Public (Infrequent) 5 mSv (0.5 rem) -
Embryo/Fetus (Total) 5 mSv (0.5 rem) (Entire gestation)
Embryo/Fetus (Monthly) 0.5 mSv (0.05 rem) -
Exam Trap: Notice the difference between the Annual limit for a worker (50) and the Total limit for a fetus (5). The fetus limit is 1/10th of the worker limit.

4.3 Shielding Concepts (HVL & TVL)

We use barriers to reduce intensity.

  • HVL (Half Value Layer): Thickness required to reduce intensity to 50%.
  • TVL (Tenth Value Layer): Thickness required to reduce intensity to 10%.
1 TVL ≈3.3 HVL
Example: If a beam is 100%, 1 HVL makes it 50%. 2 HVLs make it 25%. 3 HVLs make it 12.5%.

4.4 Facilities, Area Classification, and Postings (10 CFR Parts 20 & 35)

Radiation therapy departments operate under regulatory frameworks that define who may enter an area, what postings are required, and how exposure is controlled. On the exam, you are typically tested on vocabulary and practical meaning more than the exact regulatory text.

Area Term Practical meaning Therapy‑department examples
Restricted area Access is intentionally limited by the facility for radiation protection. Treatment vault and areas where radiation could be present during operation.
Controlled area Area where the facility can limit access for the purpose of controlling exposure, but it is typically outside the restricted area. Hallway immediately outside a vault door, console area with controlled access.
Unrestricted area Access is not limited/controlled by the facility; must meet public dose limits. Public corridors, waiting areas, and administrative spaces.
Memory cue: R‑C‑U is a “from most controlled to least controlled” ladder: Restricted → Controlled → Unrestricted.
Common posting categories you should recognize
  • Radiation area / high radiation area / very high radiation area postings (area‑based warnings).
  • Radioactive materials postings (storage/handling locations, e.g., brachytherapy sources).
  • Instructions or notices to employees (facility postings required by regulation/policy).

For exam purposes, know the category and what it communicates: hazard type, access control, and required precautions.

4.5 Primary vs Secondary Barriers (And What “Area Monitoring” Means)

Shielding design and area monitoring work together: barriers reduce exposure, and monitoring confirms areas remain safe over time.

Concept Definition Example in therapy
Primary barrier Shielding designed to stop the direct (primary) beam. Concrete wall section in the direction of gantry angles used for treatment beams.
Secondary barrier Shielding designed for scatter + leakage radiation. Vault door, control room wall, or walls not struck by the direct beam.
Area monitoring devices Instruments installed/used to survey radiation levels in/around controlled areas. Fixed vault monitors, portable survey meters for periodic checks, and records/trending.
Exam connection: If a question mentions primary vs secondary, translate it to direct beam vs scatter/leakage.

4.6 MRI Magnetic Field Screening Checklist (Safety “Must‑Know”)

MRI hazards are different from ionizing radiation hazards: the static magnetic field is always on. Screening prevents projectile accidents, implant malfunctions, and thermal injury risks.

Study tip: Know the screening buckets the exam lists, and be able to recognize examples.

Screening Category Examples to remember
Pacemakers/ICDs, neurostimulators, aneurysm clips, cochlear implants (screen status and MRI compatibility).
Metal fragments (especially eye), shrapnel, retained metal from prior injury.
Pregnancy status and conditions that affect safe positioning/monitoring.
History that suggests implants, clips, stents, or retained hardware.
Tattoos, medication patches, body‑piercing jewelry, monitoring devices, certain clothing items.
Oxygen tanks, IV poles, stretchers/wheelchairs—only MRI‑safe/conditional equipment allowed.
High‑yield concept: In MRI, the biggest “gotcha” is bringing a ferromagnetic object or non‑compatible device into the magnet environment. Screen, remove, verify, and document.
