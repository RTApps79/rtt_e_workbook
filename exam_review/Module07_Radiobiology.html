The Cell: Structure & Function

To understand how radiation kills cells, we must identify the targets within the cell.

1.1 Cell Organelles

[Image of human cell structure with organelles labeled]
Organelle Function Radiobiology Significance
Nucleus Control center; contains DNA/Chromosomes. The Master Target. Direct hits here cause cell death or mutation.
Cytoplasm Fluid (Protoplasm) outside the nucleus. Mostly water (80%). Site of Indirect Action (Radiolysis of water).
Mitochondria Powerhouse (Energy/ATP production). Damage here reduces cell function but is rarely fatal alone.
Ribosomes Protein synthesis. Site of translation (mRNA -> Protein).
Lysosomes "Garbage disposal." Contains enzymes. If ruptured by radiation, enzymes leak and digest the cell (Autolysis).
Cell Membrane Selectively permeable barrier. Holds the cell together. High doses can rupture it.
2

Direct vs. Indirect Action

Radiation damages DNA, but it does so in two different ways depending on the type of radiation.

2.1 The Mechanisms

Action Type Process Radiation Type
Direct Action Radiation hits the DNA nucleus directly. No middle-man. High LET (Neutrons, Alphas, Protons).
Indirect Action Radiation hits a water molecule → creates Free Radicals (Radiolysis of water) → Radicals poison the DNA. Low LET (X-rays, Gamma rays). *Most common in Radiation Therapy.*
3

The 4 R's of Radiobiology

This explains why we fractionate (split dose into small daily treatments) rather than giving it all at once.

3.1 Why we fractionate

The "R" What happens? Good or Bad for Therapy?
Repair Normal cells repair sub-lethal damage between fractions (within 6-24 hours). Good: Spares healthy tissue.
Repopulation Tumor cells (and normal cells) divide and regrow if treatment is prolonged. Bad: If we take too many breaks, the tumor grows back.
Reoxygenation Hypoxic (low oxygen) tumor cells get access to oxygen as the outer tumor layers die off. Good: Oxygen makes radiation more effective (OER).
Reassortment (Redistribution) Cells move into different phases of the cell cycle. We wait for tumor cells to enter a "sensitive" phase. Good: synchronizes tumor kill.
Cell Cycle Sensitivity:
  • M Phase (Mitosis): MOST Sensitive.
  • S Phase (Synthesis): LEAST Sensitive (Most resistant).
4

Law of Bergonie & Tribondeau

This law predicts which tissues will be damaged most by radiation.

4.1 The Rules of Sensitivity

Cells are MOST radiosensitive if they are:

  1. Rapidly Dividing (High mitotic rate).
  2. Undifferentiated (Stem cells/Immature).
  3. Have a Long Mitotic Future.
High Sensitivity (Radio-sensitive) Low Sensitivity (Radio-resistant)
Lymphocytes (White blood cells) Nerve Cells
Spermatogonia (Germ cells) Muscle Cells
Basal Cells (Skin) Brain Cells
Crypt Cells (GI lining) Bone Cells
Exception: Lymphocytes are the MOST radiosensitive cell in the body, even though they don't always divide rapidly. They die via apoptosis (programmed cell suicide) at very low doses (0.25 Gy).
5

Physics Factors (LET, RBE, OER)

5.1 OER (Oxygen Enhancement Ratio)

Concept: Oxygen is a radiosensitizer. It "fixes" the damage, making it permanent. Without oxygen, damage can be repaired.

  • OER Formula: Dose Hypoxic / Dose Oxygenated (to achieve same effect).
  • Value: For X-rays, OER is approx 2.5 - 3.0. (You need 3x the dose to kill hypoxic cells).

5.2 LET vs. RBE

They are directly related. As LET increases, RBE increases.

  • LET (Linear Energy Transfer): How much energy is deposited per micron of tissue.
    • High LET: Alphas, Neutrons (Bulldozers - massive damage).
    • Low LET: X-rays, Gammas (BB guns - sparse damage).
  • RBE (Relative Biological Effectiveness): Comparison of a test radiation to a standard (250 kVp x-rays).
    • Formula: Dose Standard / Dose Test.
6

Dose Response & Effects

How we plot risk and categorize radiation damage.

6.1 Stochastic vs. Deterministic (Non-Stochastic)

This is the single most important classification system in radiobiology.

Characteristic Stochastic (Probabilistic) Deterministic (Non-Stochastic)
Definition Random effects. Probability increases with dose. Certain effects. Severity increases with dose.
Threshold? NO Threshold. Any dose carries risk. HAS a Threshold. Below this dose, no effect occurs.
Examples Cancer, Genetic mutations. Cataracts, Erythema, Epilation, Sterility.
Protection Standard Basis for ALARA (Linear Non-Threshold model). Basis for Dose Limits (to prevent effect entirely).

6.2 Dose Response Curves

  • Linear Non-Threshold (LNT): A straight line starting at 0. Used for radiation protection standards (Stochastic effects). Assumes no dose is safe.
  • Sigmoid (S-shaped) Threshold: Used for Radiation Therapy. Shows that at low doses tissues repair (shoulder), then rapid cell kill, then plateaus. Deterministic response.
  • Linear Quadratic: Curved line. Fits the data for low-dose cell survival best (Dual-target theory).

6.3 Genetic Effects

Damage to the germ cells (sperm/ova) passed to future generations.

  • Doubling Dose: The dose of radiation required to double the spontaneous mutation rate in a population. Approx 1 Sv (100 rem).
  • Genetically Significant Dose (GSD): An average calculated dose to the gonads of the reproductive-age population.
7

Total Body Acute Radiation Syndromes (ARS)

What happens when the whole body receives a massive dose at once.

7.1 The Three Syndromes

Occurs in stages: Prodromal (Nausea) → Latent (False calm) → Manifest Illness → Death/Recovery.

Syndrome Dose Range (Gy) System Failure Survival Time
Hematopoietic (Bone Marrow) 1 - 10 Gy Destruction of WBC, RBC, Platelets. Death via infection or hemorrhage. 3 - 6 Weeks (Survivable with care).
Gastrointestinal (GI) 10 - 50 Gy Destruction of intestinal crypt cells. Electrolyte imbalance, dehydration, bacteria enter blood. 3 - 10 Days (Fatal).
Central Nervous System (CNS/Cerebrovascular) > 50 Gy Fluid leakage in brain, increased intracranial pressure (ICP), vasculitis. Hours - 3 Days (Fatal).
LD 50/30: The Lethal Dose to kill 50% of the population in 30 days without medical support. For humans, it is approx 3 - 4 Gy.
