Oncology is derived from "Onc/Onco-" meaning mass or tumor, and "-ology" meaning the study or science of. It is the branch of medicine dedicated to the diagnosis, treatment, and prevention of cancer.

Page 2: Understanding Cancer - Basic Concepts

Cancer is a broad term for a class of diseases characterized by uncontrolled cell growth. These abnormal cells can form masses called tumors.

Tumors:

  • Benign Tumors: These are "non-cancerous" growths. They do not typically spread to other parts of the body and are usually not life-threatening, although they can sometimes cause problems depending on their size and location.
  • Malignant Tumors: These are cancerous. Malignant cells can invade nearby tissues and spread to distant parts of the body through the bloodstream or lymphatic system. This process of spreading is called metastasis. Malignant tumors can be life-threatening if not treated effectively.

Understanding whether a tumor is benign or malignant is a crucial first step in determining treatment.

Page 3: Diagnosing Cancer

Diagnosing cancer involves a series of steps to identify the presence of cancer, determine its type, and understand its extent (stage).

Key Components of Diagnosis:

  • Physical Exam: A thorough examination performed by a physician to check for any unusual findings, lumps, or signs of disease.
  • Health History: An interview with the patient to gather information about their medical history, family history of cancer, lifestyle factors, and current symptoms.
  • Biopsy: The removal of a small sample of cells or tissue for examination under a microscope by a pathologist. This is often the definitive way to diagnose cancer and determine its specific type.
  • Diagnostic Workup: This involves various tests to detect cancer and assess its spread. It can include:
    • Laboratory tests (blood tests, urine tests, tumor markers).
    • Radiologic procedures (imaging like X-rays, CT scans, MRI scans, PET scans, ultrasounds).

An accurate and comprehensive diagnosis is essential for developing an effective treatment plan tailored to the individual patient.

Quick Check!

Which diagnostic step is generally considered the most definitive for confirming a cancer diagnosis and identifying its type?

Correct! A biopsy, where cells or tissues are examined microscopically by a pathologist, is typically the most definitive method for diagnosing cancer.

Page 4: Overview of Cancer Treatment

Once cancer is diagnosed, a treatment plan is developed. Cancer care often involves a multidisciplinary approach, meaning a team of different specialists works together. The main treatment modalities are Surgery, Medical Oncology (like Chemotherapy), and Radiation Oncology, often supported by various support services.

Goals of Treatment:

  • Definitive or Curative Intent: The primary goal is to eradicate the cancer completely and achieve a long-term cure.
  • Palliative Intent: When a cure is not considered possible, the focus shifts to relieving symptoms (like pain), improving quality of life, and potentially prolonging life.

Primary Treatment Modalities:

  • Surgery: Often the treatment of choice for localized, solid tumors. It involves the physical removal of the tumor.
  • Medical Oncology (e.g., Chemotherapy): Uses drugs to kill cancer cells. It is a systemic treatment, meaning it affects cells throughout the body, and is often used for cancers that have spread or for blood cancers.
  • Radiation Therapy: Uses high-energy radiation to shrink tumors and kill cancer cells. It is typically a localized treatment, targeting a specific area of the body.

These modalities can be used alone or in combination, depending on the type and stage of cancer and the patient's overall health.

Page 5: Treatment Timing & Combinations

The timing and combination of different cancer treatments are carefully planned to maximize effectiveness. Several terms describe these approaches:

  • Primary Treatment: This is the main treatment designed to eradicate the cancer or achieve the best chance of a cure. It could be surgery, radiation therapy, or chemotherapy, depending on the cancer type and stage.
  • Concurrent (or Concomitant) Treatment: This involves administering two or more types of treatment at the same time. For example, a patient might receive radiation therapy and chemotherapy simultaneously.
  • Adjuvant Treatment: This refers to additional treatment given *after* the primary treatment has been completed. The goal is to eliminate any remaining cancer cells that might not be detectable and to reduce the risk of recurrence (cancer coming back). Examples include chemotherapy or radiation therapy after surgery.
  • Neoadjuvant Treatment: This is treatment given *before* the primary treatment. The aim is often to shrink a tumor to make the primary treatment (like surgery or radiation) easier, more effective, or less invasive. For example, chemotherapy might be given to reduce the size of a large tumor before surgical removal.

The decision to use these approaches depends on many factors and is made by the oncology team in consultation with the patient.

Page 6: Summary & Next Steps

In this lesson, you've been introduced to the fundamentals of oncology:

  • Oncology is the study and treatment of cancer, which involves uncontrolled cell growth.
  • Tumors can be benign (non-cancerous) or malignant (cancerous, with the ability to metastasize).
  • Diagnosis involves steps like physical exams, health histories, imaging, and crucially, biopsies.
  • Treatment goals can be curative (definitive) or palliative.
  • The main treatment modalities are surgery, medical oncology (e.g., chemotherapy), and radiation therapy, often used in combination.
  • Treatment timing includes primary, concurrent, adjuvant, and neoadjuvant approaches.
