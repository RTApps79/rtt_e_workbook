Oncology comes from "Onc/Onco-" (mass/tumor) and "-ology" (study of). It is the medical specialty focused on cancer.

Page 2: Understanding Cancer - Basic Concepts

Cancer is a group of diseases characterized by uncontrolled cell growth and division. These abnormal cells can form masses called tumors.

Tumors:

  • Benign Tumors: These are non-cancerous. They typically grow slowly, are encapsulated (do not invade surrounding tissue), and do not spread to other parts of the body (metastasize). While usually not life-threatening, they can cause problems due to size or location.
  • Malignant Tumors: These are cancerous. They tend to grow rapidly, invade and destroy nearby healthy tissues, and can metastasize (spread to distant sites via blood or lymph systems) to form secondary tumors. Malignant tumors are life-threatening.

The ability to metastasize is a defining characteristic of malignant cancer.

Quick Check: Which characteristic primarily distinguishes a malignant tumor from a benign tumor?

Correct! The ability to metastasize is a key feature of malignant tumors.

Page 3: Diagnosing Cancer

An accurate cancer diagnosis is crucial for determining the appropriate treatment plan. It typically involves several steps:

Key Components of Diagnosis:

  • Patient History & Physical Exam: The physician gathers information about symptoms, risk factors, family history, and performs a physical examination.
  • Imaging Studies: Various imaging techniques (X-rays, CT, MRI, PET, Ultrasound) are used to visualize suspected tumors, determine their size and location, and check for spread.
  • Laboratory Tests: Blood tests, urine tests, and tests for tumor markers can provide important diagnostic information.
  • Biopsy: This is the definitive diagnostic procedure for most cancers. A sample of suspicious tissue is removed and examined under a microscope by a pathologist to identify cancer cells and determine the cancer type and grade (how aggressive it appears).
  • Staging: Once cancer is diagnosed, staging is performed to determine the extent of the cancer (e.g., size of the primary tumor, lymph node involvement, presence of metastasis). The TNM (Tumor, Nodes, Metastasis) system is commonly used.

Quick Check: The most definitive method for diagnosing cancer is typically:

Correct! A biopsy allows for direct examination of cells by a pathologist.

Page 4: Cancer Treatment Goals & Modalities

Cancer treatment is often managed by a multidisciplinary team and can have different objectives.

Goals of Treatment:

  • Curative (or Definitive) Intent: The primary aim is to completely eradicate the cancer and achieve a cure.
  • Palliative Intent: Focuses on relieving symptoms (like pain or obstruction), improving the patient's quality of life, and potentially prolonging life when a cure is not achievable.

Main Treatment Modalities:

  • Surgery: The physical removal of the tumor. Often a primary treatment for localized solid tumors.
  • Radiation Therapy: Uses high-energy radiation to kill cancer cells and shrink tumors. Can be curative or palliative.
  • Medical Oncology (Systemic Therapies):
    • Chemotherapy: Uses drugs to kill cancer cells throughout the body.
    • Hormone Therapy: Blocks or removes hormones to slow or stop cancer cell growth.
    • Targeted Therapy: Drugs that target specific molecules involved in cancer growth.
    • Immunotherapy: Boosts the body's own immune system to fight cancer.

Often, these modalities are used in combination to achieve the best outcome.

Page 5: Understanding Treatment Timing

The sequence and timing of treatments are critical in oncology.

Key Timing Terminology:

  • Primary Treatment: The main, first-line treatment intended to cure or control the cancer (e.g., surgery to remove a tumor).
  • Adjuvant Therapy: Treatment given after the primary treatment to eliminate any microscopic cancer cells that may remain and to reduce the risk of recurrence (e.g., chemotherapy after breast cancer surgery).
  • Neoadjuvant Therapy: Treatment given before the primary treatment. Its goal is often to shrink a tumor to make surgery less extensive or radiation therapy more effective (e.g., chemotherapy before lung cancer surgery).
  • Concurrent Therapy (or Concomitant Therapy): Administering two or more treatments at the same time (e.g., radiation therapy given together with chemotherapy for head and neck cancer). This can often enhance the effectiveness of both treatments but may also increase side effects.

Quick Check: Radiation therapy given at the same time as chemotherapy is known as:

Correct! Concurrent therapy involves administering multiple treatments simultaneously.

Page 6: Summary & Next Steps

You've now covered the foundational concepts of oncology!

Key Recap:

  • Oncology is the study of cancer, involving diagnosis, treatment, and prevention.
  • Cancer is characterized by uncontrolled cell growth, leading to malignant tumors that can metastasize, unlike benign tumors.
  • Diagnosis relies on patient history, exams, imaging, and definitive biopsies.
  • Treatment goals can be curative or palliative, using modalities like surgery, radiation therapy, and systemic therapies.
  • Treatment timing (primary, adjuvant, neoadjuvant, concurrent) is strategically planned.

This understanding is the bedrock upon which your knowledge of specific radiation therapy techniques and patient care will be built.
