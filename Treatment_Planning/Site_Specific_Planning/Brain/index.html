Page 1: Why CNS Planning Demands Precision

Radiation therapy is a cornerstone of CNS tumor management, but the brain and spinal cord's radiosensitivity makes precise planning and delivery absolutely critical. A multidisciplinary team — neurosurgeons, neurologists, medical oncologists, radiation oncologists, and neuroradiologists — typically manages these cases together, often through a dedicated brain tumor board.

MRI (with and without contrast) is the gold standard for defining tumor volumes and monitoring response; CT is used when MRI is contraindicated. Advanced techniques like MR spectroscopy, MR perfusion, and PET can help distinguish recurrent tumor from radiation necrosis.

Page 2: Positioning and Immobilization

The common CNS setup: supine, headfirst, with a thermoplastic mask, knee bolster, feet banded, and hands clasped or holding a ring. Reproducibility within 2–3 mm is essential, since small setup errors matter enormously this close to critical structures.

Fiducials are placed laterally on each side of the mask (just superior to the ear) and midline anteriorly (superior to the orbits) as consistent reference points. Full documentation — lateral and anterior setup photos, including all immobilization devices and close-ups of fiducials/bolus — is standard practice.

Special considerations: claustrophobic patients may need premedication, an open-face mask, or eyes cut out of the mask, plus extra coaching and updates during treatment. Patients with seizure risk may need a safety strap across the arms and chest.

Page 3: Target Volumes

VolumeDefinition
GTVContrast-enhancing tumor on MRI/CT, or residual visible tumor post-op
CTVGTV expanded by a margin (commonly 1–2 cm for both low- and high-grade disease) to account for microscopic spread, edited off anatomic boundaries
PTVCTV plus a 3–5 mm margin for daily setup error and image registration uncertainty — daily image guidance is required to safely use smaller PTV margins

Quick Check!

What allows a smaller PTV margin to be used safely in CNS treatment?

B. Daily image guidance directly verifies setup each day, which is what justifies tightening the PTV margin.

Page 4: Organ-at-Risk Dose Constraints

Strict dose limits protect critical CNS structures:

StructureConstraint
Optic chiasm / optic nerves50 Gy
Brainstem50 Gy
Spinal cord47 Gy (for 20 cm length)
Uninvolved normal brain45 Gy (necrosis/infarction risk)
Lacrimal gland26 Gy (dry eye)
Parotid32 Gy (xerostomia)

Proton therapy and IMRT are often selected specifically to help meet these OAR constraints while still covering the target.

Page 5: Techniques

Standard fractionated EBRT is the most common approach. Hypofractionated RT suits select patients (older adults, poorer performance status) to shorten overall treatment time. Stereotactic Radiosurgery (SRS) and Stereotactic Body Radiotherapy (SBRT) deliver highly focused, high-dose treatment in 1–5 fractions for small, well-defined lesions — commonly used for brain metastases.

Page 6: Dose and Fractionation by Tumor Type

Different CNS tumor types call for genuinely different dose/fractionation schedules:

Tumor TypeTypical Regimen
Brain stem glioma54 Gy in 30 fractions (1.8 Gy/fx)
Glioblastoma (post-op + chemo)60 Gy in 30 fractions (2 Gy/fx)
Medulloblastoma23.4–36 Gy craniospinal, then boost to 54–55.8 Gy to posterior fossa
Grade I meningioma (SRS)12–16 Gy single fraction, or 25–30 Gy/5 fx near critical structures
Grade II/III meningioma54–60 Gy (Grade II) or 59.4–70 Gy (Grade III), 1.8–2.0 Gy/fx
Metastatic spine (palliative)8 Gy/1 fx, 20 Gy/5 fx, or 30 Gy/10 fx

Quick Check!

Why does medulloblastoma treatment involve craniospinal radiation rather than a focal brain field alone?

A. Medulloblastoma commonly spreads through the cerebrospinal fluid, which is why treatment covers the entire craniospinal axis before boosting the primary site.
