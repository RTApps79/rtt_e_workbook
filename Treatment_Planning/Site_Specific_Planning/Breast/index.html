Page 1: Three Fractionation Approaches

Breast radiation therapy uses several distinct fractionation schedules, each with real tradeoffs:

ScheduleTypical Regimen
Conventional (CFRT)50 Gy in 25 fractions (2 Gy/fx)
Hypofractionation (HFRT)40 Gy in 15 fractions (2.67 Gy/fx)
Accelerated Partial Breast Irradiation (APBI)~30 Gy in 5 fractions (6 Gy/fx)

APBI's much larger fraction size demands ultra-precise delivery techniques (VMAT/SBRT-style precision) to avoid toxic hot spots while still meeting OAR constraints — the margin for error shrinks considerably compared to conventional fractionation.

Page 2: Delivery Techniques

3D-CRT (conventional tangents) uses simple opposed beams — straightforward, but with poorer dose homogeneity and higher heart/lung dose for left-sided tumors specifically.

VMAT/IMRT uses continuous or multiple shaped fields to improve homogeneity and actively sculpt dose around OARs — especially valuable for hypofractionated and APBI schedules, where precision matters most.

Page 3: Deep Inspiration Breath Hold (DIBH)

DIBH is used specifically for left-sided breast treatment — the patient holds a deep breath, which physically pushes the heart away from the chest wall, drastically reducing mean heart dose during treatment.

Quick Check!

DIBH is most relevant for which patients?

B. DIBH specifically addresses the heart's proximity to left-sided treatment fields — right-sided fields don't have the same heart-dose concern.

Page 4: OAR Constraints

StructureConstraint
Heart (left-sided treatment)Mean dose ≤ 4 Gy (goal < 1 Gy with DIBH)
Ipsilateral lungV20 ≤ 10% (whole breast treatment)
Contralateral breastMean dose ≤ 1 Gy

Note that the biological effect (EQD2) of a given physical dose depends heavily on the fractionation schedule — a constraint expressed in physical Gy means something different under conventional vs. hypofractionated vs. APBI schedules.

Quick Check!

What is the goal mean heart dose for a left-sided breast treatment using DIBH?

A. With DIBH actively pulling the heart away from the field, the goal mean heart dose drops to under 1 Gy, well below the 4 Gy constraint without DIBH.

Page 5: Putting It Together — A Planning Decision

Consider a left-sided patient being planned for whole-breast conventional fractionation. The plan will likely use tangent fields (3D-CRT or VMAT), and DIBH should be strongly considered specifically because of the left-sided heart proximity — this is exactly the kind of technique-selection decision that shows up constantly in real planning.

For a patient being considered for APBI instead, the tighter fractionation (5 fractions, larger dose per fraction) raises the bar on setup precision and technique selection well above what conventional whole-breast treatment requires.
