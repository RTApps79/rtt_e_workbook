Page 1: Respiratory Motion Defines This Site

Lung tumors can move a full 1–3 cm (sometimes more) with normal breathing, especially in the lower lobes. Nearly every planning decision for thoracic treatment traces back to managing this motion accurately.

Page 2: 4D-CT and the ITV

4D-CT simulation acquires CT data correlated with the breathing cycle, producing a set of images at different respiratory phases. The tumor position across all phases is combined into an Internal Target Volume (ITV) — the CTV expanded to cover its own physiologic motion, before the setup-margin PTV expansion is added on top.

Quick Check!

What does the ITV specifically account for, that the CTV alone does not?

B. ITV specifically captures internal physiologic motion; setup error is accounted for separately in the PTV margin added on top of the ITV.

Page 4: Target Volumes

Thoracic target volumes follow the same motion-aware sequence as the abdomen, plus a nodal consideration specific to lung disease:

VolumeThorax-specific consideration
GTVPrimary tumor, contoured on lung windows; PET/CT fusion is commonly used to help distinguish tumor from atelectasis (collapsed lung)
CTVGTV plus a margin for microscopic spread; for locally advanced disease, involved mediastinal/hilar nodal stations are included as a separate nodal CTV
ITVCTV expanded to cover the full respiratory motion envelope captured on 4D-CT — often the single largest expansion in the whole sequence for a lower-lobe tumor
PTVITV plus setup margin; for early-stage SBRT, margins are kept as tight as the motion-management technique allows, since the dose per fraction is ablative

Quick Check!

Why might a lower-lobe lung tumor need a larger ITV expansion than an upper-lobe tumor?

A. Tumors closer to the diaphragm generally experience greater respiratory excursion, which directly increases how much the ITV needs to expand to cover that motion.

Lung V20 — the percentage of total lung volume receiving 20 Gy or more — is one of the single most-quoted constraints in thoracic planning, since it correlates directly with pneumonitis risk.

Quick Check!

What does "Lung V20" specifically describe?

A. V20 is a dose-volume metric — the fraction of total lung volume receiving 20 Gy or more, a key predictor of pneumonitis risk.

Page 6: Techniques — Conventional vs. SBRT

Conventionally fractionated IMRT/VMAT (commonly 60–66 Gy in 30–33 fractions) is standard for locally advanced disease. SBRT (commonly 48–54 Gy in 3–5 fractions) has become the standard of care for medically inoperable, early-stage (small, peripheral) lung tumors — delivering ablative dose in far fewer fractions, which demands excellent immobilization, motion management, and image guidance given how little margin for error exists at that dose per fraction.
