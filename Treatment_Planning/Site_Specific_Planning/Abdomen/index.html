Abdomen Treatment Planning

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: The Central Challenge — Motion

Abdominal organs (liver, pancreas, stomach) move substantially with every breath — the liver alone can move 1–2 cm or more with normal respiration. Managing this motion is the defining planning challenge for this site, more than for almost any other.

Page 2: Positioning and Motion Management

Common setup: supine, arms up (out of the beam path), with a knee/ankle support for comfort and reproducibility. IV contrast is frequently used at simulation to help define liver, pancreatic, and vascular anatomy.

4D-CT captures the tumor's full motion envelope across the breathing cycle, letting you contour an ITV (Internal Target Volume) that accounts for that motion directly. Alternative approaches include breath-hold techniques and respiratory gating (treating only during a specific part of the breathing cycle).

Quick Check!

Why is 4D-CT particularly important for abdominal treatment planning?

A. 4D-CT's whole purpose here is capturing respiratory-driven target motion so the ITV can be built to actually cover where the tumor moves, not just where it sits on one snapshot.

Page 3: Target Volumes

Abdominal target volumes follow the standard GTV/CTV/PTV hierarchy, with two site-specific wrinkles:

VolumeAbdomen-specific consideration
GTVDefined on contrast-enhanced CT (or fused MRI/PET) given how much clearer IV contrast makes liver/pancreatic lesions and adjacent vasculature
CTVGTV plus a margin for microscopic spread, often edited off natural anatomic barriers (e.g., not extending CTV into bone or outside the peritoneal cavity where disease can't physically spread)
ITVCTV expanded specifically to cover respiratory motion, built directly from the 4D-CT motion envelope covered on the previous page
PTVITV plus a smaller setup-margin expansion — note that in a moving-target site like this, ITV sits between CTV and PTV in the expansion sequence, unlike a static site where PTV is simply CTV plus one margin

Quick Check!

In abdominal planning, where does the ITV fit in the volume expansion sequence?

A. ITV sits between CTV and PTV — motion is accounted for first (CTV to ITV), then setup uncertainty is added on top (ITV to PTV).

SBRT for liver lesions uses far tighter, ablative constraints given the much higher dose per fraction — a genuinely different planning problem than conventional fractionation.

Page 5: Techniques and Typical Dose

IMRT/VMAT is standard for conventional abdominal treatment (commonly 45–54 Gy in 25–30 fractions for pancreatic/gastric disease), allowing dose to be shaped around the many nearby OARs. SBRT is increasingly used for small, well-defined liver or pancreatic lesions (commonly 30–50 Gy in 3–5 fractions) in a limited number of high-dose fractions, but demands excellent motion management (gating or breath-hold) and image guidance given the ablative dose per fraction.
