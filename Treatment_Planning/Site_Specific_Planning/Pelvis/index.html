Page 1: One Site, Several Disease Types

The pelvis hosts several distinct disease types under one ARRT category: prostate, cervical, uterine, rectal, and anal cancers. The planning mechanics share enough in common — positioning, OARs, bladder/rectum management — to group them together, even though the actual target volumes differ by diagnosis.

Page 2: Supine vs. Prone Positioning

PositionRationale
SupineMost common; comfortable, highly reproducible, compatible with knee/ankle immobilization devices
Prone (with belly board)Used for some rectal/anal treatments — a belly board opening lets the small bowel fall anteriorly, away from the treatment field

Quick Check!

What is the main reason a belly board is used for some rectal treatments?

A. The belly board opening lets small bowel loops fall away from the field anteriorly, directly sparing bowel dose — not just a comfort measure.

Page 3: Bladder and Rectum Protocols

Both bladder and rectal filling state directly affect where pelvic organs sit day to day — this is managed deliberately, not left to chance:

A full-bladder protocol (patient drinks a set amount of water before simulation and every treatment) pushes small bowel superiorly, out of the field, and creates a more reproducible target position. Rectal protocols (empty rectum, sometimes with an enema beforehand) similarly aim for day-to-day consistency.

Page 4: Target Volumes

Because "Pelvis" spans several diagnoses, the GTV/CTV/PTV definitions look genuinely different depending on the disease:

DiagnosisCTV typically includes
ProstateProstate ± seminal vesicles; pelvic nodes if intermediate/high risk
Cervical / UterineUterus/cervix, parametria, upper vagina, and pelvic ± para-aortic nodes depending on stage
Rectal / AnalPrimary tumor/mesorectum plus the draining pelvic (and inguinal, for anal) nodal regions

PTV margins in the pelvis are chosen specifically with the bladder/rectum filling protocols from the previous page in mind — tighter margins are only safe when day-to-day organ filling is well controlled and verified with image guidance.

Quick Check!

Why does the pelvis need larger or smaller PTV margins depending on bladder/rectum protocol adherence?

A. Bladder and rectal filling directly shift pelvic organ position — the more consistent the filling, the more confidently a tighter margin can be used.

For a standard prostate case specifically, the two organs at risk you'll hear referenced constantly are the bladder and rectum — both directly adjacent to the prostate on essentially every side.

Quick Check!

What are the two primary organs at risk in a standard prostate treatment?

A. The bladder and rectum sit immediately adjacent to the prostate and are the two OARs most consistently discussed in prostate planning.

Page 6: Techniques

VMAT/IMRT is standard for definitive pelvic treatment, allowing dose to conform tightly around bladder/rectum. Image guidance (daily CBCT, or fiducial-based kV imaging for prostate) is essential given how much bladder/rectal filling can shift the target day to day. Brachytherapy plays a major role specifically in cervical and some prostate cases, often combined with external beam.
