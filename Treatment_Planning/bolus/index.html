The Role of Bolus in Radiation Therapy

An interactive guide to understanding the "bolus effect" and its importance in treatment planning.

Key Concepts: Dmax & Skin Sparing

High-energy photon beams (like those from a linear accelerator) have a unique characteristic called skin sparing. This means the maximum dose (Dmax) isn't delivered at the skin surface, but at a specific depth underneath it. The dose "builds up" from the surface to this peak, sparing superficial skin layers.

  • Dmax Depth: This depth depends on the beam's energy. Higher energy beams have a deeper Dmax.

Experiment 1: Baseline Observation

On the simulator, set Bolus to 0.0 cm and Energy to 6 MeV. Observe the "Dose Depth Curve".

Notice the low Surface Dose and see how the curve rises to a peak (Dmax) at a depth of about 1.5 cm. This is skin sparing in action.

Experiment 2: Effect of Energy

Now, keep Bolus at 0.0 cm but change the Beam Energy to 18 MeV. How does the Dmax depth change? You'll see it moves much deeper, demonstrating enhanced skin sparing with higher energy.

What is Bolus and How Does It Work?

A bolus is a tissue-equivalent material (like wax or gel) placed on the skin. It acts as an extension of the body's tissue, effectively "pulling" the dose distribution towards the surface.

  • Shifting Dmax: The main job of bolus is to shift the Dmax depth closer to the skin surface. The thickness of the bolus determines the amount of shift.
  • Increasing Surface Dose: By shifting Dmax, bolus deliberately eliminates the skin-sparing effect to treat superficial targets.

Experiment 3: The Bolus Effect

Set the Energy back to 6 MeV. Now, slowly increase the Bolus Thickness. Watch both visualizers:

The Dose Depth Curve shifts to the left, and the Surface Dose value climbs towards 100%. In the Lateral View, you see the dose build-up (red shading) starting within the green bolus layer itself.

Clinical Applications

Bolus is essential when the treatment target is at or very near the skin. Without it, the superficial part of the tumor would be under-dosed.

  • Superficial Tumors: For skin cancers or lesions on the surface.
  • Surgical Scars: To deliver a "boost" dose to a scar area where microscopic disease might remain.
  • Irregular Surfaces: To create a flat, even surface for the beam, ensuring a uniform dose underneath.
