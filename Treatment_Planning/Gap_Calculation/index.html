Field Gap Calculation

Two adjacent fields (for example, when a target is longer than the maximum field) each diverge from their own source. If their edges are placed edge-to-edge on the skin, they spread apart with depth — leaving a cold gap — or, if too close, they overlap into a hot spot. A calculated skin gap makes the diverging edges meet exactly at the chosen depth.

Why the gap exists — and the danger of getting it wrong

Each field's edge is defined by a ray diverging from its source, so a field of length L projects half-widths that fan outward by ½L × d/SSD at depth d. Two fields set edge-to-edge on the skin therefore separate underneath, under-dosing the junction. Adding the calculated skin gap lets both diverging edges arrive at the same point at depth d. Set the gap too small and the fields overlap into a hot spot; too large and a cold spot under-doses the target. In practice junctions are often feathered/moved during treatment to spread any residual dose error. This is the same beam divergence behind magnification and field enlargement at extended distance.
