Isodose Distribution

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: SSD vs. SAD Treatment Approaches

Two setup approaches define how a beam is positioned relative to the patient:

ApproachReference point
SSD (Source-to-Skin Distance)The distance from source to the patient's skin surface is fixed (commonly 100 cm); depth is measured from there
SAD (Source-to-Axis Distance)The distance from source to isocenter is fixed (commonly 100 cm); isocenter sits at a defined depth inside the patient, and the couch/gantry rotate around it

Multi-field, multi-angle plans overwhelmingly use SAD/isocentric technique, since it lets the gantry rotate to different angles without having to re-measure SSD at every new angle.

Page 2: Depth, Separation, and Key Terms

Depth for dose calculation is commonly taken at Dmax, at a specific prescription depth, or at the patient's midline. Separation (IFD) is the patient's thickness in the beam direction — surface-to-surface, either AP/PA or lateral — and it directly determines where midline and isocenter actually sit.

Isocenter is the fixed point in space the beam's central axis passes through regardless of gantry angle. Isodose lines/curves connect points receiving the same percentage of dose, and the full isodose distribution is the 2D or 3D map of dose throughout the treated volume — the entire subject of this lesson.

Page 3: Percentage Depth Dose (PDD)

Percentage Depth Dose expresses dose at any depth as a percentage of the dose at Dmax, for a fixed SSD. PDD tables are built per beam energy and field size — larger fields and higher energies both produce higher PDD at a given depth (more forward-scattered dose, deeper penetration).

Worked example: A dose of 2500 cGy is prescribed at 6.5 cm depth, where PDD = 62.5%. Given Dose (at Dmax) = 2500 / 0.625 = 4000 cGy.

Quick Check!

As field size increases, what generally happens to PDD at a given depth?

A. Larger fields produce more forward/lateral scatter contributing to dose at depth, increasing PDD.

Page 4: Extended SSD and Mayneord's F Factor

If a patient must be treated at a non-standard SSD (extended beyond the calibration distance), the PDD table values no longer directly apply — the beam's inverse-square falloff and scatter conditions have both changed.

Mayneord's F Factor corrects PDD between two different SSDs, combining an inverse-square correction with a scatter correction. A helpful mnemonic: "old and deep" (old SSD + depth) times "new and short" — capturing the ratio structure of the formula. Extending the SSD generally makes the beam "colder" (lower output) at a given point unless compensated for.

Page 5: TMR, TPR, and SAR

For SAD/isocentric technique, dose is calculated differently than for SSD technique, since the reference point (isocenter) is fixed inside the patient rather than at the skin:

  • TMR (Tissue-Maximum Ratio) — compares dose at a given depth to dose at Dmax, both at the same distance from the source, independent of SSD. This is the standard tool for SAD calculations.
  • TPR (Tissue-Phantom Ratio) — a related ratio using a reference depth other than Dmax.
  • SAR (Scatter-Air Ratio) — isolates the scatter contribution to dose, used in the Clarkson method for irregular field calculations, where the field is broken into sectors and scatter is summed sector-by-sector.

Page 6: Rotational and Arc Treatments

In a rotational/arc treatment, the beam stays on while the gantry moves continuously — a full rotation is 360°; an arc is any span less than that, with a defined start and stop angle.

Dose is delivered as cGy per degree of rotation. Depth for a rotational field is calculated using an average TAR, obtained by measuring tumor depth/radius at multiple angular intervals around the full rotation and averaging the resulting TAR values — since the beam path length through the patient changes continuously as the gantry rotates.

Quick Check!

Why is an "average TAR" used for rotational field dose calculations rather than a single TAR value?

A. As the gantry rotates, the beam's path length to the tumor changes continuously, so an average across multiple angles better represents the actual dose delivered.

Page 7: Gap Calculations for Abutting Fields

When two adjacent fields are needed to cover a target too large for one field, they can be abutted — but because photon beams diverge from their source, simply placing field edges together on the skin would create a hot spot at depth (where the diverging beams overlap) or a cold spot (where they diverge apart). A calculated gap on the skin surface compensates for this divergence.

Gap = (L1/2)(d/SSD1) + (L2/2)(d/SSD2), where L is each field's length, d is the depth where the fields should meet exactly, and SSD is each field's source-to-skin distance. Electron fields are also commonly abutted, using similar logic adapted for electron beam characteristics.

Page 8: Entrance and Exit Dose

In an opposed-field setup (e.g., AP/PA), each beam delivers its highest dose near Dmax on the entrance side, then falls off with depth — but some dose still exits the far side of the patient as the beam continues past isocenter. This exit dose from the opposing beam adds to the entrance dose of the beam on that side, which is exactly why opposed pairs of fields combine to deliver more uniform dose across the target than a single field ever could.

Page 9: Bolus

Bolus is a tissue-equivalent material placed directly on the patient's skin to modify the dose distribution — specifically to eliminate the skin-sparing effect when a superficial target needs full dose at the surface, or to compensate for irregular patient contours.
