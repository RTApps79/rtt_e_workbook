Page 1: Two Central Challenges — Motion and Heterogeneity

Thoracic planning is defined by two problems working at once, which is what sets it apart from most other sites:

  • Respiratory motion. Lung and mediastinal tumors travel with every breath. Lower-lobe lesions near the diaphragm can move well over a centimeter, so a single planning snapshot does not tell you where the target actually spends its time.
  • Tissue heterogeneity. The beam crosses low-density lung on its way to the target. The treatment planning system must apply heterogeneity (density) correction — calculating dose as if the chest were uniform water overestimates tumor dose and misrepresents the penumbra at the tumor–lung interface.

Layered on top of both is the crowd of critical structures packed into a small volume: heart, esophagus, spinal cord, and the uninvolved lung itself.

Page 2: Positioning and Motion Management

Common setup: supine, both arms raised above the head on a wingboard or T-bar grips (clearing the lateral and oblique beam paths), immobilized on a chest board or vac-lok cushion for day-to-day reproducibility.

4D-CT characterizes the tumor's motion envelope across the breathing cycle so an ITV (Internal Target Volume) can be built directly from where the target actually travels. Motion can also be reduced rather than just measured: deep inspiration breath-hold (DIBH) holds the target still and pushes the heart away from the chest wall, respiratory gating treats only during a chosen phase, and abdominal compression limits diaphragm excursion.

Quick Check!

Beyond holding the target still, what added benefit makes deep inspiration breath-hold (DIBH) especially attractive for many thoracic and left-sided cases?

A. On inspiration the expanded lung pushes the heart inferiorly and away from the chest wall, so DIBH both freezes motion and physically moves critical structures out of the high-dose region. Heterogeneity correction is a dose-calculation step and is still required regardless of breathing technique.

Page 3: Target Volumes

Thoracic targets follow the standard GTV/CTV/ITV/PTV hierarchy, with contouring nuances specific to the chest:

VolumeThorax-specific consideration
GTVContoured using both windows: the lung window for the parenchymal tumor edge and the mediastinal (soft-tissue) window for nodal and mediastinal extension. PET/CT fusion further clarifies active disease from atelectasis
CTVGTV plus a margin for microscopic spread, edited off anatomic barriers the tumor cannot cross (chest wall, intact bone, major vessels)
ITVCTV expanded to cover the respiratory motion measured on 4D-CT — lower-lobe tumors require a larger ITV than upper-lobe tumors because they move more with the diaphragm
PTVITV plus a setup-uncertainty margin. As with any moving-target site, ITV sits between CTV and PTV in the expansion sequence

Quick Check!

Why is the GTV contoured on both lung and mediastinal windows?

A. Each window reveals a different tissue interface — the parenchymal edge is clearest on lung window, soft-tissue and nodal disease on mediastinal window. Relying on one alone systematically mis-sizes the GTV.

SBRT for early-stage peripheral disease uses far tighter, ablative constraints given the very high dose per fraction — a genuinely different planning problem from conventional fractionation, with particular caution near the central "no-fly zone" around the proximal bronchial tree.

Page 5: Techniques and Typical Dose

ARRT explicitly distinguishes two thoracic treatment categories, and it is worth understanding why each is chosen:

Multi-field (non-IMRT) 3D-CRT

Classic approach using a handful of shaped static fields — for example, AP/PA fields to roughly 40–45 Gy, then off-cord oblique fields to boost the target while dropping the spinal cord out of the high-dose beam. Field shapes are drawn in beam's-eye-view with the MLC.

IMRT / Volumetric Arc Therapy (VMAT)

Modulated delivery gives better conformality around concave targets and tighter sparing when heart, cord, and esophagus crowd the target. The tradeoff is a larger low-dose bath (high V5) spread across normal lung, which can itself raise pneumonitis risk — so the "more modulation" choice is not automatically the safer one in the chest.

Typical dose: definitive NSCLC is commonly 60–66 Gy in 30–33 fractions (2 Gy/fx), frequently with concurrent chemotherapy. SBRT for early-stage, medically inoperable peripheral tumors is commonly 48–54 Gy in 3–5 fractions, with risk-adapted fractionation (e.g., 50 Gy in 5) for central lesions.

Quick Check!

What is the main tradeoff of choosing IMRT/VMAT over multi-field 3D-CRT in the thorax?

A. Modulation buys conformality and sparing of crowded OARs at the cost of spreading low dose more widely through healthy lung, which matters for pneumonitis risk. Heterogeneity correction applies to both techniques.
