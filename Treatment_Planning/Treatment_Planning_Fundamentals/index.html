Page 2: Simulation Covers Seven Anatomic Locations

Simulation (using CT, MRI, and/or PET) is organized around seven anatomic regions: Brain, Head and Neck, Thorax, Breast, Abdomen, Pelvis, and Skeletal — the exact same seven sites this workbook's Site-Specific Planning section is built around.

The goal of simulation: identify the affected area, determine the optimum treatment position, identify the target volume and adjacent normal organs relative to external skin marks, use that information for dosimetry planning, and document everything for reproducible daily setup.

Page 3: The Nine-Step CT Simulation Procedure

  1. Presimulation planning
  2. Room preparation
  3. Explanation of procedure (to the patient)
  4. Patient positioning and immobilization
  5. CT data acquisition
  6. Target and normal tissue localization
  7. Virtual simulation of treatment fields
  8. Generation of dose distributions
  9. Documentation of pertinent data

Positioning is the foundation of everything downstream — good setups and accurate treatment delivery start with good simulation and immobilization, whether that's simple (a headrest) or complex (a custom thermoplastic mask).

Page 4: Contrast and Localization

Contrast media (barium sulfate, iodinated contrast, negative contrast agents) can help localize tumor volume and normal critical structures that would otherwise be hard to distinguish on CT. Careful patient evaluation is needed beforehand, since some agents carry a real risk of severe allergic reaction.

Localization is the geometric definition of the position and extent of the tumor or anatomical structures, referenced to surface marks used for treatment setup — it ends in the placement of isocenter.

Quick Check!

What must be checked before administering contrast media during simulation?

B. Some contrast agents carry real allergic reaction risk, so careful patient evaluation is required before administration.

Page 5: Contouring and ICRU Treatment Planning Volumes

Contouring is the reproduction of a body shape and internal structures, typically drawn through the transverse plane of the treatment beam. Once contoured, the ICRU treatment planning volumes build on each other:

VolumeDefinition
GTV / CTV / PTVThe core target hierarchy (see the Brain planning lesson for a worked example)
Treated VolumeEncompasses additional margin around the target volume to account for limitations in treatment technique
Irradiated VolumeThe volume of tissue receiving a significant dose (>50% of the specified target dose) — tissue in front of, behind, and around the target

Page 6: Motion and Measuring Separation

Interfraction motion is the change in target position from one treatment day to another. Intrafraction motion is the change in target position during a single treatment delivery — respiratory motion in the thorax is the classic example.

IFD (separation) — the patient's thickness along the central axis — is most often measured directly from CT images today, though it can also be measured manually with a caliper (a sliding-leg ruler). Separation is used to help determine SSD.

Page 7: DVH and the Treatment Plan

The Dose Volume Histogram (DVH) graphs radiation dose against tissue volume — the standard tool for evaluating a plan and comparing doses between different plans or structures (directly related to the therapeutic ratio concept).

A complete treatment plan specifies: number of fields, gantry/collimator/couch angles, beam energy, field size, MLC blocking, beam modifiers, monitor units, isocenter, and the DVH itself.

Page 8: Verification

Verification is the final check before treatment is actually delivered. A Digitally Reconstructed Radiograph (DRR) is a computer-generated emulation of a radiograph from the beam's-eye view, used to compare against images taken on the actual treatment machine — confirming the anatomy-to-beam relationship matches the plan.

Orthogonal images are two images taken 90° apart, verifying alignment in two planes at once rather than just one.

Quick Check!

Why are orthogonal images (90° apart) used for verification rather than a single image?

A. One image alone can't rule out a positioning error along the direction the beam travels — a second image from a different angle is needed to confirm 3D alignment.

Page 9: Body Planes and Field Size

AP images show anatomy in the coronal plane (inferior/superior and left/right). Lateral images show anatomy in the sagittal plane (inferior/superior and anterior/posterior).

Field size is defined by the secondary collimator dimensions (width × length) — on the skin surface for SSD technique, or at isocenter for SAD technique. This is the same distinction covered in the Isodose Distribution lesson.
