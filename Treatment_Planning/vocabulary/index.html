Key Terms

Dose: The energy deposited at a specific point in a medium.
Depth (d): The distance beneath the skin surface where the prescribed dose is delivered.
Separation (IFD): The measurement of a patient's thickness from beam entry to beam exit.
Dmax: The depth at which electronic equilibrium is reached and the maximum dose occurs.
Source-Skin Distance (SSD): Distance from the radiation source to the patient's skin.
Source-Axis Distance (SAD): Distance from the radiation source to the isocenter.
Isocenter: A fixed point in space around which the gantry, collimator, and couch rotate.
Dose Rate / Machine Output: The amount of radiation exposure produced by a machine at a reference point.
Percentage Depth Dose (PDD): The ratio of the absorbed dose at a given depth to the absorbed dose at Dmax.
Tissue-Air Ratio (TAR): The ratio of the dose in tissue to the dose in free air at the same point.
Tissue-Phantom Ratio (TPR): The ratio of the absorbed dose at a depth in a phantom to the dose at a reference depth in the same phantom.
Tissue-Maximum Ratio (TMR): A special case of TPR where the reference depth is Dmax.
Backscatter Factor (BSF): The ratio of the dose rate at Dmax with a scattering medium to the dose rate in air.
Collimator Scatter Factor (Sc): Scatter radiation originating in the collimator system.
Phantom Scatter Factor (Sp): Scatter radiation originating from within the patient or phantom.
Equivalent Square (EqSq): The side length of a square field that has the same scatter characteristics as a given rectangular or irregular field.

What is Dose?

[cite_start]

Dose refers to the energy deposited at a specific point in a medium[cite: 28, 29]. It is a measure of the absorbed energy per unit mass of tissue.

    [cite_start]
  • Measured in Gray (Gy)[cite: 30].
    • [cite_start]
  • $1 \text{ Gy} = 100 \text{ rads}$ [cite: 31]
    • [cite_start]
  • $1 \text{ Gy} = 1 \text{ Joule/kg}$ [cite: 32]
    • [cite_start]
  • $1 \text{ rad} = 100 \text{ erg/g}$ [cite: 33]

The Prescription

[cite_start]

The Radiation Oncologist provides a treatment prescription that specifies the treatment plan[cite: 36]. This includes:

    [cite_start]
  • Treatment Site: The region of the tumor and any presumed surrounding tumor cells[cite: 37].
    • [cite_start]
  • Total Tumor Dose: The total amount of radiation to be delivered[cite: 38].
    • [cite_start]
  • Number of Treatments (Fractions): How many sessions the total dose is divided into[cite: 39].
    • [cite_start]
  • Dose per Treatment: The dose delivered in each session[cite: 40].
    • [cite_start]
  • Frequency: How often treatments are given (e.g., daily)[cite: 41].
[cite_start]$$ \text{Number of Treatments} = \frac{\text{Total Dose}}{\text{Dose per Fraction}} $$ [cite: 43, 44]

Example Calculation

[cite_start]

If the Total Dose is 60 Gy and the Dose per Fraction is 2.0 Gy: [cite: 45]

[cite_start]

$$ \frac{60 \text{ Gy}}{2.0 \text{ Gy/Fraction}} = 30 \text{ Treatments} $$ [cite: 46]

[cite_start]

The patient receives 30 treatments at 2.0 Gy per fraction for a total dose of 60 Gy[cite: 47].

Patient and Beam Geometry

Separation (IFD)

[cite_start]

Separation, also known as Intrafield Distance (IFD), is the measurement of the patient's thickness from the point of beam entry to the point of beam exit, measured along the beam's central axis[cite: 49, 50].

Depth (d)

[cite_start]

Depth is the distance beneath the skin surface where the prescribed dose is to be delivered[cite: 55]. [cite_start]This can be a specified depth, at the midplane, or at the isocenter [cite: 56-58].

Dmax (Depth of Maximum Dose)

[cite_start]

Dmax is the depth where the maximum radiation dose occurs[cite: 61]. [cite_start]It's the point where secondary electrons, set in motion by high-energy photons, reach a state of equilibrium[cite: 62, 66].

    [cite_start]
  • The region before Dmax is called the buildup region[cite: 63].
    • [cite_start]
  • The phenomenon of the dose being lower on the skin surface and building up to Dmax is known as the skin-sparing effect[cite: 64].

Machine Output / Dose Rate

[cite_start]

Dose Rate, or Machine Output, is the amount of radiation exposure produced by a treatment machine, specified at a reference field size and distance[cite: 86, 93, 95]. [cite_start]It's typically measured in a tissue-equivalent material[cite: 94]. [cite_start]The standard dose rate for LINACs is often calibrated as 1 cGy/MU for a 10x10 cm field at Dmax at a 100 cm distance[cite: 97].

Factors Affecting Dose Rate

    [cite_start]
  • Field Size: As field size increases, dose rate increases due to increased scatter radiation from the collimator (Sc) and the phantom/patient (Sp)[cite: 104, 105, 107, 109].
    • [cite_start]
  • Distance: As distance from the source increases, dose rate decreases due to the divergence of the beam (Inverse Square Law)[cite: 112, 113].
  • Beam Energy: Higher energy beams have different scatter and penetration characteristics, affecting the dose rate.

Inverse Square Law (ISL)

[cite_start]

The Inverse Square Law describes how the intensity of radiation decreases with the square of the distance from the source[cite: 127]. If you double the distance, the intensity becomes one-quarter of the original.

[cite_start]$$ \frac{I_1}{I_2} = \frac{(d_2)^2}{(d_1)^2} $$ [cite: 129]

This can also be written as:

[cite_start]$$ I_2 = I_1 \left( \frac{d_1}{d_2} \right)^2 $$ [cite: 191]

Equivalent Squares of Rectangular Fields

For dose calculation purposes, rectangular fields are often converted to their equivalent square field size, which is the square field that has the same scatter characteristics.

Sterling’s Approximation Formula

[cite_start]

A common method to calculate the equivalent square (EqSq) of a rectangular field[cite: 134].

[cite_start]$$ \text{EqSq} = \frac{4 \times \text{Area}}{\text{Perimeter}} = \frac{4 \times (W \times L)}{2 \times (W + L)} = \frac{2 \times (W \times L)}{W + L} $$ [cite: 135, 138-141]
[cite_start]

Example: Calculate Equivalent Square for a 25 x 5 cm field [cite: 145]

$$ \text{EqSq} = \frac{2 \times (25 \times 5)}{25 + 5} = \frac{2 \times 125}{30} = \frac{250}{30} \approx 8.33 \text{ cm} $$ [cite_start]

So, a 25x5 cm field is equivalent to an 8.33 x 8.33 cm square field[cite: 146].

Effective Field Size (FSeff)

When blocks are used to shape the beam, the field size is reduced. [cite_start]The effective field size is the equivalent square of the open (unblocked) area of the field[cite: 152, 179].

Tissue Absorption Factors

These factors account for how the radiation dose is attenuated and scattered within the patient. The choice of which factor to use depends on the treatment technique.

For Nonisocentric (SSD) Technique:
    [cite_start]
  • Percentage Depth Dose (PDD) [cite: 194, 195]
For Isocentric (SAD) Technique:
    [cite_start]
  • Tissue-Air Ratio (TAR) [cite: 197]
    • [cite_start]
  • Tissue-Phantom Ratio (TPR) [cite: 199]
    • [cite_start]
  • Tissue-Maximum Ratio (TMR) [cite: 198]

SSD vs. SAD Technique

Source-Skin Distance (SSD)

The distance from the machine's source to the patient's skin is kept constant (e.g., 100 cm). The patient is moved for each new beam angle. [cite_start]Dose is specified at a certain depth[cite: 208, 210].

Source-Axis Distance (SAD)

The distance from the source to the isocenter is constant. The isocenter is placed within the patient, and the machine rotates around this point. The SSD changes with each beam angle. [cite_start]Dose is normalized to the isocenter[cite: 211, 212].

Percentage Depth Dose (PDD)

PDD is the ratio, expressed as a percentage, of the absorbed dose at a given depth (d) to the absorbed dose at a fixed reference depth of Dmax, measured along the central axis of the beam.

$$ PDD = \frac{\text{Dose at depth d}}{\text{Dose at } D_{max}} \times 100\% $$

PDD is dependent on the following factors:

    [cite_start]
  • Increases with: Energy, Field Size, SSD [cite: 223-226]
    • [cite_start]
  • Decreases with: Depth [cite: 227, 228]

Mayneord’s F Factor

[cite_start]

This factor is used to convert the PDD from a standard SSD to a new, non-standard (often extended) SSD[cite: 230, 231].

$$ PDD_{new} = PDD_{old} \times F_{\text{factor}} $$ $$ F_{\text{factor}} = \frac{[(SSD_1 + d) / (SSD_1 + D_{max})]^2}{[(SSD_2 + d) / (SSD_2 + D_{max})]^2} $$

Tissue-Air Ratio (TAR)

TAR is the ratio of the dose at a given point in a phantom to the dose measured in free air at the same point. [cite_start]It is used for calculations in SAD treatments, particularly with lower-energy machines like Cobalt-60[cite: 238].

$$ TAR = \frac{\text{Dose in tissue}}{\text{Dose in air}} $$

TAR is dependent on the following factors:

    [cite_start]
  • Increases with: Energy, Field Size [cite: 239-241]
    • [cite_start]
  • Decreases with: Depth [cite: 242, 243]

Tissue-Phantom Ratio (TPR) and Tissue-Maximum Ratio (TMR)

[cite_start]

TPR is the ratio of the dose at a given depth in a phantom to the dose at a fixed reference depth in the same phantom[cite: 246].

TMR is a special case of TPR where the reference depth is Dmax. [cite_start]TMR is the most commonly used factor for high-energy linac calculations (SAD technique)[cite: 247].

$$ TPR = \frac{\text{Dose in tissue at depth d}}{\text{Dose in phantom at ref. depth}} $$ $$ TMR = \frac{\text{Dose in tissue at depth d}}{\text{Dose in phantom at } D_{max}} $$

TMR/TPR are dependent on the following factors:

    [cite_start]
  • Increases with: Energy, Field Size [cite: 248-250]
    • [cite_start]
  • Decreases with: Depth [cite: 251, 252]

Scatter and Backscatter

Scatter-Air Ratio (SAR)

[cite_start]

This is the ratio of the scattered dose at a point in a phantom to the dose in free space at the same point[cite: 259]. It helps in calculating dose for irregular fields.

Backscatter Factor (BSF)

[cite_start]

BSF is the ratio of the dose rate at Dmax in a phantom to the dose rate at the same point in air[cite: 262]. [cite_start]It is essentially the TAR measured at the depth of Dmax[cite: 263].

    [cite_start]
  • Dependent on energy and field size, but not SSD[cite: 264].
    • [cite_start]
  • BSF is higher for low energies and decreases as beam energy increases[cite: 265, 266].

Advanced Calculations

Gap Calculation for Abutting Fields

[cite_start]

When two radiation fields are placed next to each other, a gap must be calculated on the patient's skin to ensure the fields meet seamlessly at a specific depth, preventing hot or cold spots due to beam divergence[cite: 285, 288].

[cite_start]$$ \text{Gap} = \left( \frac{L_1}{2} \times \frac{d}{SSD_1} \right) + \left( \frac{L_2}{2} \times \frac{d}{SSD_2} \right) $$ [cite: 290]

Example Gap Calculation

[cite_start]

Find the gap on the skin to match two fields at a depth (d) of 8 cm[cite: 291].

    [cite_start]
  • Field 1: L1 = 20 cm, SSD1 = 100 cm [cite: 292, 293]
    • [cite_start]
  • Field 2: L2 = 30 cm, SSD2 = 100 cm [cite: 294, 295]

$$ \text{Gap} = \left( \frac{20}{2} \times \frac{8}{100} \right) + \left( \frac{30}{2} \times \frac{8}{100} \right) $$

$$ \text{Gap} = (10 \times 0.08) + (15 \times 0.08) $$

[cite_start]

$$ \text{Gap} = 0.8 \text{ cm} + 1.2 \text{ cm} = 2.0 \text{ cm} $$ [cite: 298]

Wedge Pairs

Wedges are beam modifiers that create a dose gradient across the field. [cite_start]They are often used in pairs to treat volumes with angled beams, creating a more uniform dose distribution[cite: 300].

The Hinge Angle is the angle between the central axes of two beams. The required Wedge Angle is related to the hinge angle.

[cite_start]

$$ \text{Wedge Angle} = 90^\circ - (0.5 \times \text{Hinge Angle}) $$ [cite: 301]

[cite_start]

$$ \text{Hinge Angle} = 180^\circ - (2 \times \text{Wedge Angle}) $$ [cite: 302]
