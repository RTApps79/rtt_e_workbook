Extended Distance (SSD) Correction

Some treatments — total body irradiation, large fields — are delivered at an extended SSD, far beyond the distance where the machine was calibrated. Because output follows the inverse square law, you must correct for it. This is inverse square applied to the treatment machine.

Step 1 — Read the problem and identify the variables

Before calculating, map each variable in the formula to the correct value from the scenario. The calculator below unlocks once your reading is correct.

At extended SSD the field is larger but the output per MU is lower — the same photons cover more area.

Why this matters in the clinic

Linacs are calibrated at a standard SSD (typically 100 cm). When a treatment requires a much larger field than the machine can produce at that distance — classically total body irradiation (TBI) — the patient is moved far from the source so the diverging beam covers the whole body. At that extended distance the output per monitor unit drops by the inverse-square factor, so the monitor units (or time) must be increased to deliver the prescribed dose. The field also enlarges in direct proportion to distance, which is exactly the geometry behind the inverse square law and, on the imaging side, magnification.
