Part 1, Page 1: Medical Terms Are Built, Not Memorized

Medical language looks intimidating until you realize it's built from a small, reusable set of pieces — mostly derived from Greek and Latin — combined the same way every time. Once you know the pieces, you can decode terms you've never seen before, rather than memorizing an endless vocabulary list.

Every medical term is built from some combination of: a root (the core meaning, usually a body part or concept), a prefix (attached to the front, modifying meaning), a suffix (attached to the end, often indicating a condition or procedure), and sometimes a combining vowel (usually "o") that links pieces together for pronunciation.

Part 1, Page 2: Roots and Combining Vowels

The root carries the core meaning — often a body part (cardi = heart, hepat = liver, nephr = kidney). When a root is joined to another root, or to a suffix that starts with a consonant, a combining vowel (almost always "o") is inserted to make the word pronounceable.

If the suffix already starts with a vowel, the combining vowel is usually dropped. Example: arthr/o (joint) + -itis (inflammation) → arthritis, not "arthroitis" — the vowel is dropped because -itis already starts with a vowel.

Quick Check!

The prefix "brady-" means:

B. Brady- means slow (e.g., bradycardia = slow heart rate) — the opposite, tachy-, means fast.

Part 1, Page 5: Decoding a Real Term

Let's put the pieces together. Take the word pericarditis:

peri (prefix: around) + card (root: heart) + itis (suffix: inflammation of)
= "inflammation around the heart"

This is the whole skill in a nutshell: break an unfamiliar term into its pieces, translate each piece, and reassemble the meaning — no memorization of the whole word required.

Quick Check!

Break down "hepatomegaly": hepat/o (liver) + -megaly (enlargement). What does it mean?

B. Hepat/o = liver, -megaly = enlargement, so hepatomegaly literally means "enlarged liver."

Part 1, Page 6: Levels of Body Organization

The body is organized in a clear hierarchy, smallest to largest: cells → tissues (groups of similar cells) → organs (structures made of multiple tissue types working together) → organ systems (organs working toward a shared function) → the organism as a whole.

Knowing this hierarchy helps you place any new term in context — is it describing something at the cellular level, or an entire organ system?

Part 1, Page 7: Body Cavities and Regions

The body has major cavities that house organs and protect them: the cranial and spinal cavities (dorsal body cavity, housing the brain and spinal cord), and the thoracic, abdominal, and pelvic cavities (ventral body cavity, housing the heart/lungs, digestive organs, and reproductive/urinary organs respectively).

Body regions further divide the body into named areas (e.g., abdominal quadrants) — a shared reference vocabulary that lets clinicians describe a location precisely without ambiguity.

Part 1, Page 8: Directional Terms

Directional terms describe location relative to a standard reference position, and they're used constantly in setup documentation:

Quick Check!

A lesion described as "medial to the nipple" is located:

A. Medial means toward the midline — the opposite, lateral, would mean away from the midline.

Part 1, Page 9: Body Planes

Body planes are imaginary flat surfaces used to describe cross-sectional views — the exact same planes you already work with in CT simulation and treatment planning:

  • Sagittal plane — divides the body into left and right
  • Frontal (coronal) plane — divides the body into anterior and posterior
  • Transverse (axial) plane — divides the body into superior and inferior

These aren't just anatomy-class vocabulary — they're literally the same three planes your CT/simulation and treatment planning images are reconstructed in.

Part 1, Page 10: Transition to Oncology Terminology

You've now covered the general skeleton of medical language: word components, prefixes, suffixes, decoding real terms, body organization, directional terms, and planes. This vocabulary applies across every area of medicine.

From here, the lesson shifts to vocabulary that's specifically built around cancer diagnosis, treatment, and documentation — the terminology you'll actually be reading and writing daily as a radiation therapist.

Part 2, Page 1: Why Oncology Has Its Own Vocabulary

Cancer diagnosis, treatment, and tumor registry documentation rely on a specialized set of word roots and suffixes layered on top of general medical terminology. This section draws on the U.S. National Cancer Institute's SEER Program tumor registrar training materials — a public resource specifically built to teach exactly this vocabulary.

The same rule applies here as in Part 1: you're not memorizing whole words, you're learning a smaller set of pieces that recombine constantly across cancer-related terms.

Quick Check!

The root carcin/o literally translates to which word?

A. Carcin/o comes from the Greek word for "crab" — the ancient association was the crab-like, spreading appearance of some tumors. Sarc/o is the one meaning "flesh."

Part 2, Page 3: -oma vs. Carcinoma vs. Sarcoma

This distinction matters clinically, not just linguistically:

-oma alone just means "tumor" — it doesn't say benign or malignant (e.g., a lipoma, a benign fatty tumor).
Carcinoma = a malignant tumor of epithelial origin (skin, glands, linings of organs) — the most common category of cancer.
Sarcoma = a malignant tumor of connective tissue origin (bone, muscle, fat, cartilage) — much less common than carcinoma, but distinct enough to always be named separately.

So "osteosarcoma" (oste/o = bone + sarcoma) is a malignant bone tumor, while "adenocarcinoma" (aden/o = gland + carcinoma) is a malignant tumor arising from glandular tissue.

Quick Check!

A patient's chart notes "esophageal stenosis." What is happening to the esophagus?

A. -stenosis means narrowing/stricture — the opposite process, -ectasis, means expansion or dilation.

Part 2, Page 6: Growth and Formation Terms

A cluster of related suffixes describe tissue growth patterns — and the differences between them matter enormously in oncology:

-plasia (formation, growth): hyperplasia = increased number of normal cells (usually benign); dysplasia = abnormal cell changes (potentially precancerous); neoplasia = new, uncontrolled growth (the general process behind tumor formation, benign or malignant).

-plasm (growth, formation, a formed substance): as in neoplasm, a new abnormal growth of tissue — the general clinical term for what's colloquially called a "tumor."

-genic (producing, forming): as in carcinogenic (cancer-causing) or radiogenic.

-oid (resembling, like): as in lymphoid (resembling lymphatic tissue).

Break down "chondrosarcoma": chondr/o means cartilage. What does the full term mean?

A. Chondr/o (cartilage) + sarcoma (malignant connective-tissue tumor) = a malignant cartilage tumor.

Part 2, Page 8: Abbreviations in Cancer Documentation

Beyond full terms, oncology charting relies heavily on standardized abbreviations — staging notation (T/N/M), grading (G1–G4), and treatment-related shorthand you'll see constantly in a chart. Getting comfortable reading these quickly is just as important as knowing the full-word vocabulary, since most day-to-day documentation uses the abbreviated form.
