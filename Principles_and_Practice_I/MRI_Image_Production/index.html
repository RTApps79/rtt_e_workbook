MRI overview

MRI uses a strong magnetic field, radiofrequency pulses, and gradient fields to create images with excellent soft-tissue contrast and no ionizing radiation [file:20].

  • The magnet aligns hydrogen nuclei.
  • RF pulses disturb that alignment.
  • Signals are received and converted into images.

Magnet and spin

The main magnet aligns hydrogen nuclei so their tiny magnetic moments point in a preferred direction. This creates the starting condition needed for signal generation [file:20].

  • Protons behave like tiny magnets.
  • Without the main field, their directions are more random.
  • Alignment gives MRI its measurable signal source.

RF excitation

A radiofrequency pulse tips the aligned spins away from equilibrium so they can generate a detectable signal as they return toward alignment [file:20].

  • RF energy excites the spins.
  • The flip angle describes how far they are tipped.
  • The system then listens for the returning signal.
Check: What happens after the spins are tipped by the RF pulse?

Relaxation and signal

Once the RF pulse stops, the spins relax back toward equilibrium and emit signal that the receiver coil can detect. T1 and T2 behavior help determine how bright or dark tissues appear [file:20].

  • Relaxation is the return to baseline.
  • Longitudinal and transverse behaviors differ.
  • Signal decay is part of normal MR physics.

Gradients and encoding

Gradient coils create small, controlled changes in magnetic field strength so the scanner can determine where each signal originated from. This is how MRI creates spatial information [file:20].

  • Gradients vary the field across space.
  • They enable frequency and phase encoding.
  • They are essential for making images from raw signal.
Check: What problem do gradients solve during image formation?

Slice selection

By combining a gradient with a carefully timed RF pulse, the scanner excites only a selected slice of tissue. This limits the signal to a thin section of the body [file:20].

  • The slice is chosen by frequency and field location.
  • Only spins in the targeted slice are excited.
  • That lets the scanner build image by image through the body.

Contrast mechanisms

MRI contrast depends on tissue properties and on how the pulse sequence is designed. T1, T2, proton density, diffusion, perfusion, and other factors can all alter how tissues appear [file:20].

  • Sequence choice changes appearance.
  • Different tissues relax differently.
  • Functional information can also be captured.

K-space idea

The scanner does not build the image directly in picture form. It collects spatial frequency information first, often explained using k-space, and then reconstructs the image from that data [file:20].

  • Central data affect overall brightness and contrast.
  • Outer data affect detail and sharp edges.
  • Ordering of acquisition influences image quality.
Check: What do you think changes if the center of k-space is sampled differently?

Image reconstruction

After signal acquisition, the system transforms raw data into an image that radiologists and RT teams can interpret. The final appearance depends on reconstruction and sequence parameters [file:20].

  • Raw data become a visible image.
  • Noise, motion, and sequence choice affect the result.
  • Good acquisition produces useful anatomy for planning and guidance.
Check: Why do motion and acquisition settings matter before reconstruction?

Summary and review

MRI produces images by aligning hydrogen nuclei, exciting them with RF, encoding location with gradients, and reconstructing the acquired signal into visible anatomy. The same principles that make MRI useful also explain why sequence choice affects contrast [file:20].

  • Magnet creates alignment.
  • RF excites and produces signal.
  • Gradients encode position.
  • Reconstruction turns signal into an image.
