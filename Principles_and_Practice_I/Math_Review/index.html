Math Review

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: Three Calculations You'll Use Constantly

This lesson covers three math skills that come up in treatment planning and daily practice: the wavelength/frequency relationship (the physics foundation behind imaging and beam energy), magnification (correcting for beam divergence in images and field size), and the inverse square law (how dose rate changes with distance). None of these require more than algebra — the challenge is usually setting the problem up correctly, not the arithmetic itself.

Page 2: Wavelength and Frequency

All electromagnetic waves travel at the speed of light, c = 3.0 × 108 m/s, related to frequency (ν) and wavelength (λ) by:

c = ν × λ    so    ν = c / λ    and    λ = c / ν

Worked example: Find the wavelength of a wave with frequency 4.5 × 1014 Hz.
λ = c / ν = (3.0 × 108) / (4.5 × 1014) = 6.67 × 10-7 m

Quick Check!

If frequency increases, what happens to wavelength?

B. Frequency and wavelength are inversely related — as one goes up, the other goes down, since their product must stay equal to the constant speed of light.

Page 3: Magnification

Because a beam diverges from its source, any image or field projected onto a surface is always slightly larger than the actual object — this is magnification.

M = SID / SOD   (Source-to-Image Distance ÷ Source-to-Object Distance)

Worked example: Source-to-object distance (SOD) = 90 cm, object-to-detector distance (OTD) = 35 cm.
Step 1 — find SID: SID = SOD + OTD = 90 + 35 = 125 cm
Step 2 — find M: M = SID / SOD = 125 / 90 = 1.38

Page 4: The Inverse Square Law

Beam intensity falls off with distance from the source — specifically, it's inversely proportional to the square of the distance.

I1 × D1² = I2 × D2²

Worked example: Intensity is 2 cGy/MU at 100 cm. What's the intensity at 130 cm?
I2 = I1 × (D1/D2)² = 2 × (100/130)² = 2 × 0.592 = 1.18 cGy/MU

Quick Check!

As distance from the source increases, beam intensity:

B. Because the relationship is inverse-square, intensity drops off much faster than distance increases — doubling distance cuts intensity to a quarter, not a half.
