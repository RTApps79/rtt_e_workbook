Practice Problems

Work each problem out fully before revealing the answer. These combine magnification, field size at varying SSD, and inverse-square dose corrections — the same math you'll be asked to verify in an actual clinic.

1. An object in the patient (e.g., the width of a vertebral body) measures 5.3 cm. Its image on the receptor measures 7.5 cm. What is the magnification factor?

M = image size / object size = 7.5 / 5.3 = 1.42

2. Beam intensity is 30 cGy/MU at 100 cm. What is the intensity at 90 cm?

I₂ = I₁ × (D₁/D₂)² = 30 × (100/90)² = 30 × 1.235 = 37.0 cGy/MU

3. A treatment is prescribed at 100 cm SAD. If the field size indicator reads 14 cm, what is the field size at the patient's surface if the SSD is 75 cm?

Field size scales linearly with distance from the source: FS(surface) = FS(indicator) × (SSD/SAD) = 14 × (75/100) = 10.5 cm

4. A simulator field size display shows 10 × 16 cm at 100 cm SAD. What will the field size measure on the patient's skin at 89 cm SSD?

10 × (89/100) = 8.9 cm, and 16 × (89/100) = 14.24 cm → 8.9 × 14.24 cm

5. A dose was calculated to be 250 cGy at 80 cm SSD. What would the new dose be if the patient were actually treated at 95 cm SSD (same monitor units)?

D₂ = D₁ × (D₁/D₂)² = 250 × (80/95)² = 250 × 0.709 = 177.3 cGy — notice this is a real underdose if the SSD error goes uncorrected.

6. A patient was to receive 40 Gy in 20 daily fractions of 2 Gy at 80 cm SSD. If every treatment was mistakenly given at 70 cm SSD instead (same MU setting each day), what total dose would the patient actually receive?

Total dose = 40 × (80/70)² = 40 × 1.306 = 52.2 Gy — a significant, clinically serious overdose. This is exactly why verifying SSD/SAD setup every fraction matters, not just at simulation.

7. A field size measures 15 × 15 cm at isocenter, and 10 × 10 cm on the patient's skin. What is the magnification factor?

M = larger (farther/isocenter) size / smaller (closer/skin) size = 15 / 10 = 1.5

8. A patient has an IFD (intra-field distance / patient separation) of 20 cm. In an SAD treatment, what is the magnification factor if the SID is 120 cm?

In an SAD technique, isocenter is placed at mid-separation depth — so the object being imaged (isocenter) sits at SOD = SAD = 100 cm, the standard convention. The IFD confirms *how* that mid-depth was found (half of the 20 cm separation), but isocenter itself is still at 100 cm from the source.
M = SID / SOD = 120 / 100 = 1.2

These calculations use simplified geometric relationships for teaching purposes; always follow your department's verified calculation and QA procedures for actual patient treatment.
