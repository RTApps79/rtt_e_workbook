Genitourinary Tumors: Radiation Therapy Planning and Delivery

This lesson provides detailed information on the preplanning, planning, and delivery steps for radiation therapy in various genitourinary cancer sites, with enhanced focus on precise field borders.

Bladder Cancer (Urothelial Carcinoma of the Bladder)

Bladder cancer is the sixth most common cancer in the United States, predominantly urothelial carcinoma. Management varies significantly based on whether the disease is non-muscle invasive (NMIBC) or muscle invasive (MIBC).

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • NMIBC: External Beam Radiation Therapy (EBRT) alone is rarely appropriate. Concurrent chemoradiotherapy may be considered for recurrent high-grade Ta or T1 disease in non-cystectomy candidates.
  • MIBC: Bladder preservation with concurrent chemoradiotherapy is a primary treatment option for stage II or IIIA disease. RT alone or TURBT alone may be options for selected patients not candidates for cystectomy or definitive chemoradiotherapy.
  • Adjuvant RT: May be considered for pT3-4, positive nodes/margins after radical cystectomy (category 2B).
  • Palliative RT: For symptom control in metastatic disease.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: History (hematuria, urinary frequency), physical exam, office cystoscopy (enhanced if available), urine cytology.
  • Imaging:
    • Abdomen/pelvis CT or MRI, including upper urinary tract.
    • Chest imaging (CT chest preferred).
    • Bone scan or MRI if clinical suspicion or symptoms of bone metastases.
    • FDG-PET/CT (category 2B) may be beneficial for MIBC (≥cT2 disease) but not for NMIBC.
  • TURBT: Transurethral Resection of Bladder Tumor with bimanual examination under anesthesia (EUA) to confirm diagnosis, stage, and resect visible tumor. Adequate muscle in specimen is crucial for MIBC.
  • Pathology Review: Histology (urothelial, squamous, adenocarcinoma, small cell), grade (low/high), depth of invasion (Ta, T1, T2, T3, T4). Subtype histology (micropapillary, plasmacytoid, sarcomatoid) should be identified.
  • Renal Function: Estimate Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR) to assess cisplatin eligibility.
  • Genetic Counseling: Consider germline testing and genetic counselor referral for younger age at presentation or family history of Lynch syndrome.

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Patient Positioning: Supine. Empty bladder preferred for daily reproducibility; full bladder acceptable for tumor boosts with image guidance.
  • Immobilization: Reproducible setup.
  • Contrast: IV contrast for CT/MRI. Oral contrast for bowel delineation.
  • Target Volume Delineation:
    • GTV: Gross tumor.
    • CTV: GTV + margin for microscopic extension (e.g., whole bladder). For node-positive disease, consider boosting grossly involved nodes.
    • PTV: CTV + setup margin.
    • OARs: Rectum, small bowel, femoral heads, bone marrow.
  • Nodal RT: Elective treatment to pelvic lymph nodes (hypogastric, obturator, internal/external iliac, perivesical, sacral, presacral) is optional and should consider comorbidities and toxicity risks. Common iliac nodes for N3 disease.
  • Traditional Treatment Borders (4-field Technique):
    • Anteroposterior fields: Extend laterally 1.5 cm to the bony pelvis at its widest; exclude the inferior corners to protect femoral heads.
    • Lateral fields: Extend anteriorly 1.5-2 cm from the most anterior aspect of the bladder. The posterior border extends 2.5 cm posterior to the most posterior aspect of the bladder and falls within the rectum.
    • Inferior border: Below the middle of the obturator foramen.
    • Superior border: L5-S1 or at the superior S1 joint.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Concurrent Chemoradiotherapy (MIBC):
    • Whole bladder with or without pelvic nodal RT: 39.6-50.4 Gy (1.8-2.0 Gy/day).
    • Boost to whole or partial bladder: 60-66 Gy.
    • Hypofractionated schedule: 55 Gy in 20 fractions over 4 weeks (noninferior to 64 Gy in 32 fractions).
    • Radiosensitizers: Cisplatin alone, low-dose gemcitabine, 5-FU and mitomycin (preferred). Cisplatin and 5-FU, or cisplatin and paclitaxel (other recommended).
  • Adjuvant RT (post-cystectomy pT3/pT4, pN+): 45-50.4 Gy to cystectomy bed and pelvic lymph nodes. Boost involved margins/extranodal extension to 54-60 Gy if feasible.
  • Palliative RT: 30 Gy in 10 fractions or 21 Gy in 3 fractions.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Techniques: Multiple fields from high-energy linear accelerators. IMRT/VMAT for conformal delivery.
  • Setup Verification: Daily image guidance (IGRT) is preferred, especially for bladder-only or boost fields.
  • Chemotherapy: Concurrent chemotherapy is recommended for added tumor cytotoxicity. Cisplatin is the preferred radiosensitizer.

Kidney Tumors (Renal Cell Carcinoma)

Renal Cell Carcinoma (RCC) is the most common type of kidney cancer in adults, with clear cell histology being the most prevalent subtype. It is often diagnosed incidentally.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • Primary RT (Definitive): Stereotactic Body Radiation Therapy (SBRT) may be considered for medically inoperable patients with Stage I, II, or III kidney cancer.
  • Oligometastatic Disease: SBRT/Stereotactic Radiosurgery (SRS) or ablative techniques for limited metastatic sites.
  • Palliative RT: For symptomatic bone metastases or other sites of disease.
  • Adjuvant RT: Generally not recommended after nephrectomy due to lack of overall survival (OS) benefit, but may be considered in very specific high-risk cases (e.g., positive margins, N+).

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: History, physical exam, labs (CBC, comprehensive metabolic panel, LDH).
  • Imaging: Abdomen CT or MRI (renal protocol, with and without IV contrast preferred). Chest CT. Bone scan or brain MRI if symptomatic. FDG-PET/CT not routinely recommended for staging.
  • Biopsy: Consider for small lesions to obtain diagnosis and guide active surveillance or ablative techniques. Essential prior to ablative therapies.
  • Staging: AJCC TNM staging. Risk stratification using MSKCC or IMDC criteria.
  • Genetic Evaluation: Consider for multiple renal masses, young age at diagnosis, or family history of RCC syndromes (e.g., von Hippel-Lindau [VHL], Hereditary Leiomyomatosis and Renal Cell Cancer [HLRCC], Birt-Hogg-Dubé syndrome [BHDS], Tuberous Sclerosis Complex [TSC]).
  • Renal Function: Crucial to assess Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR) for systemic therapy eligibility.

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Patient Positioning: Supine with arms up (e.g., in a wing board or arm board), knee bolster. Immobilization devices (e.g., custom vaclok) for reproducibility.
  • Contrast: IV and oral contrast for CT simulation.
  • Motion Management: Crucial due to respiratory motion. Techniques include 4D-CT, abdominal compression, Deep Inspiration Breath-Hold (DIBH), and respiratory gating.
  • Target Volume Delineation (Primary Kidney Tumor):
    • GTV: Gross tumor.
    • CTV: GTV plus margin (e.g., 5-10mm).
    • ITV: CTV plus margin for organ motion (if free-breathing).
    • PTV: ITV/CTV plus setup margin (e.g., 5-10mm).
  • Organs at Risk (OARs):
    • Contralateral Kidney: Strict dose limits (e.g., D50% ≤8 Gy, or Mean <18 Gy for both kidneys).
    • Liver: Mean <25-30 Gy.
    • Spinal Cord: Max ≤45 Gy.
    • Bowel: Minimize dose.
  • Field Placement Borders (Primary Kidney Tumor - Conventional AP/PA):
    • Superior: Includes the kidney tumor with a 1 cm margin (or extends to the dome of the diaphragm if tumor is high).
    • Inferior: Includes the kidney and entire preoperative tumor extent with a 1 cm margin.
    • Medial: Includes the entire width of the vertebral bodies with enough contralateral extension to include the para-aortic lymph nodes while excluding the opposite kidney.
    • Lateral: Encompasses the abdominal wall (outside peritoneal line).
  • Regional Lymph Nodes: Para-aortic and lumbar nodes. Lymph node dissection may be considered for resectable or palpable adenopathy.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Primary Disease (SBRT for non-surgical candidates):
    • Single fraction: 26 Gy.
    • 3-4 fractions: 42-48 Gy.
    • 5 fractions: 40-50 Gy. (Target Biologically Effective Dose [BED] ≥80 Gy assuming an alpha/beta ratio of 10).
  • Postoperative RT (Adjuvant): 45-50 Gy in 5 weeks (generally not routinely recommended, but if used for pT3-4, N+, positive margins, then 45-50.4 Gy).
  • Palliative RT: 30 Gy in 10 fractions or 8 Gy in 1 fraction.
  • Metastatic Disease (SBRT/SRS):
    • Spine SBRT: 16-24 Gy in 1 fraction; 20-24 Gy in 2 fractions; 24-27 Gy in 3 fractions; 30-40 Gy in 5 fractions.
    • Other body sites SBRT: 16-24 Gy in 1 fraction; 48-60 Gy in 3 fractions; 35-60 Gy in 4-5 fractions.
    • Brain SRS: 15-24 Gy in 1 fraction (volume dependent); Fractionated SRS (24-27 Gy in 3 fractions, 25-35 Gy in 5 fractions).

Treatment Delivery:

  • Techniques: IMRT/VMAT (preferred for OAR sparing), 3D-CRT, SBRT/SRS. Proton therapy may be considered for OAR sparing.
  • Setup Verification: Daily Image-Guided Radiation Therapy (IGRT) is routinely used (CBCT, MRI-guided RT if available). Fiducial markers may be implanted for SBRT targeting.
  • Patient Care: Close monitoring of renal function during and after RT.

Upper Tract Urothelial Carcinoma (UTUC) - Renal Pelvis and Ureter

UTUC is less common than bladder cancer, originating in the renal pelvis or ureter. Treatment is primarily surgical, with growing roles for neoadjuvant chemotherapy and organ-sparing approaches.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • Adjuvant RT or chemoradiotherapy is generally **not recommended** after nephroureterectomy due to lack of conclusive data demonstrating OS benefit.
  • May be considered in very limited, specific situations (e.g., positive margins, high-risk features) but is not standard.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: Hematuria, flank pain, or obstruction.
  • Imaging:
    • CT or MR urography (preferred for upper tract collecting system).
    • Ureteroscopy with biopsy or percutaneous biopsy/selective washings.
    • Chest X-ray or CT for metastasis.
    • Bone scan if clinical suspicion.
  • Renal Function: Assess GFR, especially for cisplatin eligibility.
  • Genetic Counseling: Consider for Lynch syndrome risk.

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Target Volume: Typically includes the tumor bed and regional lymph nodes if RT is considered (e.g., for positive margins).
  • Regional Lymphadenectomy: Recommended for high-grade tumors. For renal pelvis/upper ureter, includes para-aortic/paracaval nodes from renal hilum to aortic/IVC bifurcation. For mid-ureter, includes common iliac, external iliac, obturator, hypogastric. For distal ureter, includes common iliac, external iliac, obturator, hypogastric.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy: Cisplatin-based (e.g., gemcitabine and cisplatin) is preferred for higher stage/grade or concerning radiographic findings, as renal function may decline post-nephroureterectomy.
  • Adjuvant Chemotherapy: For pT2-4 or pN+ disease after nephroureterectomy, adjuvant platinum-based chemotherapy significantly improves DFS. Nivolumab may also be considered.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Primary Treatment: Nephroureterectomy with bladder cuff and regional lymphadenectomy is standard.
  • Nephron-Sparing Endoscopic Treatment: Option for low-grade, low-volume (<1.5 cm), unifocal papillary tumors. May involve mitomycin ureteral gel. Requires long-term surveillance.
  • Perioperative Intravesical Chemotherapy: Strongly considered after nephroureterectomy with bladder cuff to reduce intravesical recurrence.

Primary Carcinoma of the Urethra

Primary carcinoma of the urethra is rare, with squamous cell carcinoma being the most common histology. Treatment is highly individualized based on tumor location (male non-prostatic vs. female urethra) and stage.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • Definitive RT (organ preservation): For cT2 cN0 disease, 66-70 Gy EBRT to gross disease with margins. Concurrent chemotherapy is encouraged. Prophylactic nodal RT (inguinal and low pelvic for female/distal male; pelvic for proximal male) is strongly considered.
  • Locally Advanced (T3-T4, N+): 45-50.4 Gy EBRT to gross disease and regional nodes. Boost gross primary to 66-70 Gy, gross nodal to 54-66 Gy. Concurrent chemotherapy.
  • Postoperative Adjuvant RT: 45-50.4 Gy to resection bed, inguinal/pelvic nodes. Boost involved margins/gross residual to 54-70 Gy. Concurrent chemotherapy.
  • Recurrent Disease: RT (66-74 Gy to gross disease) with or without elective nodal RT.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: Cystourethroscopy, EUA, transurethral or transvaginal biopsy.
  • Imaging: Chest X-ray or CT, MRI of pelvis with and without contrast.
  • Lymph Nodes: If palpable inguinal lymph nodes, Chest/abdomen/pelvis CT with contrast and lymph node biopsy.

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Target Volume: Primary tumor with margins, and regional lymph nodes (inguinal, pelvic).
  • Regional Lymphadenectomy: May be performed for palpable nodes or high-risk disease.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Definitive RT: 66-70 Gy in 1.8-2.0 Gy fractions.
  • Adjuvant RT: 45-50.4 Gy, with boosts to 54-70 Gy.
  • Concurrent Chemotherapy: Regimens used for bladder cancer (e.g., cisplatin-based, 5-FU/mitomycin).
  • Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy/Chemoradiation: May be considered for T2 disease.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Tis, Ta, T1: Repeat TUR followed by intraurethral chemotherapy (BCG, mitomycin, gemcitabine). Total urethrectomy if prior radical cystectomy.
  • T2 (Male, Pendulous): Distal urethrectomy or partial penectomy. Chemoradiotherapy or urethrectomy ± cystoprostatectomy for bulbar urethra.
  • T2 (Female): Chemoradiation or urethrectomy + cystectomy (organ-sparing when feasible).
  • T3, T4, N+: Multimodal approach (surgery, systemic therapy, radiation). Neoadjuvant chemotherapy (if urothelial histology) followed by consolidative surgery/RT. Chemoradiation preferred for squamous cell.
  • Recurrence: Systemic therapy, total penectomy, RT, or pelvic exenteration.
