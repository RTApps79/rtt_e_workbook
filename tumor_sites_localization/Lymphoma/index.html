Lymphomas: Radiation Therapy Planning and Delivery

This lesson provides detailed information on the preplanning, planning, and delivery steps for radiation therapy in various lymphoma subtypes, including both B-cell and T-cell lymphomas.

General Principles of Radiation Therapy for Lymphomas

Radiation therapy (RT) is a critical component in the multidisciplinary management of lymphomas. Modern RT techniques aim to deliver highly conformal doses to target volumes while minimizing exposure to surrounding healthy tissues, thereby reducing long-term toxicities.

Key Principles:

  • Accurate Target Definition: Precise delineation of gross tumor volume (GTV), clinical target volume (CTV), and planning target volume (PTV) is paramount. This relies on advanced imaging like PET/CT and MRI.
  • Involved-Site Radiation Therapy (ISRT): This is the standard approach, limiting radiation fields to only the initially involved nodal and extranodal sites, sparing adjacent uninvolved organs. This minimizes long-term complications.
  • Advanced RT Technologies:
    • IMRT/VMAT: Intensity-Modulated Radiation Therapy and Volumetric Modulated Arc Therapy are highly conformal techniques that shape radiation beams to the tumor, minimizing dose to organs at risk (OARs).
    • Proton Therapy: May offer significant advantages in specific instances to spare OARs due to its Bragg peak phenomenon, which allows for a sharp dose fall-off.
    • Image-Guided Radiation Therapy (IGRT): Daily imaging (e.g., cone-beam CT) to verify patient position and target localization, ensuring accurate delivery.
    • Motion Management: Techniques like 4D-CT, deep inspiration breath-hold (DIBH), or respiratory gating are crucial for tumors affected by respiratory motion (e.g., mediastinal, abdominal).
  • Quality Assurance: Rigorous quality assurance measures are essential throughout the planning and delivery process.
  • Dose Constraints: Strict dose limits are applied to critical OARs such as the heart (including coronary arteries and valves), lungs, kidneys, spinal cord, esophagus, bone marrow, breasts, stomach, muscle/soft tissue, and salivary glands.

General Dose Guidelines (Conventional Fractionation, 1.5-2.0 Gy daily fractions):

  • Consolidation after chemotherapy: 30-36 Gy for complete response (CR), 36-50 Gy for partial response (PR).
  • RT as primary treatment (if unfit for chemotherapy): 40-55 Gy.
  • In combination with HCT: 20-36 Gy, depending on sites of disease and prior RT exposure. Hypofractionation may be used to expedite HCT.
  • Palliative RT: 2-30 Gy in 1-10 fractions, depending on symptoms and goals.
  • Bridging RT with CAR T-cell Therapy: Localized disease 20-40 Gy; extensive disease 20-30 Gy.

Follicular Lymphoma (FL)

Follicular Lymphoma is the most common indolent non-Hodgkin lymphoma. Treatment strategies vary based on stage and tumor burden.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • Primary RT: For localized (Stage I-II) disease, ISRT is a highly effective treatment option, often curative.
  • Consolidation: May be used after systemic therapy for residual disease.
  • Palliative RT: For symptom management in advanced disease.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: H&P, B symptoms, performance status, evaluation of nodal areas (Waldeyer's ring, liver, spleen).
  • Lab Tests: CBC with differential, LDH, comprehensive metabolic panel, Hepatitis B testing.
  • Imaging: PET/CT scan (preferred) or C/A/P CT with contrast. PET/CT is crucial for staging and detecting transformation.
  • Biopsy: Excisional or incisional biopsy preferred. Bone marrow biopsy for staging or unexplained cytopenias.
  • Prognostic Indices: FLIPI-1, FLIPI-2, m7-FLIPI (molecular) for risk stratification.

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Patient Positioning: Supine, arms up. Immobilization devices for reproducibility.
  • Target Volume Delineation (ISRT):
    • GTV: Based on pre-chemotherapy/biopsy imaging (PET/CT, CT, MRI).
    • CTV: GTV + margin (e.g., 1-2 cm) to encompass original suspicious volume, sparing adjacent uninvolved organs. For RT alone, larger volumes may be considered to cover potential microscopic disease.
    • PTV: CTV + setup margin.
  • OARs: Lungs, heart, kidneys, spinal cord.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Localized Disease (Stage I-II):
    • ISRT: 24-30 Gy (1.5-2.0 Gy/fraction).
    • Ultra-low dose: 4 Gy in 2 fractions (for selected cases, requires careful follow-up).
  • Consolidation after Chemoimmunotherapy (CR): 24-30 Gy. For PR, 36 Gy.
  • Palliative RT: 2 Gy x 2 fractions or 4 Gy x 1 fraction. Up to 30 Gy for select circumstances.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Techniques: 3D conformal, IMRT/VMAT, or proton therapy.
  • Setup Verification: Image-guided RT (IGRT) is important.
  • Sequencing: RT may be used alone, or in combination with anti-CD20 mAb (rituximab/obinutuzumab) +/- chemotherapy.

Marginal Zone Lymphomas (MZL)

MZLs include extranodal (EMZL), nodal (NMZL), and splenic (SMZL) subtypes. EMZL often associated with chronic inflammation/infections.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • EMZL (Stomach): ISRT is highly effective for H. pylori-negative disease or refractory H. pylori-positive disease.
  • EMZL (Non-gastric): ISRT is an effective initial treatment for localized disease.
  • NMZL: ISRT is preferred for localized (Stage I-II contiguous) disease.
  • SMZL: Palliative ISRT may be used for symptomatic sites.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: H&P, performance status, B symptoms, specific site evaluation (e.g., endoscopy for gastric).
  • Lab Tests: CBC, LDH, comprehensive metabolic panel, Hepatitis B/C testing.
  • Imaging: PET/CT or C/A/P CT with contrast. EUS for gastric MZL.
  • Biopsy: Excisional biopsy preferred. For gastric, endoscopic biopsy with H. pylori stain.
  • Molecular: t(11;18) for gastric EMZL. MYD88 for differentiation from WM.

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Target Volume Delineation (ISRT):
    • EMZL (Stomach): Entire affected organ (stomach).
    • EMZL (Non-gastric): Entire affected organ (e.g., orbit, salivary gland, thyroid) if well localized.
    • NMZL: Involved nodal regions.
  • OARs: Dependent on site (e.g., GI tract, kidneys, liver, spinal cord, optic structures).

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Definitive RT: 24-30 Gy (1.5-2.0 Gy/fraction).
  • EMZL (Stomach): 24 Gy in 16 fractions (1.5 Gy/fraction) to minimize GI toxicity. Ultra-low dose (4 Gy in 2 fractions) for selected cases.
  • Orbital/Salivary Gland MZL: 4 Gy in 2 fractions may be considered.
  • Palliative RT: 2-30 Gy.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Techniques: 3D conformal, IMRT/VMAT.
  • Setup Verification: IGRT.
  • Sequencing:
    • EMZL (Stomach): H. pylori eradication therapy first. If refractory or H. pylori negative, then ISRT or rituximab.
    • Non-gastric EMZL/NMZL: ISRT alone, or rituximab +/- chemotherapy. Active surveillance for selected cases.

Mantle Cell Lymphoma (MCL)

MCL is an aggressive B-cell lymphoma, often presenting at advanced stages. Treatment is complex and stratified by patient eligibility for aggressive therapy and TP53 mutation status.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • Primary RT: For localized (Stage I or contiguous Stage II non-bulky) disease, ISRT (30-36 Gy) alone is an option.
  • Consolidation: May be used after chemoimmunotherapy (24-36 Gy for CR/PR).
  • Palliative RT: For symptomatic sites.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: H&P, B symptoms, performance status, evaluation of nodal areas, liver, spleen.
  • Lab Tests: CBC, LDH, comprehensive metabolic panel, uric acid, Hepatitis B/C testing.
  • Imaging: PET/CT scan (preferred) or C/A/P CT with contrast. Neck CT with contrast.
  • Biopsy: Excisional or incisional biopsy. Bone marrow biopsy to confirm stage I-II.
  • Molecular: TP53 sequencing (essential). Ki-67 proliferation index (prognostic). FISH for t(11;14).
  • CNS Evaluation: Lumbar puncture for blastic variant or CNS symptoms.

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Target Volume Delineation (ISRT):
    • GTV: Gross tumor.
    • CTV: GTV + margin.
    • PTV: CTV + setup margin.
  • OARs: Dependent on site.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Localized Disease (Stage I/II non-bulky): ISRT 30-36 Gy.
  • Consolidation after Chemoimmunotherapy: 24-30 Gy for CR, 36 Gy for PR.
  • Palliative RT: 2-30 Gy.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Techniques: 3D conformal, IMRT/VMAT.
  • Sequencing:
    • Localized: ISRT alone, or less aggressive chemoimmunotherapy +/- ISRT.
    • Advanced Stage (Classical TP53 wildtype): Aggressive induction (e.g., R-HyperCVAD, NORDIC, TRIANGLE) followed by HDT/ASCR and rituximab/BTKi maintenance.
    • Advanced Stage (Classical TP53 mutated): Clinical trial preferred. BOVen, or less aggressive induction regimens.

Diffuse Large B-Cell Lymphoma (DLBCL)

DLBCL is the most common aggressive lymphoma. Treatment is intensive and often involves multiagent chemoimmunotherapy.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • Consolidation: For limited-stage (Stage I-II non-bulky) disease, RT is often used after R-CHOP. For bulky disease or extensive mesenteric disease, RT to initially bulky sites may be beneficial.
  • CNS Prophylaxis: Cranial or craniospinal RT may be considered in very high-risk CNS relapse cases.
  • Palliative RT: For symptom control.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: H&P, B symptoms, performance status, evaluation of nodal areas, liver, spleen.
  • Lab Tests: CBC, LDH, comprehensive metabolic panel, uric acid, Hepatitis B/C, HIV.
  • Imaging: PET/CT scan (preferred) or C/A/P CT with contrast. Brain MRI/CT for CNS risk.
  • Biopsy: Excisional biopsy preferred. FISH for MYC, BCL2, BCL6 rearrangements. NGS panel.
  • CNS Evaluation: Lumbar puncture for high-risk CNS involvement.

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Target Volume Delineation (ISRT):
    • GTV: Gross tumor.
    • CTV: GTV + margin.
    • PTV: CTV + setup margin.
  • OARs: Heart, lungs, spinal cord, kidneys, GI tract.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Consolidation after Chemoimmunotherapy:
    • CR (PET negative): 30-36 Gy.
    • PR (PET positive): 36-50 Gy.
    • Refractory disease: 40-55 Gy.
  • Primary RT (without chemoimmunotherapy): 40 Gy.
  • Palliative RT: 20-30 Gy in 5-10 fractions.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Techniques: 3D conformal, IMRT/VMAT.
  • Sequencing:
    • Limited Stage: R-CHOP (3-4 cycles) +/- ISRT. PET-directed therapy is common.
    • Advanced Stage: R-CHOP (6 cycles) or Pola-R-CHP (preferred for IPI ≥2). DA-EPOCH-R for certain subtypes (PMBL, double/triple-hit).
    • CNS Prophylaxis: Systemic high-dose methotrexate and/or intrathecal methotrexate/cytarabine.

Primary Mediastinal Large B-Cell Lymphoma (PMBL)

PMBL is a distinct aggressive B-cell lymphoma arising in the mediastinum, often with unique molecular features and a tendency for local spread.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • Consolidation: ISRT is often considered after chemotherapy, especially for residual mediastinal masses.
  • Primary RT: For patients unfit for systemic therapy.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: H&P, B symptoms, performance status, large anterior mediastinal mass.
  • Lab Tests: CBC, LDH, comprehensive metabolic panel.
  • Imaging: PET/CT scan (essential), C/A/P CT with contrast.
  • Biopsy: Excisional biopsy preferred. Expert hematopathology review is essential.

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Patient Positioning: Supine, arms up. 4D-CT simulation for respiratory motion.
  • Target Volume Delineation (ISRT):
    • GTV: Pre-chemotherapy gross tumor.
    • CTV: GTV + margin.
    • PTV: CTV + setup margin.
  • OARs: Heart, lungs, breasts, esophagus, spinal cord. Minimizing dose to these is critical.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Consolidation after Chemoimmunotherapy: 30-36 Gy for CR, 36-50 Gy for PR.
  • Primary RT (unfit for chemotherapy): 40-55 Gy.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Techniques: IMRT/VMAT preferred due to mediastinal location and critical OARs. Proton therapy may be considered.
  • Sequencing: DA-EPOCH-R (6 cycles) or R-CHOP (4-6 cycles) as first-line. ISRT for consolidation, especially for PET-positive residual masses.

High-Grade B-Cell Lymphomas (HGBL)

HGBLs are aggressive lymphomas with specific genetic rearrangements (e.g., MYC and BCL2/BCL6) that confer a poor prognosis. They include "double-hit" and "triple-hit" lymphomas.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • Consolidation: ISRT may be considered for early-stage disease or localized residual disease after chemotherapy.
  • CNS Prophylaxis: Cranial or craniospinal RT may be considered in very high-risk CNS relapse cases.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: H&P, B symptoms, performance status. Often present with poor prognostic variables (elevated LDH, bone marrow/CNS involvement).
  • Lab Tests: CBC, LDH, comprehensive metabolic panel.
  • Imaging: PET/CT scan (preferred) or C/A/P CT with contrast. Brain MRI/CT for CNS risk.
  • Biopsy: Excisional biopsy preferred. FISH for MYC, BCL2, BCL6 rearrangements (essential). NGS panel.
  • CNS Evaluation: Lumbar puncture for CNS symptoms or high risk.

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Target Volume Delineation (ISRT): Similar to DLBCL.
  • OARs: Similar to DLBCL.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Consolidation after Chemoimmunotherapy: 30-36 Gy for CR, 36-50 Gy for PR.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Techniques: Intensive chemoimmunotherapy regimens (DA-EPOCH-R, R-HyperCVAD, R-CODOX-M/IVAC) are typically used.
  • CNS Prophylaxis: Systemic high-dose methotrexate and/or intrathecal chemotherapy.
  • Consolidation: HDT/ASCR may be considered, but its role is not fully established.

Burkitt Lymphoma (BL)

BL is a highly aggressive B-cell lymphoma characterized by rapid growth and frequent extranodal involvement. It requires immediate, intensive, multiagent chemotherapy.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • Consolidation: Palliative ISRT may be considered for patients with less than CR to induction therapy.
  • CNS Prophylaxis: Essential component of all regimens, often involving intrathecal and/or high-dose systemic chemotherapy. Cranial RT is rarely used for prophylaxis.
  • Palliative RT: For symptom control.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: H&P, B symptoms, performance status, bulky abdominal masses, bone marrow involvement, CNS involvement.
  • Lab Tests: CBC, LDH, uric acid, comprehensive metabolic panel, Hepatitis B/C, HIV.
  • Imaging: PET/CT scan (preferred, but do not delay therapy) or C/A/P CT with contrast. Brain MRI for suspected CNS involvement.
  • Biopsy: Excisional biopsy preferred. FISH for MYC rearrangement (essential).
  • CNS Evaluation: Lumbar puncture and CSF flow cytometry (essential).

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Target Volume Delineation: Typically involves systemic treatment due to rapid dissemination. RT fields are usually limited to palliative sites or for CNS prophylaxis.
  • OARs: Standard OARs, with particular attention to CNS structures for prophylaxis.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Palliative ISRT: Doses are typically individualized for symptom relief.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Techniques: Intensive multiagent chemoimmunotherapy regimens (e.g., CODOX-M/IVAC, HyperCVAD, DA-EPOCH-R).
  • CNS Prophylaxis: Mandatory with all regimens, using high-dose systemic methotrexate and/or intrathecal methotrexate/cytarabine.
  • Consolidation: Autologous HCT is generally not recommended in CR1 after induction therapy due to excellent outcomes with chemotherapy alone.

Peripheral T-Cell Lymphomas (PTCL)

PTCLs are a heterogeneous group of aggressive T-cell lymphomas. Treatment approaches vary significantly by subtype, often involving multiagent chemotherapy and sometimes consolidation with hematopoietic cell transplant (HCT).

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • Consolidation: ISRT may be used for localized disease after chemotherapy, especially for ALCL, ALK-positive.
  • Palliative RT: For symptom management in relapsed/refractory disease.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: H&P, B symptoms, performance status, full skin exam, nodal areas, liver, spleen.
  • Lab Tests: CBC, LDH, comprehensive metabolic panel, uric acid, Hepatitis B/C, HIV/HTLV-1/2.
  • Imaging: FDG-PET/CT (preferred) or C/A/P CT with contrast. Neck CT, Head CT/MRI for CNS symptoms.
  • Biopsy: Excisional or incisional biopsy preferred. Adequate immunophenotyping (IHC, flow cytometry). EBER-ISH.
  • Molecular Analysis: Clonal TCR gene rearrangements (useful). Cytogenetics (FISH for DUSP22, TP63, ALK). NGS panel for TFH subtypes (TET2, IDH2, RHOA, DNMT3A).
  • Prognostic Indices: IPI, PIT, T-cell score.

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Target Volume Delineation (ISRT):
    • GTV: Pre-chemotherapy gross tumor.
    • CTV: GTV + margin.
    • PTV: CTV + setup margin.
  • OARs: Lungs, heart, kidneys, spinal cord, esophagus, bone marrow, salivary glands.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Consolidation after Chemotherapy (CR): 30-36 Gy. For PR, 40-50 Gy.
  • Palliative RT: 20-36 Gy in 5-18 fractions.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Techniques: 3D conformal, IMRT/VMAT.
  • First-line Therapy: Multiagent chemotherapy (e.g., Brentuximab vedotin + CHP for CD30+; CHOEP, CHOP, DA-EPOCH for others). Consolidation with autologous HCT often considered.
  • Relapsed/Refractory: Clinical trial preferred. Single agents (e.g., Belinostat, Brentuximab vedotin, Duvelisib, Pralatrexate, Romidepsin, Ruxolitinib) or combination regimens (e.g., DHA+platinum, ESHAP+platinum, GDP, GemOx, ICE). Allogeneic HCT often considered.
  • Supportive Care: TLS prophylaxis, CMV reactivation monitoring/prophylaxis, PJP prophylaxis.

Extranodal NK/T-Cell Lymphomas (ENKL)

ENKL is a rare, predominantly extranodal lymphoma, often of nasal type. It is strongly associated with EBV infection and requires non-anthracycline-based chemotherapy.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • Primary RT: Essential component of initial treatment for localized (Stage I-II) nasal type, often combined with chemotherapy.
  • Consolidation: For localized disease after chemotherapy.
  • Palliative RT: For symptom management.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: H&P, B symptoms, performance status, ENT evaluation (nasopharynx, nasal cavity, paranasal sinuses), testicles, skin.
  • Lab Tests: CBC, LDH, comprehensive metabolic panel, uric acid, EBV viral load (quantitative PCR), Hepatitis B/C, HIV/HTLV-1/2.
  • Imaging: FDG-PET/CT, C/A/P CT with contrast. MRI/CT for nasal cavity, hard palate, anterior fossa.
  • Biopsy: Hematopathology review (excisional/incisional preferred). Adequate immunophenotyping (cCD3ε, CD2, CD56, TIA1, granzyme B). EBER-ISH (essential).
  • Prognostic Indices: PINK, PINK-E.
  • CNS Evaluation: Ophthalmologic exam, lumbar puncture for CSF analysis (if indicated).

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Patient Positioning: Supine. Immobilization.
  • Target Volume Delineation (ISRT):
    • GTV: Combined abnormalities from endoscopy, CT, MRI, FDG-PET/CT.
    • CTV: Entire involved cavity and adjacent structures (e.g., bilateral nasal cavities, ipsilateral maxillary sinus, bilateral anterior ethmoids). Involved nodes should be included.
    • PTV: CTV + setup margin.
  • OARs: Eyes, optic nerves, brainstem, salivary glands. IMRT strongly preferred.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • RT alone (if unfit for chemotherapy): 50-55 Gy.
  • RT with Chemotherapy: 45-56 Gy.
  • Combined Modality Therapy:
    • CCRT (e.g., 50 Gy with DeVIC).
    • Sequential chemoradiation (e.g., Modified SMILE followed by 45-50.4 Gy RT).
    • Sandwich chemoradiation (e.g., P-GEMOX/GELAD followed by RT, then more chemotherapy).
  • Palliative RT: 20-36 Gy.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Techniques: IMRT is strongly preferred.
  • Sequencing: Non-anthracycline-based chemotherapy regimens (e.g., SMILE, P-GEMOX, DDGP) are preferred. RT is essential for localized disease.
  • Consolidation: HCT may be considered for CR/PR.
  • Supportive Care: TLS prophylaxis, antiemetics.

Breast Implant-Associated Anaplastic Large Cell Lymphoma (BIA-ALCL)

BIA-ALCL is an uncommon T-cell lymphoma arising around breast implants, predominantly textured ones. It often presents as a delayed periprosthetic effusion.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • Adjuvant RT: For local residual disease after incomplete surgical excision or partial capsulectomy, especially if systemic therapy is not feasible or lymph nodes are positive. Doses typically 24-36 Gy.
  • Palliative RT: For symptom control in advanced disease.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: H&P, physical signs (effusion, enlargement, mass, ulceration) >1 year post-implantation.
  • Imaging: Ultrasound of breast/axilla. Breast MRI with/without contrast. FDG-PET/CT for staging in selected cases.
  • Biopsy: FNA biopsy of fluid (minimum 50 mL) around implant with cell block, IHC, flow cytometry. Biopsy of mass if present.
  • Pathology Review: Tumor cells are CD30+, ALK-, T-cell monoclonal. Secondary review by tertiary center.
  • Genetic Testing: Consider NGS for high-risk mutations (e.g., TP53, JAK-STAT3 pathway).

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Patient Positioning: Supine. Immobilization.
  • Target Volume Delineation: Local residual disease, regional lymph nodes.
  • Bolus: May be needed for superficial lesions.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Adjuvant RT: 24-36 Gy for local residual disease.
  • Palliative RT: Doses individualized for symptom relief.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Techniques: 3D conformal, IMRT/VMAT.
  • Primary Treatment: Complete surgical excision of implant, capsule, and associated mass with biopsy of suspicious nodes.
  • Systemic Therapy: Brentuximab vedotin (BV) alone or with CHP (cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, prednisone) for extended disease (Stage II-IV) or incomplete excision with residual disease.
  • Follow-up: Clinical and imaging surveillance.

T-Cell Large Granular Lymphocytic Leukemia (T-LGLL)

T-LGLL is an indolent T-cell lymphoproliferative disorder often associated with autoimmune conditions and cytopenias. Treatment is initiated for symptomatic disease.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • RT is typically not a primary treatment for T-LGLL.
  • May be used in rare cases for localized symptomatic splenomegaly or lymphadenopathy if refractory to systemic therapy.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: H&P (splenomegaly, lymphadenopathy, autoimmune disease), B symptoms, performance status.
  • Lab Tests: CBC with differential (persistent LGL increase), comprehensive metabolic panel. Serologic markers for autoimmune disease.
  • Biopsy: Peripheral blood smear analysis, flow cytometry (essential). Bone marrow biopsy (useful for low LGL counts).
  • Molecular Analysis: STAT3 and STAT5B mutations (useful). T-cell clonality (essential).

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Target Volume Delineation: Localized symptomatic areas (e.g., spleen).
  • OARs: Surrounding organs.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Doses are typically palliative.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Primary Treatment: Low-dose methotrexate, oral cyclophosphamide, or cyclosporine (with or without corticosteroids). Ruxolitinib for refractory disease.
  • Response Assessment: After 4 months, assess hematologic parameters and LGL counts.
  • Supportive Care: Management of underlying autoimmune disease and cytopenias.

T-Cell Prolymphocytic Leukemia (T-PLL)

T-PLL is a rare, aggressive T-cell malignancy. It is characterized by rapid progression and often presents with lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, and high WBC counts.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • RT may be used for localized, symptomatic disease (e.g., splenomegaly, lymphadenopathy) as part of palliative care.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: H&P (skin, lymph nodes, spleen, liver), B symptoms, performance status.
  • Lab Tests: CBC with differential, LDH, comprehensive metabolic panel. HTLV-1/2 serology.
  • Biopsy: Peripheral blood smear analysis, flow cytometry (essential). Cytogenetics (FISH for inv(14), t(14;14), t(X;14), trisomy 8).
  • Molecular Analysis: Clonal TCR gene rearrangements (useful).

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Target Volume Delineation: Localized symptomatic areas.
  • OARs: Surrounding organs.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Doses are typically palliative.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Primary Treatment: Alemtuzumab (IV) monotherapy is preferred. FCM (fludarabine, mitoxantrone, cyclophosphamide) followed by alemtuzumab, or alemtuzumab + pentostatin are alternatives.
  • Consolidation: Allogeneic HCT is a potentially curative option for responders. Autologous HCT may be considered if allogeneic is not feasible.
  • Relapsed/Refractory: Pentostatin, ruxolitinib, bendamustine, or retreatment with alemtuzumab.
  • Supportive Care: TLS prophylaxis, CMV monitoring/prophylaxis.

Adult T-Cell Leukemia/Lymphoma (ATLL)

ATLL is an aggressive T-cell malignancy caused by HTLV-1. It has four subtypes (smoldering, chronic, acute, lymphoma) with varying prognoses and treatment intensities.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • RT may be used in selected cases with localized, symptomatic disease for palliation.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: H&P (skin, lymph nodes, spleen, liver), B symptoms, performance status.
  • Lab Tests: CBC with differential, comprehensive metabolic panel, LDH, serology for strongyloides, HTLV-1/2 serology (essential).
  • Imaging: FDG-PET/CT scan (preferred) or C/A/P CT with contrast. CNS evaluation (Head CT/MRI, lumbar puncture) for acute/lymphoma subtypes.
  • Biopsy: Peripheral blood smear analysis, flow cytometry (essential). Biopsy of lymph nodes/skin/GI/bone marrow if peripheral blood is non-diagnostic.
  • Molecular Analysis: NGS panel for recurrent mutations (TP53, IRF4, PD-L1, CDKN2A). CCR4 expression.
  • Prognostic Indices: iATL-PI (indolent), ATL-PI (acute/lymphoma).

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Target Volume Delineation: Localized symptomatic lesions.
  • OARs: Surrounding organs.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Doses are typically palliative.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Smoldering (Asymptomatic): Observation.
  • Smoldering (Symptomatic): Skin-directed therapies for skin lesions. Zidovudine + IFN-alfa for tumor lesions.
  • Chronic: Zidovudine + IFN-alfa (all risk groups). Combination chemotherapy for high-risk.
  • Acute/Lymphoma: Combination chemotherapy (e.g., DA-EPOCH, Brentuximab vedotin + CHP for CD30+). Zidovudine + IFN-alfa for acute subtype.
  • Consolidation: Allogeneic HCT for high-risk chronic, acute, or lymphoma subtypes responding to initial therapy.
  • Relapsed/Refractory: Clinical trial preferred. Mogamulizumab (for CCR4+), Lenalidomide, Brentuximab vedotin (for CD30+), Arsenic trioxide, Alemtuzumab, Bortezomib, Pralatrexate.
  • Supportive Care: Strongyloides screening/treatment, PJP prophylaxis.

Hepatosplenic T-Cell Lymphoma (HSTCL)

HSTCL is a rare, aggressive T-cell lymphoma characterized by liver, spleen, and bone marrow involvement, often with systemic symptoms and HLH. It has a poor prognosis.

Role of Radiotherapy:

  • RT is generally not a primary treatment due to the systemic nature.
  • May be used for localized symptom palliation.

Preplanning and Assessment:

  • Clinical Evaluation: H&P (hepatosplenomegaly, cytopenias, HLH symptoms), B symptoms, performance status.
  • Lab Tests: CBC, LDH, comprehensive metabolic panel, uric acid. HLH workup (ferritin, sCD25, triglycerides, fibrinogen). Hepatitis B/C, HIV/HTLV-1/2.
  • Biopsy: Bone marrow or liver core biopsy (essential). IHC (CD2, CD3, CD4, CD5, CD8, CD56, TCRβ, TCRγδ, TIA-1, granzyme B). Flow cytometry.
  • Molecular Analysis: Clonal TCR gene rearrangements (useful). Karyotype/FISH for isochromosome 7q, trisomy 8. NGS panel (STAT3, STAT5B, SETD2, INO80, ARID1B).

Planning Technique (Simulation & Localization):

  • Target Volume Delineation: Localized symptomatic areas (e.g., spleen).
  • OARs: Surrounding organs.

Dose/Fractionation:

  • Doses are typically palliative.

Treatment Delivery:

  • Primary Treatment: Intensive non-CHOP-based chemotherapy (e.g., ICE, DHA+platinum, DA-EPOCH, HyperCVAD, IVAC).
  • Consolidation: Allogeneic HCT (preferred) for CR/PR. Autologous HCT if allogeneic is not feasible.
  • Relapsed/Refractory: Clinical trial preferred. Purine analogs (cladribine, pentostatin), or regimens for PTCL-NOS (e.g., Alemtuzumab, Pralatrexate, Duvelisib, ESHAP).
  • Supportive Care: HLH management, TLS prophylaxis.
