Understanding SDoH & Their Impact in Radiation Oncology

Introduction to Social Determinants of Health (SDoH)

Page 1: What are Social Determinants of Health?

Social Determinants of Health (SDoH) are the non-medical factors that influence health outcomes. They are the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age.

These circumstances are shaped by the distribution of money, power, and resources at global, national, and local levels. SDoH can have a greater impact on health than individual behaviors or even healthcare access.

(Definition adapted from the World Health Organization - WHO)

Page 2: Key Domains of SDoH

SDoH are often grouped into five key domains (as identified by Healthy People 2030/CDC):

  • Economic Stability: Poverty, employment status, food security, housing stability (e.g., homelessness, consistent access to utilities).
  • Education Access and Quality: Early childhood education, high school graduation, enrollment in higher education, language and literacy skills, health literacy.
  • Health Care Access and Quality: Access to health care services (including specialists), access to primary care, insurance coverage, quality of care received.
  • Neighborhood and Built Environment: Quality of housing, access to transportation, availability of healthy foods, air and water quality, neighborhood crime and violence, walkability.
  • Social and Community Context: Social support systems, community cohesion, civic participation, experiences of discrimination or stigma, stress, exposure to trauma, incarceration history.

Page 3: Why SDoH Matter in Cancer Care

SDoH play a critical role across the entire cancer care continuum:

  • Risk and Prevention: Exposure to environmental carcinogens (e.g., pollution in certain neighborhoods), access to cancer screening services, and ability to adopt healthy lifestyles can all be influenced by SDoH.
  • Diagnosis: Factors like lack of insurance, transportation barriers, or low health literacy can lead to delayed cancer diagnosis, often at later, less treatable stages.
  • Treatment: A patient's ability to access specialized cancer centers, complete complex and lengthy treatment regimens (like radiation therapy), and manage side effects effectively is heavily impacted by SDoH.
  • Survivorship and Outcomes: Access to follow-up care, management of long-term side effects, mental health support, and overall quality of life post-treatment are also shaped by these social and economic factors.

Page 4: SDoH & the Radiation Therapy Patient Experience

Radiation therapy often involves daily treatments for several weeks, creating specific challenges related to SDoH:

  • Transportation: Daily travel to the RT center can be a significant burden without reliable personal or public transport, especially for elderly patients or those living far away.
  • Financial Strain: Treatment costs, even with insurance, can be high. Add lost wages (for patient and/or caregiver), childcare costs, and travel/lodging expenses, and the financial toxicity can be immense.
  • Social Support: Managing side effects, attending appointments, and coping emotionally is much harder without a strong support system. Isolation can worsen treatment tolerance and outcomes.
  • Housing & Nutrition: Stable housing is crucial for recovery. Access to nutritious food is vital, especially when dealing with side effects like mucositis, dysphagia, or fatigue that affect appetite and energy.
  • Health Literacy: Understanding the complex RT plan, potential side effects, consent forms, and self-care instructions requires adequate health literacy. Language barriers can further complicate this.
  • Geographic Disparities: Access to advanced RT techniques (e.g., proton therapy, SBRT) may be limited in rural or underserved areas, leading to disparities in treatment options.

Page 5: The Affective Dimension of SDoH

The impact of SDoH is not just practical; it's deeply emotional and psychological:

  • Patients facing multiple SDoH barriers may experience increased stress, anxiety, and depression.
  • Constant worry about finances, transportation, or childcare can overshadow their focus on healing.
  • Feelings of helplessness, shame, or being overwhelmed are common.
  • Past negative experiences or perceived discrimination can lead to distrust in the healthcare system.

As healthcare professionals, recognizing these affective components is vital. Empathy, active listening, and patient-centered communication are key to building trust and providing truly supportive care that acknowledges the whole person, not just their disease.

Case 1: Sarah Chen

Age: 56

Diagnosis: Squamous Cell Carcinoma of the Left Tonsil, Stage I (HPV-Positive)

Treatment: Definitive Chemoradiation (IMRT 70 Gy/35 fx + concurrent Cisplatin)

Key EMR Notes: PEG tube placed pre-treatment. Nutrition and Speech Pathology consults pre-treatment. Lives with husband, David. Extensive supportive care medications prescribed.

Discussion Prompts for Sarah Chen:

1. SDoH Identification: Based on this snapshot, what potential SDoH challenges might Sarah and her husband be facing?
2. Impact Analysis: How might these challenges affect Sarah's treatment adherence, side effect management, and nutritional status?
3. Affective Domain - Empathy: What might be Sarah's primary emotional concerns beyond the cancer itself?
4. Healthcare Team Actions: What specific actions could the RT team take to support Sarah?

Case 2: Emily Carter

Age: 6 years old

Diagnosis: High-Risk Medulloblastoma

Treatment: Post-surgery/chemo, now for CSI 36 Gy(RBE) + Boost to 54 Gy(RBE) via IMPT (6-7 weeks).

Key EMR Notes: Custom pediatric mask/body mold. Daily IGRT. Insurance: Medicaid. Mother is emergency contact.

Discussion Prompts for Emily Carter:

1. SDoH Identification: What SDoH factors might be prominent for Emily's family?
2. Impact Analysis - Child's Perspective: How might daily RT impact a 6-year-old emotionally and socially?
3. Affective Domain - Parent's Perspective: What are the primary stressors for Emily's mother?
4. Healthcare Team Actions & Resources: What support services are critical for Emily and her family?

Case 3: Carlos Jones

Age: Adult (Assumed)

Diagnosis: Kaposi Sarcoma (KS), HIV-Associated. ACTG Stage: T1 I1 S0.

Treatment: Palliative EBRT for oral & leg lesions, concurrent systemic chemo (Liposomal Doxorubicin) & ART.

Key EMR Notes: Multiple painful lesions, lymphedema. CD4 180 (improving). Medicaid. Referred from ID.

Discussion Prompts for Carlos Jones:

1. SDoH Identification: What SDoH factors are likely at play for Carlos?
2. Impact of Competing Health Needs: How might managing both HIV and KS impact Carlos?
3. Affective Domain - Stigma & Trust: How might societal stigma affect Carlos's care experience?
4. Healthcare Team Actions: What are key supportive care needs for Carlos?

Case 4: Maria Garcia

Age: 60

Diagnosis: Metastatic NSCLC with Multiple Brain Metastases.

Treatment: Palliative WBRT 30 Gy/10 fx.

Key EMR Notes: Headaches, L-sided weakness, cognitive changes. ECOG 2. Requires walker. Hospice referral. DNR/DNI.

Discussion Prompts for Maria Garcia:

1. SDoH in Palliative Care: What SDoH factors are crucial for Maria and her son?
2. Impact of Functional Decline: How does Maria's weakness impact her and her son?
3. Affective Domain - Goals of Care: What are Maria's and Juan's likely primary emotional concerns?
4. Healthcare Team Actions & Communication: What is the RT team's role in supporting Maria, Juan, and coordinating with hospice?
