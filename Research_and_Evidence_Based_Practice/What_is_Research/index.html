What is Research? Why It Matters in Radiation Therapy

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: Research Is the Engine, Not the Guesswork

Research is what drives radiation therapy forward. Without the scientific method, treatments would be based on tradition and guesswork rather than precision and evidence. Understanding research isn't just an academic requirement — it's how the field actually improves.

Page 2: The Scientific Method

Research follows a rigorous, logical path to discover new facts or solve clinical problems:

StepWhat it means
1. ObserveNotice a clinical problem
2. QuestionAsk "is there a better way?"
3. HypothesisMake a testable prediction
4. ExperimentTest the prediction
5. AnalyzeReview the data
6. ConcludeDecide what the data actually shows

Understanding research means understanding that it's a systematic process — not a casual search for information that happens to confirm what you already believed.

Page 3: Evidence-Based Practice

Evidence-Based Practice (EBP) integrates the best available clinical research with patient values and clinical expertise to drive standard-of-care decisions. None of these three legs works alone: research evidence without clinical judgment is just data; clinical judgment without evidence is just habit; and either without patient values ignores the person actually receiving care.

Quick Check!

Which best describes Evidence-Based Practice?

B. EBP is the integration of all three — not blind adherence to the newest study alone.

Page 4: Three Categories of Research in RT

Research in this field isn't only about dose. It falls into three broad categories:

  • Clinical: comparing treatment techniques, toxicity rates, dose constraints
  • Educational: investigating how tools like VERT simulation improve student readiness
  • Administrative: improving department workflows, scheduling, and patient wait times

All three are legitimate, publishable research — a meaningful research project doesn't have to involve a clinical trial.

Page 5: Peer Review and Gaps in the Literature

Peer review is the process by which experts in the field evaluate research before publication, checking quality and validity. It's not a formality — it's the mechanism that keeps flawed or overstated findings from becoming accepted practice unchallenged.

A gap in the literature is a problem or question current research hasn't yet answered. Finding a genuine gap — not just a topic you find interesting — is the real starting point for original research, and it's exactly what the next module (Research Questions, Problem Statements, and Literature Searching) walks through.

Page 6: Start Looking at Your Daily Work Critically

You don't have to wait for a formal assignment to start thinking like a researcher. Start looking at your daily clinical or classroom tasks with a critical eye. That "frustrating workflow" or "confusing student assignment" might be the perfect seed for a research question of your own.

Quick Check!

A therapist notices that a particular setup verification step routinely takes much longer than scheduled and often causes delays. What research category does investigating this best fit?

A. A workflow/scheduling problem like this is administrative research, not a question about treatment technique or outcome.

Page 7: Reading Research With a Critical Eye

As you read published research, don't just extract the conclusion — check how the researchers got there. Was the sample size reasonable? Was the comparison group appropriate? Does the stated conclusion actually match what the data showed, or does it overreach? Building this habit now will make your own research work sharper.

Page 8: Wrap-Up

You've covered the scientific method, Evidence-Based Practice, the three categories of RT research, peer review, and what a genuine gap in the literature looks like.

Next: turning an observation from your daily work into an actual research question and problem statement — and learning how to search the literature to find out if someone's already answered it.
