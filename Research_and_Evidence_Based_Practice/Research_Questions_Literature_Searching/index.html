Research Questions & Literature Searching

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: From Observation to Searchable Question

Once you've noticed a problem worth investigating, the next step is converting that observation into a rigorous, searchable question. A vague question like "How do we improve treatments?" or "How do we make learning easier?" yields too much irrelevant information to be useful. You need precision — whether your focus is inside the treatment vault or inside the classroom.

Page 2: The Problem Statement

Before asking a question, define the problem. A problem statement clearly identifies the gap between the current state and a desired state, and it's what justifies why a project is worth doing in the first place.

Clinical example: Current state — prostate patients frequently experience delays from inadequate bladder filling on daily CBCT. Desired state — patients maintain consistent bladder volumes, reducing table time. The gap — it's unclear whether a standardized hydration protocol beats standard verbal instructions at maintaining target volumes.

Educational example: Current state — students struggle to visualize 3D anatomy from 2D textbook diagrams. Desired state — students demonstrate strong spatial awareness before entering clinic. The gap — whether VR-based training systems improve clinical readiness compared to traditional didactic learning.

Page 3: The PICO Framework

To bridge the gap, formulate a specific question. PICO is the gold standard for breaking a broad topic into a testable inquiry. Originally built for clinical medicine, its logic applies just as well to educational and administrative research.

LetterStands forExample
PPopulation / ProblemProstate cancer patients, undergraduate RT students, therapists performing chart checks
IInterventionA hydration protocol, a VR simulation system, a standardized safety checklist
CComparisonVerbal instructions, standard 2D textbooks, unstructured workflows
OOutcomeDaily CBCT volume consistency, exam scores, incident report rates

Clinical PICO: In prostate cancer patients undergoing VMAT (P), does a standardized hydration protocol (I) compared to standard verbal instructions (C) improve daily CBCT bladder volume consistency (O)?

Non-clinical PICO: In undergraduate radiation therapy students (P), does VR-based training (I), compared to traditional 2D didactic lectures (C), improve spatial anatomy recognition and clinical setup accuracy (O)?

Quick Check!

In the clinical PICO question above, what is the "C" (Comparison)?

A. The comparison is the current standard practice the intervention is being measured against — here, verbal instructions.

Page 4: Searching the Literature

Once your PICO question is set, find out what's already known. Databases like PubMed, CINAHL, and ERIC (for educational research) rely heavily on Boolean operators to narrow or broaden a search:

  • AND — narrows the search; both terms must be present (e.g., radiotherapy education AND virtual reality)
  • OR — broadens the search; either term qualifies, useful for synonyms (e.g., simulation OR VR training)
  • NOT — excludes terms (e.g., prostate cancer NOT brachytherapy)

Page 5: Screening What You Find

When screening search results, read the Abstract first — specifically the Objective and Conclusion — to quickly judge whether an article actually fits your PICO parameters before reading the whole thing.

Favor peer-reviewed articles published within the last 5–7 years to keep your evidence current, unless you have a specific reason to cite older foundational work.

Quick Check!

You want articles about VR training OR simulation training in radiologic education, but want to exclude anything about surgical simulation. Which search captures this?

A. OR broadens between the two training terms, AND narrows to the right field, and NOT excludes the irrelevant surgical topic.

Page 6: Is There Really a Gap?

Sometimes a literature search reveals the question has already been thoroughly answered — that's a useful finding too, and it means either refining your question further or picking a genuinely open angle. A real gap in the literature is a question current research hasn't yet resolved, not just a topic that happens to interest you.

Page 7: Wrap-Up

You've covered writing a problem statement, building a PICO question, using Boolean search operators, and screening literature efficiently.

Next: once you know what's already been studied, you need to choose the research design that will actually let you answer your specific question.
