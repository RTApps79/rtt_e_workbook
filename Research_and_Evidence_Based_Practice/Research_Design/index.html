Research Design

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: The Blueprint for Your Study

A research design is the architectural blueprint of a study — it determines exactly how you'll collect and analyze data to answer your research question. Choosing the wrong design doesn't just make a study weaker; it can make the question un-answerable no matter how good your data collection is.

Page 2: Three Approaches to Data

The type of question you ask dictates the type of data you need:

  • Quantitative — numerical data, statistical procedures, testing hypotheses and causal relationships
  • Qualitative — nonnumerical data (interviews, focus groups) exploring subjective experiences or complex phenomena in depth
  • Mixed-Methods — combines both to give a more complete answer than either alone

Page 3: Descriptive Studies

Descriptive studies collect data without changing or manipulating the environment — you observe and describe, such as a survey or case study. They're excellent for establishing baseline understanding of a situation, but they can't establish cause and effect.

Page 4: True Experimental Design

A true experimental study introduces a treatment or procedure to test a cause-and-effect hypothesis. It requires four strict elements: manipulation of a variable, a control group, random assignment, and random selection.

In a clinical setting, hitting all four of these is often genuinely difficult — you frequently can't randomly assign patients to receive or not receive a treatment believed to be beneficial.

Page 5: Quasi-Experimental Design

In clinical settings, assigning people to specific conditions is sometimes impossible or unethical. Quasi-experimental research still evaluates cause-and-effect relationships, but without the strict randomization of a true experiment.

Example: comparing patient outcomes between two different, pre-existing hospital locations — you didn't randomly assign patients to each location, but you can still compare outcomes between the groups that already exist.

Quick Check!

You use a phantom to compare VMAT delivery times when the gantry rotates clockwise vs. counterclockwise. What approach and design does this represent?

B. You're gathering numerical data (time) and actively manipulating a variable (rotation direction) in a controlled setting — that's quantitative and experimental, not just observational.

Page 6: Matching Design to Real-World Constraints

The "best" design on paper isn't always the feasible one. A true experimental design with full randomization might be methodologically ideal but practically impossible to get approved or to execute with your available patient population, timeframe, or access. Choosing a design is always a negotiation between rigor and what you can actually pull off responsibly.

Page 7: Evaluating a Source's Design

When you're reading someone else's published research, identifying their design tells you a lot about how much weight to put on their conclusions. A descriptive study showing an association is not the same evidence as a true experimental study showing causation — even if both papers report a "statistically significant" result.

Page 8: Wrap-Up

You've covered quantitative, qualitative, and mixed-methods approaches, plus descriptive, true experimental, and quasi-experimental designs, and how feasibility shapes design choice in practice.
