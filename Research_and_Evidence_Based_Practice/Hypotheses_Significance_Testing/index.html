Hypotheses & Significance Testing

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: The Foundation of the Scientific Method

Hypothesis testing is the formal process by which new scientific information gets added to what's already known, and previously held beliefs get rigorously tested for validity. If your hypothesis holds up, new knowledge is gained. If it doesn't, that's not failure — it's exactly how the process is supposed to work.

Page 2: The Steps of Hypothesis Testing

  1. Gather background information (literature review)
  2. Formulate the null hypothesis (H₀)
  3. Design the study
  4. Decide on the significance level (α)
  5. Collect the data
  6. Calculate the sample statistic (p)
  7. Reject or fail to reject the null hypothesis, by comparing p to α
  8. Use the result to inform the next question

Page 3: Null and Alternative Hypotheses

A hypothesis is a declarative statement about how the study relates a cause (independent variable) to an effect (dependent variable).

Null Hypothesis (H₀): assumes NO relationship or difference. This is the hypothesis formal statistical testing is actually built around — you're trying to find enough evidence to reject it.

Alternative Hypothesis (Ha or H₁): assumes a difference or association DOES exist. This is what the researcher expects or hopes to find.

Quick Check!

Which hypothesis does a statistical test directly evaluate evidence against?

A. Formal tests are built to attempt to reject the null hypothesis; you never directly "prove" the alternative.

Page 4: The Central Limit Theorem

How do we mathematically show a difference exists? Most statistical tests rest on the Central Limit Theorem (CLT): if you take equally sized samples from a population, the distribution of those sample means will form a normal (bell-shaped) curve — even if the original population wasn't normally distributed — as long as the sample size is "large enough" (a common rule of thumb is n ≥ 30).

This is why so many statistical tests assume normality of the sampling distribution rather than the raw data itself.

Page 5: p-Values and Confidence Intervals

p-value: the probability of getting your study's result by chance if the null hypothesis were actually true. If p ≤ .05, the result is statistically significant (reject H₀). If p > .05, it's not (fail to reject H₀).

Caution: a p-value only signals significance — it says nothing about the size or clinical importance of the effect.

Confidence Intervals (CI): give the actual range of plausible values for the effect, which is genuinely more clinically useful than a p-value alone. Non-overlapping 95% CIs between groups generally indicate a significant difference; heavy overlap generally does not.

Page 6: Type I and Type II Errors

Because statistics work on probability, not certainty, two specific kinds of errors are possible:

  • Type I Error (α) — a false positive. You reject a true null hypothesis: you claim your intervention worked when it actually didn't. Example: adopting an expensive new immobilization device because the data falsely suggested it improved accuracy.
  • Type II Error (β) — a false negative. You fail to reject a false null hypothesis: a real difference exists, but your study didn't detect it — often because the sample size was too small. Example: abandoning an actually-effective skin-sparing protocol because the study "failed" to prove it worked.

Quick Check!

A study is underpowered due to a small sample and misses a real effect that was actually there. This is:

B. Failing to detect a real effect — often due to insufficient sample size — is the classic signature of a Type II error.

Page 7: Statistical Power

To avoid a Type II error, a study needs strong statistical power (1 − β) — the probability of correctly rejecting the null hypothesis when it actually is false. Determining power requires four pieces: alpha (usually .05), the desired power (usually .80), the expected effect size, and the sample size.

In practice: bigger expected effects and larger samples both increase power, which is why sample-size planning matters before you ever collect data, not after.

Page 8: Choosing the Right Statistical Test

Your variable's level of measurement (NOIR) determines which test is valid:

TestWhen to use it
Independent t-testComparing means of exactly two independent groups, continuous data
Paired t-testComparing two measurements from the same subjects
One-way ANOVAComparing means of three or more groups, continuous data
Chi-squareRelationship between two categorical (nominal) variables
Pearson correlationWhether two continuous variables move together, no distinct groups
