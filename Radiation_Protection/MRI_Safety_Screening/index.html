MRI Safety Screening

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: A Different Kind of Hazard

MRI doesn't use ionizing radiation, but the MR environment has its own serious, unique safety hazards — and as RT departments increasingly use MRI for simulation and treatment planning, screening awareness matters even outside a dedicated MRI department.

The defining hazard of an MRI suite is its strong static magnetic field, which is always on — even when the scanner isn't actively imaging. Unlike a LINAC, you can't just walk away from the hazard by leaving the beam off; the magnet's field is a constant, permanent feature of the room.

Page 2: The Three-Tier Safety Classification

Every implant, device, and piece of equipment used in or near the MR environment must be labeled with one of three official classifications:

LabelMeaning
MR UnsafeShould never enter the MRI scanner room. Patients with an MR Unsafe device should not be scanned.
MR ConditionalMay safely enter the scanner room only under the very specific conditions stated in its labeling.
MR SafePoses no safety hazard anywhere in the MR environment. No scanning restrictions.

Quick Check!

A patient has a device labeled "MR Conditional." Is it automatically safe to scan them?

B. "Conditional" means safe only under specific, verified conditions — never assume it's safe without confirming the exact conditions are met for that specific scanner.

Page 3: What "Conditional" Actually Means

For a device intended to enter the bore of the MRI system, its labeling must be matched against the specific scanner being used, checking:

  • Static field strength (e.g., is this rated for 1.5T, 3T?)
  • Maximum spatial field gradient
  • dB/dt limitations (usually only relevant for active implants like pacemakers)
  • SAR limits (Specific Absorption Rate — RF energy deposition)
  • Any other device-specific conditions, such as restrictions on which coils may be used

Expected temperature rise and imaging artifact extent may also be listed — these inform the risk/benefit decision but are not pass/fail conditions the way the items above are.

Page 4: Equipment That Stays Outside the Bore

Items not intended to enter the bore — like most ancillary room equipment — are conditional in a different way: they typically carry gauss line positioning restrictions (must stay outside a specified field-strength boundary) or must be tethered or affixed to an immovable part of the room.

This is why oxygen tanks, IV poles, and other equipment brought into an MR suite must be MR-rated versions specifically, not just "whatever was in the supply closet." A standard ferromagnetic oxygen tank becomes a dangerous projectile in a strong magnetic field — this is called the missile effect, and it's the signature hazard unique to the MR environment.

Page 5: Screening Patients — Implants and Foreign Bodies

Before any patient enters the MR environment, screening covers several categories:

  • Biomedical implants — pacemakers, defibrillators, aneurysm clips, cochlear implants, neurostimulators. Many modern devices are MR Conditional, but this must be verified per-device, never assumed.
  • Ferrous foreign bodies — metal shavings or fragments (a real concern for patients with a occupational metalworking history), shrapnel. Ocular metal fragments are a particular concern given proximity to the eye.

Page 6: Screening Patients — Conditions and External Items

  • Medical conditions — pregnancy is specifically screened for and factors into the risk/benefit discussion
  • Prior diagnostic or surgical procedures — surgical hardware, clips, or stents from past procedures may not be documented in the chart the technologist has access to
  • Topical or externally applied items — tattoos (some pigments contain metallic compounds), medication patches (may have a metallic backing), body piercings, monitoring devices, and clothing with metal components (zippers, underwire)

Quick Check!

A patient has a medication patch. Why does this matter for MRI screening?

B. Metallic-backed patches can heat significantly during scanning, posing a real burn risk — this is exactly why "topical or externally applied items" is its own screening category.

Page 7: Ancillary Equipment in the MR Suite

Beyond the patient, everything else brought into the scanner room needs the same scrutiny: oxygen tanks, IV poles, monitoring equipment, wheelchairs, and stretchers. All of these need MR-safe or MR-conditional versions specifically designed for the MR environment — a standard hospital wheelchair is a serious missile-effect hazard.

Page 8: The RTT's Role

Even in a radiation therapy department that primarily uses MRI for simulation and fusion rather than diagnostic imaging, the same screening discipline applies every time a patient or piece of equipment approaches the scanner. When in doubt about a device's classification, the answer is always to verify against its actual labeling — never assume based on appearance, general device category, or what a similar-looking device was rated for.

Wrap-up: you've covered the three-tier MR Safe/Conditional/Unsafe classification system, what "conditional" requires for bore vs. non-bore equipment, the missile effect, and the categories of patient and equipment screening.
