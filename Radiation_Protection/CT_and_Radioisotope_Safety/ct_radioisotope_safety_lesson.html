CT and Radioisotope Safety

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: Two Very Different Modalities, One Safety Lens

CT and PET/nuclear medicine work completely differently — one uses an external X-ray tube, the other uses radioactive tracers injected into the patient — but both raise real dose and safety questions RTTs need to understand, especially as fusion imaging with these modalities becomes more common in treatment planning.

Page 2: CT Dose Optimization — Pitch

Pitch is the relationship between how far the patient couch moves per rotation and the width of the X-ray beam. When pitch is approximately 1, spiral CT dose is comparable to conventional CT.

Increasing pitch (e.g., from 1 to 1.5) means the couch moves farther per rotation relative to the beam width — covering more anatomy per rotation, which decreases patient dose (at some potential cost to image quality/noise if not compensated for elsewhere).

Quick Check!

Increasing CT pitch from 1 to 1.5 generally has what effect on patient dose?

B. Higher pitch covers more anatomy per rotation, which generally decreases patient dose.

Page 3: Tube Current Modulation and Iterative Reconstruction

Tube current modulation is an automatic exposure control method that adjusts mA in real time based on patient anatomy as the scan progresses — reducing dose in thinner body regions without sacrificing image quality where more attenuation exists.

Iterative reconstruction is a repeated data-processing technique that reconstructs CT images with the lowest possible noise — allowing the raw scan to be acquired at a lower dose while still producing a diagnostically usable image.

Page 4: CTDIvol and Interslice Scatter

CTDIvol (CT Dose Index, volume) is a standardized dose marker representing the average absorbed dose within the scanned volume — the number you'll see reported directly on the scanner console for every study.

Interslice scatter refers to radiation scattering from the slice actually being imaged into adjacent slices — a real, if secondary, contributor to total dose in a multi-slice acquisition.

Patient positioning matters more than it might seem: miscentering a patient by just 2 cm in the CT gantry can increase their radiation dose by as much as 25% — automatic exposure control systems are calibrated assuming correct centering.

Quick Check!

A patient is miscentered by 2 cm in the CT gantry. What's the approximate dose impact?

B. Even a small 2 cm miscentering error can increase dose by as much as 25%, since automatic exposure control assumes correct centering.

Page 5: How PET Actually Works

PET (Positron Emission Tomography) examines metabolic processes rather than anatomy directly. A radioactive tracer — most commonly FDG (fluorodeoxyglucose), a radioactive glucose analog — is injected and taken up preferentially by metabolically active cells, including many cancers.

As the tracer's radioisotope decays, it emits a positron, which almost immediately collides with a nearby electron. This collision produces annihilation radiation: two 511-keV photons traveling in opposite directions, which the PET scanner detects in coincidence to localize where the decay occurred.

Quick Check!

PET imaging is fundamentally based on detecting:

B. PET detects the two 511-keV annihilation photons produced when an emitted positron collides with an electron — that's the entire basis of the imaging technique.

Page 6: Radioisotopes in Nuclear Medicine

Radioisotopes are unstable atoms that spontaneously transform to reach a more stable configuration — recall from the Radioactivity lesson that isotopes share the same number of protons but differ in neutron count.

Technetium-99m is the most widely used short-lived radioisotope in diagnostic nuclear medicine, valued for its short half-life (minimizing patient dose) and favorable gamma energy for imaging.

Page 7: Radiological Emergency Response

A dirty bomb (radioactive dispersal device) combines a conventional explosive with radioactive material — the danger is primarily from the explosion and contamination spread, not necessarily from a high radiation dose.

In a radiological emergency specifically involving radioactive iodine, potassium iodide (KI) can be administered to saturate the thyroid gland with stable (non-radioactive) iodine, blocking uptake of the radioactive form and reducing thyroid dose and cancer risk.

Quick Check!

What can be administered to block thyroid uptake of radioactive iodine in an emergency?

A. Potassium iodide saturates the thyroid with stable iodine, blocking uptake of the radioactive form.
