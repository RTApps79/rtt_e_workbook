Page 1: The Three Pillars of Radiation Protection

Radiation protection is the protection of people from the harmful effects of ionizing radiation exposure. It's achieved through three principles: justification (the benefit must outweigh the risk), limitation (dose limits aren't exceeded), and optimization (exposure kept as low as reasonably achievable) — the same ALARA framework covered in its own lesson, built on Time, Distance, and Shielding.

Page 3: Dose Equivalent and Effective Dose

Dose Equivalent: EqD = Dose (Gy) × Wr (radiation weighting factor, based on the radiation's LET)

Effective Dose: EfD = Wr × Wt (tissue weighting factor) × Dose — accounts for partial-body or nonuniform irradiation.

Worked example: Alpha particles have Wr = 20. The lung has Wt = 0.12. If the lungs receive an absorbed dose of 0.5 Gy from alpha radiation:
EfD = 20 × 0.12 × 0.5 = 1.2 Sv

Tissue weighting factors: gonads 0.25, breast 0.15, red bone marrow/lung/colon/stomach 0.12, bladder/liver/esophagus 0.05, thyroid/bone surfaces 0.03, skin 0.01.

Quick Check!

X-rays have a Wr of 1. If the thyroid (Wt = 0.03) receives an absorbed dose of 2 Gy from X-rays, what's the effective dose?

A. EfD = Wr × Wt × Dose = 1 × 0.03 × 2 = 0.06 Sv.

Page 4: Occupational and Public Dose Limits

Dose limits are set by the NCRP and follow a linear, non-threshold dose-response relationship — any dose carries some risk, so limits aim to keep that risk acceptably low.

Population / SiteLimit
Radiation worker, whole body (annual)50 mSv/yr
Radiation worker, cumulative10 mSv × age (e.g., 20 y/o worker: 200 mSv lifetime)
Organs and extremities500 mSv/yr
Lens of eye (occupational)150 mSv/yr
General public (whole body)1 mSv/yr (1/10 of occupational)
General public, lens of eye15 mSv/yr

Notice the general public limit is consistently 1/10 of the occupational limit for the same site — a pattern worth remembering rather than memorizing each number in isolation.

Page 5: The Pregnant Worker

The first trimester is when the embryo is most radiosensitive. NCRP guidance calls for voluntary disclosure of pregnancy — a worker isn't required to disclose, but once she notifies her supervisor, she becomes a Declared Pregnant Worker (DPW).

Once declared, fetal dose must not exceed 0.5 mSv/month and 5 mSv for the entire pregnancy. A second monitoring badge is worn at waist level (rather than collar level) to better estimate fetal dose.

Quick Check!

Is a pregnant worker required to disclose her pregnancy to become a Declared Pregnant Worker?

B. NCRP guidance treats disclosure as voluntary — the DPW status and its added protections only begin once she chooses to notify her supervisor.

UNSCEAR / BEIRStudy and report on the biological effects of ionizing radiation
EPA / FDAEnvironmental and product/device-related radiation oversight

Every institution using radiation-producing equipment or radioactive materials must have a Radiation Safety Officer (RSO), who specifies required survey meters and dosimeters, supervises their calibration and proper use, and reviews personnel dose reports for anomalies.

Page 7: Radiation Detection Instruments

Beyond personal dosimeters, several instruments detect and measure radiation in the environment or at the treatment unit:

InstrumentPrimary use
Ionization chamberCounts ion pairs; calibrates linear accelerators and measures treatment beam characteristics
Geiger-Müller (GM) counterGas-filled tube, audible signal; surveys rooms/personnel after implants, finds lost sealed sources
Portable ionization chamber ("cutie pie")Surveys high-radioactivity storage areas and around machines like HDR units
Scintillation counterSodium iodide crystal + photomultiplier tube; highly sensitive, mainly used in nuclear medicine
Proportional counterGas-filled neutron detector; discriminates contamination type, used in some CT scanners

Quick Check!

Which instrument is used to calibrate a linear accelerator's output?

B. Ionization chambers are the standard instrument for calibrating LINAC output and measuring beam characteristics.

Page 8: Personnel Monitoring Devices

Personal dosimeters fall into two categories: active (electronic dosimeters, pocket ionization chambers — give immediate readings) and passive (film badges, TLDs, OSLs — require processing).

DeviceHow it worksWorth knowing
Pocket ionization chamberGas-filled chamber, reads exposure directlyImmediate, accurate reading; but expensive, easily damaged, no permanent record
Film badgeFilm between metal filters (Al, Cu, Pb, or Sn)Inexpensive and accurate; but can't be worn long-term, can fog from heat/humidity
TLD (Thermoluminescent Dosimeter)Lithium fluoride (LiF) crystals emit light proportional to dose when heatedReusable, won't fog, wearable up to 3 months; but no permanent record after readout
OSL (Optically Stimulated Luminescence)Aluminum oxide (Al₂O₃) crystals, read via laser stimulationMore sensitive than film/TLD, can be reanalyzed, long-term stability

Page 9: Wearing and Reading Your Monitor

Monitors are worn at collar level, above the waist, near the thyroid — the area typically receiving the highest exposure. Declared Pregnant Workers wear a second badge at waist level.

General rules: never share badges, never intentionally expose them, report accidental exposure or contamination immediately, don't wear your badge during your own medical imaging/treatment, and never take it to another institution.

A dosimeter badge report breaks dose into specific types: DDE (Deep Dose Equivalent, whole-body at 1 cm depth), LDE (Lens Dose Equivalent, at 0.3 cm depth), SDE,WB (Shallow Dose Equivalent, whole body, 0.007 cm depth), and SDE,ME (Shallow Dose Equivalent, maximum extremity).

Quick Check!

You accidentally leave your film badge on a counter near a treatment room overnight. What should you do?

B. Accidental badge exposures must be reported right away so the reading isn't misattributed to real occupational dose.
