Facility Safety Systems

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: Controlling Exposure at the Facility Level

Personal dosimeters tell you how much dose someone received — but a facility also needs physical and procedural systems that prevent unnecessary exposure from happening in the first place. This lesson covers area classification, warning systems, interlocks, and shielding design.

Page 2: Controlled vs. Uncontrolled Areas

Area typeDefinitionDose limit
Controlled areaAccess is controlled specifically for radiation protection; must be clearly marked1 mSv/week (50 mSv/year)
Uncontrolled areaIndividuals present aren't monitored by a radiation safety program0.1 mSv/week (5 mSv/year)

Notice the uncontrolled area limit is 1/10th of the controlled area limit — the same public-vs-occupational ratio that shows up throughout radiation protection.

Quick Check!

A hospital hallway adjacent to a treatment vault, where members of the public may walk without radiation monitoring, is best classified as:

B. Since the people present aren't monitored by a radiation safety program, this space is classified — and dose-limited — as uncontrolled.

Page 3: Warning Signs and Lights

Entrances to treatment rooms must be clearly marked with signs warning of potential radiation presence.

Important distinction: warning lights (at the control panel, room entrance, and on the treatment machine) indicate the machine is turned on — they do not necessarily indicate radiation is actually being produced at that instant. Don't confuse "machine powered on" with "beam on."

Page 4: Door Interlocks

Treatment room entrances are engineered so that opening the door automatically turns off the machine or returns a source to a safe shielded position. This is a hard physical safety mechanism, not just a procedural rule — it functions even if a therapist forgets a step.

Page 5: Staying Connected to the Patient

Because the therapist must leave the room during beam-on, maintaining both visual and auditory contact with the patient is essential — typically via closed-circuit TV and a two-way intercom. This isn't just about patient comfort; it's how a therapist would immediately notice a medical emergency or a patient movement problem during treatment.

Page 6: Emergency Off Controls

Emergency off buttons immediately remove electrical power from the treatment machine. They're placed in multiple locations for a reason — treatment room walls, on the treatment couch itself, near the exit, and at the control panel — so that whoever needs to stop the machine, wherever they are in the room, can reach one quickly.

Quick Check!

Why are emergency off controls placed in multiple locations throughout the treatment room?

A. Multiple emergency-off locations exist so that in an actual emergency, someone doesn't have to cross the room to reach a single control.

Page 7: Mazes and Shielding Construction

Treatment rooms are physically built to prevent radiation leakage and scattered radiation from escaping a high-dose area:

  • Mazes — bent hallway entrances that force scattered radiation to bounce multiple times (losing energy each time) before it could reach an open doorway, instead of a straight shot out
  • Doors — lead-lined, often around 7 inches thick, and can weigh up to 4,200 lbs
  • Walls — thick concrete construction, engineered based on the beam energy the room is designed for

This is why treatment vaults can't simply be relocated within a building without significant construction — the room itself is part of the radiation safety system.

Page 8: Wrap-Up

You've covered controlled vs. uncontrolled area classification, warning signs and lights, door interlocks, patient communication systems, emergency off controls, and maze/shielding construction.

Together with Personnel Monitoring & Dosimetry and ALARA, this completes the core radiation protection picture: how much dose is tracked, how it's limited by policy, and how the facility itself is physically built to control it.
