Why MRI is used in RT

MRI is increasingly used in radiotherapy because it provides excellent soft-tissue contrast, can show functional information, and supports real-time imaging without ionizing radiation [file:20].

  • Improves tumor and organ visualization.
  • Helps setup accuracy and adaptive workflows.
  • Supports motion tracking without extra dose.

MRI hazards in RT

In the RT environment, MRI safety concerns come from the static magnetic field, gradient fields, and RF energy used during imaging [file:20].

  • Static field: attraction, torque, projectile risk.
  • Gradient field: induced currents, nerve stimulation, noise.
  • RF field: heating and burn risk, especially with conductive materials.

Static field risk

The static magnetic field can exert translational force and torque on ferromagnetic objects, creating dangerous projectile effects if equipment or devices are brought too close [file:20].

  • Risk increases with stronger fields and proximity.
  • Material composition matters.
  • The 5-gauss line is a key safety boundary.

Gradient and RF hazards

Rapidly switched gradient fields can induce currents, stimulation, and acoustic noise, while RF energy can heat tissue and conductive materials [file:20].

  • Gradient switching: dB/dt-related effects.
  • RF exposure: SAR and heating.
  • Conductive loops and skin contact can increase burn risk.

Zoning and access

The ACR four-zone concept helps manage MRI access in radiotherapy settings, with progressively more restricted access from public areas to the scanner room [file:20].

  • Zone I: public access.
  • Zone II: controlled transition area.
  • Zone III: screened area with restricted access.
  • Zone IV: scanner room.

Screening workflow

Screening should begin as early as possible, ideally before the simulation or treatment visit, and should be repeated daily because safety status can change over a course of treatment [file:20].

  • Use questionnaires and record review.
  • Confirm implants, devices, and accessories.
  • Perform daily checks before each fraction.
  • Use ferromagnetic screening when indicated.

Device labeling

Items and devices used near the MR environment should be identified as MR Safe, MR Conditional, or MR Unsafe, with MR Conditional devices only used under specified conditions [file:21].

  • MR Unsafe: do not enter the scanner room.
  • MR Conditional: only under labeled conditions.
  • MR Safe: no known MR hazards.
Check: What does MR Conditional mean?

Implants and accessories

Passive implants, fiducials, immobilization devices, and carbon-fiber accessories can cause forces, heating, image artifacts, or geometric distortion depending on their material and configuration [file:20].

  • Implants may displace or heat.
  • Carbon fiber can create susceptibility artifacts.
  • Vacuum cushions and loops require burn prevention.

QA equipment in RT

Physics QA devices often need special evaluation because of projectile risk, electronic interference, image artifacts, and measurement changes in the MR environment [file:20].

  • Check for ferromagnetic parts.
  • Keep sensitive electronics outside the 5-gauss line.
  • Consider motor and sensor redesigns for MR use.

Training and review

Safe MRI practice in RT requires written policies, regular review, and training for staff working in the MR environment, especially in zones III and IV [file:20].

  • Use onboarding plus annual refresher education.
  • Define MR personnel responsibilities clearly.
  • Update procedures as technology and workflows change.
