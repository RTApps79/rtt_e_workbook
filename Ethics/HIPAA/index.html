Page 3: What is HIPAA?

HIPAA stands for the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996. It establishes national standards to protect sensitive patient health information, safeguard privacy, and allow necessary information flow.

Key Terms:

  • Protected Health Information (PHI): Identifiable health information in any form.
  • Covered Entities: Providers, plans, clearinghouses.
  • Business Associates: External partners with PHI access.

Its goal is to foster trust by protecting PHI privacy.

Page 4: The HIPAA Privacy Rule

The Privacy Rule sets standards for using and sharing PHI.

Key Patient Rights:

  • Access their PHI.
  • Amend their PHI.
  • Request Restrictions on uses/disclosures.
  • Receive an Accounting of Disclosures.
  • Request Confidential Communications.

Permitted Uses & Disclosures:

Allowed for Treatment, Payment, Operations (TPO); To the Individual; Incidental (with safeguards); and Required by Law.

The Minimum Necessary Standard applies: use/disclose only the minimum PHI needed.

Page 5: The HIPAA Security Rule

The Security Rule protects Electronic PHI (ePHI) - its Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability.

Requires three types of safeguards:

  • Administrative: Policies, training, risk analysis, workforce clearance.
  • Physical: Workstation security, facility access controls, device/media controls.
  • Technical: Access controls (passwords), audit controls, integrity controls, transmission security (encryption).

Page 6: Breach Notification Rule

A "breach" is an impermissible use or disclosure of PHI that compromises its security or privacy, unless there's a low probability PHI was compromised (based on risk assessment).

The rule requires notifying: Affected Individuals, HHS (Dept. of Health & Human Services), and sometimes the Media (if >500 individuals affected).

Know your facility's internal breach reporting procedure.

Quick Check!

If a stolen, unencrypted laptop contains the ePHI of 600 patients, who must be notified?

Correct! Since more than 500 individuals are affected, individuals, HHS, and prominent media outlets in the jurisdiction must be notified.

Page 7: Common Dilemmas & Scenarios (Part 1)

Dilemma 1: "Elevator Talk"

Don't discuss patients in public areas. Best Practice: Wait for a private, secure area.

Dilemma 2: Social Media Sharing

Avoid photos in clinical areas. Best Practice: Never post PHI or patient images/details (even de-identified if potentially re-identifiable) without explicit, written patient consent for that specific purpose.

Quick Check!

Is confirming a patient's presence by name to someone asking in a busy waiting room a HIPAA violation?

Correct! Yes, it likely is. Disclosing even a patient's presence without authorization can be a violation. Follow facility policy for directory information.

Page 8: Common Dilemmas & Scenarios (Part 2)

Dilemma 3: Family/Friend Inquiries

Don't disclose PHI without patient authorization or if not permitted by HIPAA (e.g., patient incapacitated and disclosure is in their best interest and they haven't objected). Best Practice: Politely decline, cite privacy, direct to facility's patient information process.

Dilemma 4: "Just Looking" in the EMR

Accessing records out of curiosity is a violation. Best Practice: Only access PHI for legitimate job-related reasons (role-based access).

Quick Check!

Is leaving an Electronic Medical Record (EMR) screen open and unattended briefly acceptable if you're nearby?

Correct! No. Always lock or log off your workstation when leaving it unattended, even briefly, to prevent unauthorized access.

Page 9: HIPAA, Ethics, and Your Responsibility

HIPAA compliance is not just a legal requirement; it's an ethical obligation. It aligns closely with professional codes of conduct, such as the ARRT Code of Ethics (specifically Principle 9 regarding confidentiality) and Rules of Ethics (Rule 18 regarding improper management of patient records).

Your Role as an RTT:

  • Be Vigilant: Always be mindful of patient privacy and PHI security.
  • Know Policies: Understand and follow your facility's HIPAA policies and procedures.
  • Ask Questions: If unsure about a HIPAA-related issue, seek clarification from your supervisor or compliance officer.
  • Report Concerns: Promptly report potential violations or breaches according to facility protocol.
  • Keep Learning: Stay updated on HIPAA regulations and best practices.

Upholding HIPAA builds patient trust and maintains the integrity of the healthcare profession.
