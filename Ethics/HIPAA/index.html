Page 3: What is HIPAA?

HIPAA stands for the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996. It's a US federal law designed to establish national standards to protect sensitive patient health information, safeguard privacy, and allow the necessary flow of health information for quality care.

Key Terms:

  • Protected Health Information (PHI): Any health information that can be linked to a specific individual (name, address, health status, etc.) in any form (electronic, paper, oral).
  • Covered Entities: Healthcare providers (like RTTs), health plans, and healthcare clearinghouses.
  • Business Associates: External persons or companies helping covered entities with access to PHI.

The primary goal is to protect PHI privacy, fostering trust in the healthcare system.

Page 4: The HIPAA Privacy Rule

The Privacy Rule sets the standards for who can use and share PHI.

Key Patient Rights:

  • Right to Access: Inspect and get a copy of their PHI.
  • Right to Amend: Request corrections to inaccurate PHI.
  • Right to Request Restrictions: Ask for limits on PHI use/sharing.
  • Right to an Accounting of Disclosures: Ask for a list of certain PHI shares.
  • Right to Confidential Communications: Request specific contact methods.

Permitted Uses & Disclosures:

PHI can be used/disclosed *without* specific authorization for: TPO (Treatment, Payment, Operations), To the Individual, Incidental Disclosures (with safeguards), and When Required by Law.

The Minimum Necessary Standard requires limiting PHI use/sharing to the minimum needed.

Page 5: The HIPAA Security Rule

The Security Rule focuses on Protecting Electronic PHI (ePHI), ensuring its Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability.

This involves three types of safeguards:

  • Administrative Safeguards: Policies, procedures, risk analyses, training, sanctions.
  • Physical Safeguards: Protecting physical access, securing workstations, monitor positioning.
  • Technical Safeguards: Access controls (passwords), audit controls, integrity controls, transmission security (encryption).

Regular review and updating of these safeguards is essential.

Page 6: Breach Notification Rule

A "breach" is an impermissible use or disclosure that compromises PHI privacy or security.

The rule dictates procedures for notifying: Affected Individuals (within 60 days), HHS, and (if >500) the Media.

It's crucial to know your facility's procedure for reporting potential breaches internally.

Quick Check!

If a stolen, unencrypted laptop contains the ePHI of 600 patients, who must be notified?

Correct! Since more than 500 individuals are affected, individuals, HHS, and likely the media must be notified.

Page 7: Common Dilemmas & Scenarios (Part 1)

Dilemma 1: "Elevator Talk"

Discussing patients in public areas is a potential violation. Best Practice: Wait until you are in a private, secure area.

Dilemma 2: Social Media Sharing

Photos in clinical areas can inadvertently capture PHI. Best Practice: Avoid taking photos/videos in patient care areas. Never post patient images without authorization.

Quick Check!

A nurse asks you in the busy waiting room if "John Smith" has finished his treatment yet. Is it okay to answer "Yes, he's just getting down"?

Correct! No, confirming someone is a patient and their status in a public area is a potential violation. Suggest moving to a private space.

Page 8: Common Dilemmas & Scenarios (Part 2)

Dilemma 3: Family/Friend Inquiries

You cannot disclose PHI unless authorized. Best Practice: Politely decline, citing privacy laws.

Dilemma 4: "Just Looking" in the EMR

Accessing PHI out of curiosity is a serious violation. Best Practice: Never access a record without a job-related reason.

Quick Check!

You leave a patient's EMR open on the console screen while you step out briefly to grab a supply. Is this acceptable?

Correct! No. Unattended screens must be locked or logged off.

Page 9: HIPAA, Ethics, and Your Responsibility

HIPAA aligns with ARRT Code Principle 9 (Privacy) and Rule 18 (Violating Federal Rules).

Your Role as an RTT:

  • Be Vigilant.
  • Know Your Policies.
  • Ask Questions.
  • Report Concerns.
  • Continuous Learning.

Upholding HIPAA builds trust and maintains professional integrity.
