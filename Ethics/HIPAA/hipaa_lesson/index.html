Page 3: What is HIPAA?

HIPAA stands for the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996. It establishes national standards to protect sensitive patient health information, safeguard privacy, and allow necessary information flow.

Key Terms:

  • Protected Health Information (PHI): Identifiable health information in any form.
  • Covered Entities: Providers, plans, clearinghouses.
  • Business Associates: External partners with PHI access.

Its goal is to foster trust by protecting PHI privacy.

Page 4: The HIPAA Privacy Rule

The Privacy Rule sets standards for using and sharing PHI.

Key Patient Rights:

  • Access, Amend, Request Restrictions, Accounting of Disclosures, Confidential Communications.

Permitted Uses & Disclosures:

Allowed for TPO, To the Individual, Incidental (with safeguards), and Required by Law.

The Minimum Necessary Standard applies.

Page 5: The HIPAA Security Rule

The Security Rule protects Electronic PHI (ePHI) - its Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability.

Requires three types of safeguards:

  • Administrative: Policies, training, risk analysis.
  • Physical: Workstation security, access control.
  • Technical: Passwords, audits, encryption.

Page 6: Breach Notification Rule

A "breach" is an impermissible use or disclosure.

The rule requires notifying: Affected Individuals, HHS, and sometimes the Media.

Know your facility's internal reporting procedure.

Quick Check!

If a stolen, unencrypted laptop contains the ePHI of 600 patients, who must be notified?

Correct! Since more than 500 individuals are affected, individuals, HHS, and likely the media must be notified.

Page 7: Common Dilemmas & Scenarios (Part 1)

Dilemma 1: "Elevator Talk"

Don't discuss patients in public areas. Best Practice: Wait for a private area.

Dilemma 2: Social Media Sharing

Avoid photos in clinical areas. Best Practice: Never post PHI or patient images without explicit consent.

Quick Check!

Is confirming a patient's presence in a busy waiting room a violation?

Correct! No (meaning, it *is* a violation). Confirming status in public breaches privacy.

Page 8: Common Dilemmas & Scenarios (Part 2)

Dilemma 3: Family/Friend Inquiries

Don't disclose without authorization. Best Practice: Politely decline, cite privacy.

Dilemma 4: "Just Looking" in the EMR

Accessing records out of curiosity is a violation. Best Practice: Never access without a job reason.

Quick Check!

Is leaving an EMR open unattended briefly acceptable?

Correct! No. Always lock or log off.

Page 9: HIPAA, Ethics, and Your Responsibility

Upholding HIPAA builds trust.
