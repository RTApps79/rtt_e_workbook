Foundations of Professionalism: Understanding ARRT Standards of Ethics

Interactive Lesson: The ARRT Standards of Ethics

Page 1: Introduction & How to Use This Lesson

The Standards of Ethics of The American Registry of Radiologic Technologists (ARRT) apply to all currently or formerly certified/registered individuals, and to applicants. Radiologic Technology encompasses disciplines like radiography, radiation therapy, nuclear medicine, and more.

Statement of Purpose: The primary purpose of these ethics requirements is to identify individuals who have internalized professional values that lead them to act in the best interests of patients. These standards provide proactive guidance, promote ethical behavior, and support ARRT's mission of high standards in patient care by restricting those who exhibit inconsistent behavior.

The Standards consist of two main parts: the Code of Ethics and the Rules of Ethics.

Page 3: Introduction to ARRT Standards of Ethics

The Standards of Ethics of The American Registry of Radiologic Technologists (ARRT) apply to all currently or formerly certified/registered individuals, and to applicants. Radiologic Technology encompasses disciplines like radiography, radiation therapy, nuclear medicine, and more.

Statement of Purpose: The primary purpose of these ethics requirements is to identify individuals who have internalized professional values that lead them to act in the best interests of patients. These standards provide proactive guidance, promote ethical behavior, and support ARRT's mission of high standards in patient care by restricting those who exhibit inconsistent behavior.

The Standards consist of two main parts: the Code of Ethics and the Rules of Ethics.

Page 4: The ARRT Code of Ethics - Guiding Principles (Part 1)

The Code of Ethics is an aspirational guide for professional conduct related to patients, employers, colleagues, and other healthcare team members. It aims to maintain high ethical conduct and ensure patient protection, safety, and comfort.

Key Principles Include:

  • Principle 1: Act professionally, respond to patient needs, and support colleagues in providing quality care.
  • Principle 2: Advance the profession's objective to serve humanity with full respect for dignity.
  • Principle 3: Deliver care unrestricted by personal attributes or illness nature, and without discrimination.
  • Principle 4: Practice based on theoretical knowledge, use equipment appropriately, and employ proper procedures.
  • Principle 5: Assess situations, exercise care and judgment, assume responsibility, and act in the patient's best interest.

Quick Check!

A technologist refuses to provide care to a patient based on their national origin. This directly violates which principle of the Code of Ethics?

Principle 3 states: "The Registered Technologist delivers patient care and service... without discrimination on the basis of race, color, creed, religion, national origin...".

Page 5: The ARRT Code of Ethics - Guiding Principles (Part 2)

Continuing with the aspirational Code of Ethics:

  • Principle 6: Act as an agent by observation and communication to obtain pertinent information for diagnosis/treatment, recognizing interpretation and diagnosis are outside the RTT scope.
  • Principle 7: Use equipment and procedures according to accepted standards, minimizing radiation exposure to all.
  • Principle 8: Practice ethically and protect the patient's right to quality care.
  • Principle 9: Respect patient confidences and privacy, revealing information only as required by law or to protect welfare.
  • Principle 10: Continually strive to improve knowledge and skills through CE, sharing knowledge, and investigating new practices.
  • Principle 11: Refrain from using illegal drugs or controlled substances that impair professional judgment or ability to practice safely.

Quick Check!

An RTT discussing a patient's identifiable medical information with a friend outside of work, without any legal requirement or welfare concern, would violate Principle 9 of the Code of Ethics.

Yes, Principle 9 emphasizes respecting patient confidences and privacy.

Page 6: Introduction to the ARRT Rules of Ethics

The Rules of Ethics are the second part of the Standards. Unlike the Code, these are mandatory and enforceable standards of minimally acceptable professional conduct for all RTTs and Candidates. They are intended to promote the protection, safety, and comfort of patients.

Key Points:

  • Enforceable: Violations are subject to sanctions.
  • Duty to Report: Registered Technologists must notify ARRT of any ethics violations (their own or others'), including state licensing issues and criminal charges/convictions, within 30 days or at annual renewal, whichever is first. Applicants must report within 30 days of occurrence.

Quick Check!

Are the ARRT Rules of Ethics merely suggestions for best practice?

No, the Rules of Ethics are mandatory and enforceable standards of minimally acceptable professional conduct.

Page 7: Highlights from the Rules of Ethics - Part 1

Violations subject RTTs/Candidates to sanctions. Categories include:

1. Fraud or Deceptive Practices:
  • In procuring/maintaining certification, employment, or state licenses (e.g., altering documents, falsely claiming ARRT registration).
  • False/misleading communications about education, credentials, or experience.
  • Fraudulent billing practices (e.g., Medicare/Medicaid fraud).
2. Subversion:
  • Subverting ARRT exams, CQR (including SSAs), or Education Requirements. This includes disclosing exam/SSA info, soliciting/receiving exam/SSA info, copying/transmitting materials, or providing false CE/clinical info.
  • Failure to cooperate with ARRT investigations, including making false statements.

Quick Check!

Sharing specific questions remembered from an ARRT certification exam with students preparing for the exam is acceptable professional behavior.

No, this is a form of examination subversion.

Page 8: Highlights from the Rules of Ethics - Part 2

3. Unprofessional Conduct:
  • Failure to conform to minimal acceptable standards or governmental rules regarding practice; or practice that may create unnecessary danger.
  • Sexual misconduct with a patient or sexually demeaning behavior.
  • Unethical conduct likely to deceive, defraud, or harm the public or patient.
4. Scope of Practice Violations:
  • Performing procedures one is not competent to perform.
  • Improper delegation or acceptance of functions that could endanger a patient.
5. Fitness to Practice: Inability to practice with reasonable skill/safety due to illness, alcohol/drugs, or mental/physical condition.
6. Improper Management of Patient Records: False entries or failing to protect confidential patient information.

Quick Check!

An RTT documents that a complex procedure was performed when, in fact, a simpler version was done. Is this an ethical violation?

Yes, this would be improper management of patient records by making a false entry, and potentially fraudulent billing.

Page 9: Highlights from the Rules of Ethics - Part 3 & Duty to Report

7. Violation of State/Federal Law or Regulatory Rule:
  • Violating narcotics or controlled substance laws.
  • Violating a rule from a state/federal authority or certification board leading to licensure/certification action, or failing to report such actions to ARRT.
  • Criminal proceedings (convictions, guilt findings even if adjudication withheld, certain pleas, sex offender registration).
8. Duty to Report:
  • Knowing of a violation by any RTT or Candidate and failing to promptly report it in writing to ARRT.
  • Failing to immediately report to one's supervisor an error made in connection with patient care (imaging, treating, or caring) that could be potentially harmful, unethical, or improper. This duty exists whether or not actual injury occurred.

Ethical Crossroads: Case Study Activity

Instructions

Read each scenario. Consider the ARRT Code of Ethics and Rules of Ethics discussed in the lesson. Using the text areas, respond to the prompts. Focus on identifying the ethical principles/rules at play, potential consequences, and the most appropriate course of action for an RTT or student.

Interaction Note: If this lesson is part of a course, consider discussing your responses with peers or your instructor to gain different perspectives.

Scenario 1: Confidentiality and Social Media

You are a radiation therapy student. On your lunch break, you see a social media post from a fellow student in your program. The post doesn't name patients but describes a challenging case treated that day, including some details about the patient's condition and treatment plan. The post ends with, "#radiationtherapy #savinglives".

Ethical Analysis Prompts:

1. ARRT Standards: Which principles from the ARRT Code of Ethics (e.g., patient privacy, professionalism) and which specific ARRT Rules of Ethics (e.g., relating to patient information) are potentially being violated by your classmate's post? Explain why.
2. Your Responsibility: According to the ARRT Rules of Ethics (Rule 21), what is your ethical obligation if you believe a violation has occurred? What are the potential consequences if you ignore the post?
3. Course of Action: Describe the steps you would take, considering both direct communication with your classmate and your reporting responsibilities. How would you apply principles of effective interprofessional communication and emotional intelligence in this situation?

Scenario 2: Resource Allocation and Patient Advocacy

You are an RTT student working with a patient experiencing significant side effects. The patient expresses concern about affording medications and transportation. Your RTT preceptor seems dismissive, saying, "Everyone struggles, they just need to figure it out." You know there are resources available.

Ethical Analysis Prompts:

1. Ethical Principles: Which ARRT Code of Ethics principles (e.g., responding to patient needs, acting in patient's best interest, quality patient care) are relevant here? Does the preceptor's response align with these principles?
2. Student's Role & Responsibility: As a student, what is your ethical responsibility towards the patient in this situation? How does this relate to patient advocacy?
3. Navigating Hierarchy & Action: Considering your student status and the preceptor's comment, what are ethical and professional ways to ensure the patient's concerns are addressed? What specific ARRT Rules could be violated if the patient's needs are ignored, potentially leading to treatment interruption? (e.g., Rule 7, Unprofessional Conduct).

Scenario 3: Observing a Potential Error

During a treatment setup, you (student or RTT) notice your preceptor (or peer RTT) seems rushed and makes a small adjustment to the treatment machine that you believe might be incorrect based on the plan. You have a nagging feeling something isn't right.

Ethical Analysis Prompts:

1. Immediate Ethical Concern: Which ARRT Code of Ethics principles (e.g., patient safety, professional judgment, acting in patient's best interest) and Rules of Ethics (e.g., Rule 7, unprofessional conduct; Rule 22, duty to report error) are paramount here?
2. Communication Strategy (Assertiveness & EI): How would you voice your concern respectfully but assertively, especially if the person is more experienced or seems rushed? How can Emotional Intelligence help you here?
3. Consequences of Inaction vs. Action: What are the potential ethical and practical consequences if you say nothing and an error occurs? What if you speak up and are mistaken? How does the ARRT "Duty to Report Error" (Rule 22) apply even if no actual harm occurred?

Scenario 4: Impaired Colleague

An RTT, Sarah, notices her long-time colleague and friend, RTT John, has been frequently arriving late, seems disheveled, and she thought she smelled alcohol on his breath one morning. Today, during a complex setup, John seems uncharacteristically clumsy and irritable. Sarah is worried about patient safety and John's well-being.

Ethical Analysis Prompts:

1. ARRT Standards: Which ARRT Code of Ethics principles (e.g., supporting colleagues in quality care, patient safety) and Rules of Ethics (e.g., Rule 13, Fitness to Practice; Rule 21, Duty to Report Violation) are relevant to Sarah's observations?
2. Dilemma and Action: What is the ethical dilemma Sarah faces (loyalty to friend vs. patient safety/professional responsibility)? What immediate actions should Sarah consider taking to ensure patient safety for the current treatment?
3. Reporting Responsibilities: What are Sarah's responsibilities regarding reporting her concerns about John to her supervisor and potentially to ARRT, according to the Rules of Ethics? How can she do this professionally and ethically?

General Reflection on ARRT Standards of Ethics

1. Why is it important for the ARRT to have both an aspirational Code of Ethics and mandatory Rules of Ethics? How do they complement each other?
2. The "Duty to Report" (Rules 21 & 22) can be challenging. What factors might make it difficult for an RTT to report a colleague's ethical violation or an error, and how can these barriers be overcome?
3. How does adherence to the ARRT Standards of Ethics contribute to the overall quality and safety of patient care in radiation oncology and the trustworthiness of the profession?
