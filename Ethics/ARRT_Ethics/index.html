Page 1: Introduction & How to Use This Lesson

The Standards of Ethics of The American Registry of Radiologic Technologists (ARRT) apply to all currently or formerly certified/registered individuals, and to applicants. Radiologic Technology encompasses disciplines like radiography, radiation therapy, nuclear medicine, and more.

Statement of Purpose: The primary purpose of these ethics requirements is to identify individuals who have internalized professional values that lead them to act in the best interests of patients. These standards provide proactive guidance, promote ethical behavior, and support ARRT's mission of high standards in patient care by restricting those who exhibit inconsistent behavior.

The Standards consist of two main parts: the Code of Ethics and the Rules of Ethics.

Page 3: Introduction to ARRT Standards of Ethics

The Standards of Ethics of The American Registry of Radiologic Technologists (ARRT) apply to all currently or formerly certified/registered individuals, and to applicants. Radiologic Technology encompasses disciplines like radiography, radiation therapy, nuclear medicine, and more.

Statement of Purpose: The primary purpose of these ethics requirements is to identify individuals who have internalized professional values that lead them to act in the best interests of patients. These standards provide proactive guidance, promote ethical behavior, and support ARRT's mission of high standards in patient care by restricting those who exhibit inconsistent behavior.

The Standards consist of two main parts: the Code of Ethics and the Rules of Ethics.

Page 4: The ARRT Code of Ethics - Guiding Principles (Part 1)

The Code of Ethics is an aspirational guide for professional conduct related to patients, employers, colleagues, and other healthcare team members.

Key Principles Include:

  • Principle 1: Act professionally, respond to patient needs, and support colleagues.
  • Principle 2: Advance the profession's objective to serve humanity with dignity.
  • Principle 3: Deliver care unrestricted by personal attributes or illness, without discrimination.
  • Principle 4: Practice based on theory, use equipment appropriately, employ proper procedures.
  • Principle 5: Assess situations, exercise care and judgment, assume responsibility, act in patient's best interest.

Quick Check!

A technologist refuses to provide care to a patient based on their national origin. This directly violates which principle of the Code of Ethics?

Principle 3 states: "The Registered Technologist delivers patient care and service... without discrimination on the basis of race, color, creed, religion, national origin...".

Page 5: The ARRT Code of Ethics - Guiding Principles (Part 2)

Continuing with the aspirational Code of Ethics:

  • Principle 6: Act as an agent by observation and communication for diagnosis/treatment.
  • Principle 7: Use equipment and procedures to minimize radiation exposure.
  • Principle 8: Practice ethically and protect patient's right to quality care.
  • Principle 9: Respect patient confidences and privacy.
  • Principle 10: Continually strive to improve knowledge and skills.
  • Principle 11: Refrain from using illegal drugs or substances impairing judgment.

Quick Check!

An RTT discussing a patient's identifiable medical information with a friend outside of work, without any legal requirement or welfare concern, would violate Principle 9 of the Code of Ethics.

Yes, Principle 9 emphasizes respecting patient confidences and privacy.

Page 6: Introduction to the ARRT Rules of Ethics

The Rules of Ethics are mandatory and enforceable standards of minimally acceptable professional conduct.

Key Points:

  • Enforceable: Violations are subject to sanctions.
  • Duty to Report: RTTs must notify ARRT of ethics violations (own or others') within 30 days or at annual renewal. Applicants must report within 30 days of occurrence.

Quick Check!

Are the ARRT Rules of Ethics merely suggestions for best practice?

No, the Rules of Ethics are mandatory and enforceable.

Page 7: Highlights from the Rules of Ethics - Part 1

Violations subject RTTs/Candidates to sanctions. Categories include:

1. Fraud or Deceptive Practices:
  • In procuring/maintaining certification, employment, or licenses.
  • False/misleading communications about credentials.
  • Fraudulent billing.
2. Subversion:
  • Subverting ARRT exams, CQR, or Education Requirements.
  • Failure to cooperate with ARRT investigations.

Quick Check!

Sharing specific questions remembered from an ARRT certification exam with students preparing for the exam is acceptable professional behavior.

No, this is a form of examination subversion.

Page 8: Highlights from the Rules of Ethics - Part 2

3. Unprofessional Conduct: Incl. failure to conform to standards, sexual misconduct, conduct likely to deceive/harm.
4. Scope of Practice Violations: Performing procedures not competent to do, improper delegation.
5. Fitness to Practice: Inability to practice safely due to illness, alcohol/drugs, mental/physical condition.
6. Improper Management of Patient Records: False entries, failing to protect confidential info.

Quick Check!

An RTT documents that a complex procedure was performed when, in fact, a simpler version was done. Is this an ethical violation?

Yes, this would be improper management of patient records and potentially fraudulent.

Page 9: Highlights from the Rules of Ethics - Part 3 & Duty to Report

7. Violation of State/Federal Law or Regulatory Rule: Incl. narcotics laws, licensure actions, criminal proceedings.
8. Duty to Report:
  • Knowing of a violation by any RTT/Candidate and failing to promptly report to ARRT.
  • Failing to immediately report to one's supervisor an error made in patient care that could be harmful, unethical, or improper.

Ethical Crossroads: Case Study Activity

Instructions

Read each scenario. Consider the ARRT Code of Ethics and Rules of Ethics. Respond to the prompts in the text areas. Focus on identifying ethical principles/rules, potential consequences, and appropriate actions.

Scenario 1: Confidentiality and Social Media

You are a radiation therapy student. On your lunch break, you see a social media post from a fellow student. The post doesn't name patients but describes a challenging case treated that day, including some details about the patient's condition and treatment plan. The post ends with, "#radiationtherapy #savinglives".

Ethical Analysis Prompts:

1. ARRT Standards: Which Code of Ethics principles and Rules of Ethics are potentially violated? Explain.
2. Your Responsibility: According to ARRT Rules (Rule 21), what is your obligation? Consequences if ignored?
3. Course of Action: Describe steps you would take (communication, reporting). Apply EI.

Scenario 2: Resource Allocation and Patient Advocacy

You are an RTT student working with a patient experiencing significant side effects. The patient expresses concern about affording medications and transportation. Your RTT preceptor seems dismissive, saying, "Everyone struggles, they just need to figure it out." You know there are resources available.

Ethical Analysis Prompts:

1. Ethical Principles: Which ARRT Code of Ethics principles are relevant? Does the preceptor's response align?
2. Student's Role & Responsibility: What is your ethical responsibility towards the patient? How does this relate to patient advocacy?
3. Navigating Hierarchy & Action: What are ethical ways to ensure patient's concerns are addressed? Which ARRT Rules could be violated if needs are ignored?

Scenario 3: Observing a Potential Error

During a treatment setup, you (student or RTT) notice your preceptor (or peer RTT) seems rushed and makes a small adjustment to the treatment machine that you believe might be incorrect based on the plan. You have a nagging feeling something isn't right.

Ethical Analysis Prompts:

1. Immediate Ethical Concern: Which ARRT Code of Ethics principles and Rules of Ethics are paramount here?
2. Communication Strategy (Assertiveness & EI): How would you voice your concern respectfully but assertively? How can EI help?
3. Consequences of Inaction vs. Action: Potential consequences if you say nothing and an error occurs? What if you speak up and are mistaken? How does ARRT "Duty to Report Error" apply?

Scenario 4: Impaired Colleague

An RTT, Sarah, notices her long-time colleague and friend, RTT John, has been frequently arriving late, seems disheveled, and she thought she smelled alcohol on his breath one morning. Today, during a complex setup, John seems uncharacteristically clumsy and irritable. Sarah is worried about patient safety and John's well-being.

Ethical Analysis Prompts:

1. ARRT Standards: Which ARRT Code of Ethics principles and Rules of Ethics are relevant to Sarah's observations?
2. Dilemma and Action: What is the ethical dilemma Sarah faces? What immediate actions should Sarah consider for patient safety?
3. Reporting Responsibilities: What are Sarah's responsibilities regarding reporting concerns about John to her supervisor and potentially ARRT?

General Reflection on ARRT Standards of Ethics

1. Why is it important for ARRT to have both an aspirational Code and mandatory Rules? How do they complement each other?
2. The "Duty to Report" can be challenging. What factors might make it difficult, and how can barriers be overcome?
3. How does adherence to ARRT Standards contribute to quality/safety in radiation oncology and profession trustworthiness?
