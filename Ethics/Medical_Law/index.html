Foundations of Professionalism: Medical Ethics & Law for RTTs

Interactive Lesson: Navigating Ethical and Legal Responsibilities

Page 1: Introduction to Ethics, Law, and Morals

In healthcare, our actions are guided by several overlapping systems that define right and wrong conduct.

  • Ethics: The systematic study of rightness and wrongness of human conduct and character as known by natural reason. Professional Ethics are the internal controls of a profession based on human values or moral principles, often codified.
  • Law: Regulations established by a government and applicable to people within a certain political subdivision. Laws represent the minimum standard of conduct.
  • Morals: Generally accepted customs, principles, or habits of right living and conduct in a society and an individual's practice in relation to these.

While an action might be legal, it may not be ethical. A professional ethic demands a standard of conduct that goes beyond mere conformity to law. For Radiation Therapists, this is formalized in the ARRT Standards of Ethics.

Page 2: The ARRT Code of Ethics - An Aspirational Guide

The Code of Ethics forms the first part of the ARRT Standards. It is an aspirational guide for professional conduct. It outlines the fundamental principles RTTs should strive to uphold.

Key Principles (Part 1):

  • Principle 1: Act professionally, respond to patient needs, and support colleagues.
  • Principle 2: Provide services with full respect for the dignity of mankind.
  • Principle 3: Deliver care without discrimination.
  • Principle 4: Practice technology based on knowledge and use equipment appropriately.
  • Principle 5: Exercise care, discretion, and judgment, and assume responsibility for professional decisions.

Quick Check!

An RTT providing a lower standard of care to a patient due to their socioeconomic status would be violating which principle of the Code of Ethics?

B. Principle 3 explicitly states that care must be delivered without discrimination.

Page 3: The ARRT Code of Ethics (Part 2)

Key Principles (Continued):

  • Principle 6: Act as an agent through observation and communication, recognizing interpretation and diagnosis are outside the RTT scope.
  • Principle 7: Demonstrate expertise in minimizing radiation exposure (ALARA).
  • Principle 8: Practice ethically and protect the patient's right to quality care.
  • Principle 9: Respect patient confidentiality and privacy.
  • Principle 10: Continually strive to improve knowledge and skills (Continuing Education).
  • Principle 11: Refrain from substance use that impairs professional judgment.

Quick Check!

An RTT looks at the day's treatment images and tells the patient, "It looks like your tumor is definitely shrinking." This action potentially violates which principle?

C. Principle 6 states that interpretation and diagnosis are outside the scope of practice for an RTT.

Page 4: The ARRT Rules of Ethics - Mandatory Standards

The Rules of Ethics are the second part of the Standards. These are mandatory and enforceable standards of minimally acceptable professional conduct.

Violating these rules can lead to sanctions from the ARRT, ranging from reprimands to revocation of certification.

Key Categories of Rules:

  • Fraud or deceptive practices (e.g., in obtaining certification or billing).
  • Subversion (e.g., cheating on exams, falsifying CE credits).
  • Unprofessional conduct (e.g., creating danger for a patient, sexual misconduct).
  • Scope of practice violations (e.g., performing procedures one is not competent to do).
  • Fitness to practice issues (e.g., impairment due to substance use).
  • Improper management of patient records (e.g., false entries, HIPAA violations).
  • Violations of state/federal laws or regulations.
  • Failing to report violations or errors.

A key takeaway is the Duty to Report: RTTs must report their own and others' violations to the ARRT, and must immediately report patient care errors to their supervisor.

Page 5: Introduction to Medical Law - Torts

A tort is a civil wrong or injury (other than a breach of contract) for which the court provides a remedy in the form of damages. In healthcare, torts often relate to a breach of duty owed to the patient.

Intentional Torts:

  • Assault: An action that causes a person to fear or expect immediate bodily harm. No physical contact is necessary. Example: Threatening a patient to make them hold still.
  • Battery: Any unlawful touching without justification or excuse. Performing a procedure on the wrong patient or without consent constitutes battery, even if no injury occurs.
  • False Imprisonment: Restraining an individual or restricting their freedom against their will. Using immobilization devices on a competent patient who does not consent could be considered false imprisonment.
  • Defamation: Harming a person's reputation by making false public statements.
    • Slander: Spoken defamation.
    • Libel: Written defamation (including social media posts).

Page 6: Medical Law - Negligence

Negligence is the most common basis for medical malpractice claims. It is a failure to use such care as a reasonably prudent person would use under similar circumstances. In healthcare, the standard is a "reasonably prudent healthcare professional."

For a patient to win a negligence claim, they must prove four elements:

  1. Duty: The healthcare provider owed a duty of care to the patient (defined by the Standard of Care).
  2. Breach: The provider breached that duty by failing to act according to the standard of care.
  3. Causation: The provider's breach of duty was the direct cause of the patient's injury.
  4. Damages: The patient sustained an actual injury or damages for which they can be compensated.

Example: An RTT forgets to raise the side rails on a stretcher for a sedated patient. The patient falls and breaks a hip.
Duty (protect patient) -> Breach (didn't raise rails) -> Causation (fall caused fracture) -> Damages (broken hip).

Quick Check!

If a therapist performs a procedure perfectly but the patient has a rare, unpreventable complication, has negligence occurred?

B. A bad outcome does not automatically equal negligence. All four elements, including a breach of duty, must be proven.

Page 7: Medical Law - Consent

A fundamental legal and ethical principle is that patients have the right to make decisions about their own bodies. This is upheld through the process of consent.

Implied Consent:

Inferred from a person's actions. Example: A patient extends their arm for a blood pressure reading. This is typically sufficient for non-invasive, routine procedures.

Informed Consent:

Required for invasive procedures or treatments with significant risks (like radiation therapy). It is a process, not just a form. The provider must disclose:

  • The nature of the procedure.
  • The risks and benefits involved.
  • Reasonable alternatives to the procedure.
  • The likely outcome if no treatment is chosen.

The patient must demonstrate understanding and voluntarily agree. This must be documented, usually on a signed consent form.

Quick Check!

A patient signs a consent form but later states they did not understand the risks explained by the physician. Is the consent valid?

B. A signature alone is not enough. The patient must have adequate information and understanding to make an informed decision.

Ethical Crossroads: Case Study Activity

Instructions

Read each scenario carefully. Respond to the prompts in the text areas, focusing on identifying the relevant ethical principles and ARRT Standards, analyzing the situation, and describing a professional course of action.

Scenario 1: Confidentiality and Social Media

You are a radiation therapy student. On your lunch break, you see a social media post from a fellow student. The post doesn't name any patients but describes a particularly challenging case, including some details about the patient's condition and treatment plan. The post ends with, "#radiationtherapy #savinglives".

Ethical Analysis Prompts:

1. ARRT Standards Violation: Which principles from the ARRT Code of Ethics (e.g., patient privacy, professionalism) and which specific ARRT Rules of Ethics are potentially being violated by your classmate's post? Explain why.
2. Your Ethical Responsibility: According to the ARRT Rules of Ethics (Rule 21), what is your obligation in this situation? What are the potential consequences of ignoring the post?
3. Professional Course of Action: Describe the steps you would take, considering both direct communication with your classmate and your reporting responsibilities. How would you apply principles of effective communication?

Scenario 2: Patient Advocacy and Resource Allocation

You are an RTT student working with a patient who is experiencing significant side effects. The patient expresses concern about affording their medications and transportation to the clinic. Your RTT preceptor seems dismissive, saying, "Everyone struggles, they just need to figure it out."

Ethical Analysis Prompts:

1. Ethical Principles at Play: Which ARRT Code of Ethics principles (e.g., responding to patient needs, acting in patient's best interest) are relevant here? Does the preceptor's response align with these principles?
2. Student's Role & Responsibility: As a student, what is your ethical responsibility towards the patient in this situation? How does this relate to patient advocacy?
3. Navigating Hierarchy & Action: Considering your student status and the preceptor's comment, what are ethical and professional ways to ensure the patient's concerns are addressed? What specific ARRT Rules could be violated if the patient's needs are ignored, potentially leading to treatment interruption?

Scenario 3: Observing a Potential Error

During a treatment setup, you (student or RTT) notice your preceptor seems rushed and makes a small adjustment to the treatment machine that you believe is incorrect based on the plan. You have a nagging feeling something isn't right.

Ethical Analysis Prompts:

1. Immediate Ethical Concern: Which ARRT Code of Ethics principles and which Rules of Ethics (e.g., Rule 7, unprofessional conduct; Rule 22, duty to report error) are paramount here?
2. Communication Strategy (Assertiveness & EI): How would you voice your concern respectfully but assertively, especially if the person is more experienced or seems rushed? How can Emotional Intelligence help you here?
3. Consequences of Inaction vs. Action: What are the potential ethical and practical consequences if you say nothing and an error occurs? What if you speak up and are mistaken? How does the ARRT "Duty to Report Error" (Rule 22) apply even if no actual harm occurred?

Scenario 4: Informed Consent & Patient Autonomy

A patient scheduled for a complex IMRT treatment seems confused. As you are positioning them, they say, "I'm not really sure what this machine does, my doctor just said it was the best option and I signed the form." The patient appears anxious and hesitant to proceed.

Ethical & Legal Analysis Prompts:

1. Ethical & Legal Principles: What is the primary ethical principle at stake here? From a legal perspective, what is the potential issue with the patient's consent?
2. RTT's Role and Responsibility: What is your immediate responsibility as the RTT in this situation? Should you proceed with the treatment? Why or why not?
3. Course of Action: Describe the exact steps you would take to address this situation professionally and ethically, ensuring the patient's rights are respected and care proceeds safely.

General Reflection on Ethics & Law

1. Why is it important for the ARRT to have both an aspirational Code of Ethics and mandatory Rules of Ethics? How do they complement each other?
2. The "Duty to Report" can be challenging. What factors might make it difficult for an RTT to report a colleague's ethical violation or an error, and how can these barriers be overcome?
3. How does adherence to the ARRT Standards of Ethics contribute to the overall quality and safety of patient care and the trustworthiness of the profession?
