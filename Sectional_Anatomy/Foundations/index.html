Page 1: Reading a Cross-Section — The Three Planes

Every image a therapist reviews at the console is a slice through the body in one of three planes. Reading them fluently is the foundation of both contouring and daily setup verification.

PlaneDivides the body intoWhat you see
Axial (transverse)Superior / inferiorA "doughnut" slice viewed from the patient's feet — the patient's right is on the left of your screen (radiologic convention)
CoronalAnterior / posteriorA front-facing slice, like looking at the patient standing before you
SagittalLeft / rightA side-profile slice; the midsagittal plane splits the body into equal halves

The radiologic convention for axial images (patient's right on the viewer's left) is why a right-lung tumor appears on the left of your screen. Getting this backwards is one of the most consequential orientation errors in the department.

Page 2: Couch Movements and Image Planes

Each plane corresponds to a physical couch or scan axis, which is exactly how a therapist translates an image shift into a table move:

  • Axial stack → longitudinal (in/out). Scrolling through axial slices moves you superior–inferior, the same axis as the couch's long travel.
  • Vertical (up/down) and lateral (left/right) couch moves reposition the patient within the coronal and sagittal planes.

When a verification image shows the target 5 mm too inferior, the correction is a longitudinal couch shift — the plane you saw the error in tells you which axis to move.

Quick Check!

On a standard axial CT slice viewed in radiologic convention, where does a structure in the patient's right lung appear?

A. Axial images are conventionally displayed as if looking up from the patient's feet, so the patient's right is on the viewer's left. Reversing this flips laterality — a critical setup error.

Page 3: The Hounsfield Scale

CT numbers are reported in Hounsfield Units (HU), a scale anchored so that water = 0 HU and air = −1000 HU. Every tissue sits at a characteristic value, which is what lets you identify a structure by its brightness before you even recognize its shape.

TissueApproximate HU
Air (lung)−1000 (−500 in aerated lung)
Fat−100 to −50
Water0
Soft tissue / muscle+20 to +80
Cortical bone+400 to +1000+

In treatment planning, HU values also feed the dose calculation directly — the planning system converts CT number to electron density, which is why an accurate, artifact-free simulation scan matters for more than just contouring.

Quick Check!

On the Hounsfield scale, what value is assigned to water?

B. The scale is defined with water at 0 HU and air at −1000 HU. Bone runs strongly positive; fat is mildly negative.

Page 4: Window Width and Window Level

A CT holds far more grayscale range than a monitor or the eye can display at once, so the image is windowed to show the tissues of interest:

  • Window Level (WL) — the center HU value of the displayed range (sets overall brightness).
  • Window Width (WW) — the range of HU spread across black-to-white (sets contrast). A narrow width gives high contrast over a small HU band; a wide width shows more tissues but flatter contrast.
Window presetReveals
Lung window (wide WW, low WL)Lung parenchyma, airways, pneumothorax
Soft-tissue / mediastinal windowOrgans, vessels, nodal disease
Bone window (very wide WW)Cortex, trabeculae, lytic/blastic lesions
Brain window (narrow WW)Gray–white differentiation, edema, hemorrhage

This is why the same thoracic slice must be read on both lung and mediastinal windows — each reveals a tissue interface the other hides.

Page 5: From Diagnostic Image to Daily Verification

Sectional anatomy underpins two core radiation-therapy tasks:

  1. Contouring & localization at simulation — the target and organs at risk are drawn slice by slice on the planning CT.
  2. Daily anatomic verification at the machine — before beam-on, the therapist compares in-room imaging against the reference to confirm the anatomy sits where the plan expects.

Image-guided verification generally means matching on one of two things: a bony match (aligning skeletal landmarks on kV/kV or CBCT to the DRR) or a soft-tissue match (aligning the organ/target itself on CBCT, e.g., prostate or bladder). Knowing which structures are reliable surrogates in each region is the whole point of the lessons that follow.

Quick Check!

B. Matching to the organ/target directly is a soft-tissue match — important where the target moves independently of bone, as the prostate does relative to the pelvis.
