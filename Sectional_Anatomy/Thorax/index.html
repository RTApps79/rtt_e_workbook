Page 1: The Thorax — Two Challenges, and How We Image It

Thoracic planning is defined by respiratory motion and tissue heterogeneity (the beam crosses low-density lung, so the plan must apply density correction). The thorax is read on two windows: a lung window for parenchyma and airways, and a mediastinal window for vessels, nodes, and the heart.

Boundaries and levels orient you: the thoracic inlet superiorly, the sternal angle / carina at ~T4–T5, and the diaphragm inferiorly (moving with respiration).

Quick Check!

The trachea bifurcates into the main bronchi at the carina, at approximately which level?

B. The carina sits at about T4–T5, near the sternal angle — a stable midline landmark for localization and setup checks.

Quick Check!

Compared with an upper-lobe tumor, a lower-lobe tumor generally requires:

A. Lower-lobe tumors sit near the diaphragm and move more with respiration, so their ITV (from 4D-CT) is typically larger than an upper-lobe tumor's.

Page 4: Mediastinal Structures and Nodes

On the mediastinal window you must confidently name the great vessels — ascending and descending aorta, aortic arch, superior vena cava, the azygos vein arching over the right main bronchus — plus the esophagus posteriorly and the spinal cord in the canal.

Thoracic nodal stations (e.g., paratracheal, subcarinal, hilar) guide elective volumes and are described relative to these mediastinal landmarks. Explore them in the workbook's 3D lymphatics model.

Quick Check!

Which vessel arches over the right main bronchus to drain into the superior vena cava?

B. The azygos vein arches over the right main bronchus to join the SVC — a classic mediastinal landmark.

Page 5: Anatomic Verification in a Moving Chest

Thoracic verification is dominated by respiratory motion, so bony matching alone is insufficient for a target that slides with breathing.

  • Setup verified with CBCT: match first to the vertebrae/carina, then assess the tumor against the ITV.
  • Motion managed with 4D-CT-derived ITVs, breath-hold, or gating.
  • A pure bony match can leave a mobile tumor outside the high-dose region even when the spine lines up perfectly.

Quick Check!

Why can a perfect vertebral bony match still leave a lower-lobe lung tumor mispositioned?

A. Lung tumors move with breathing relative to the skeleton, so the target must be verified against the ITV, not inferred from bone alone.

Page 6: Wrap-Up

You've explored normal lung and airway anatomy, a lung carcinoma, and thoracic nodal anatomy in 3D, connected them to dual-window mediastinal reading and the carina landmark, and seen why respiratory motion makes thoracic verification a target-based task.
