Foundations of Patient Care in Radiation Therapy

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: The Compassionate & Competent Therapist

Purpose: Why This Matters

This lesson introduces fundamental patient care skills crucial for Radiation Therapists. Mastering these skills ensures patient safety, comfort, and contributes to a positive treatment experience, while upholding professional standards.

Task: What You Will Do

You will learn about correct patient identification, effective communication, monitoring vital signs, principles of safe patient transfer, oxygen administration basics, infection control, and an overview of BLS/AED.

Criteria for Success

By the end of this lesson, you should be able to:

  • Describe the steps for accurate patient identification.
  • List key vital signs and their importance.
  • Explain the principles of safe patient transfer and body mechanics.
  • Outline basic steps for O2 administration and universal precautions.
  • Recognize the components of BLS and the role of an AED.

As a Radiation Therapist, you are often the healthcare professional a patient sees most frequently during their treatment course. Your care and attention significantly impact their experience.

Page 2: Patient Identification, Instruction & Communication

Ensuring you are treating the correct patient and that they understand procedures is paramount.

Accurate Patient Identification [cite: 1, 6, 9]

  • Always use at least two patient identifiers before any procedure or treatment. Common identifiers include:
    • Patient's full name
    • Date of birth
    • Medical record number
    • Photo ID (in some cases)
  • Actively involve the patient by asking them to state their name and date of birth. Do not just ask "Are you Mr. Smith?"

Clear Patient Instruction & Communication [cite: 1, 6]

  • Introduce yourself by name and title clearly[cite: 9].
  • Explain procedures in simple, understandable terms, avoiding overly technical jargon.
  • Verify the patient understands instructions and allow them to ask questions.
  • Practice active listening and show empathy.

Quick Check: When identifying a patient, what is the minimum number of identifiers you should use?

Correct! At least two patient identifiers are required for safe practice[cite: 1, 9].

Page 3: Measuring & Understanding Vital Signs

Vital signs provide important information about a patient's physiological status. RTTs should be competent in obtaining and understanding them[cite: 1].

Key Vital Signs:

  • Weight: Perform measurement and document accurately[cite: 1]. Weight is critical for some dose calculations and monitoring nutritional status.
  • Temperature: Obtain reading from a thermometer (glass or electronic) and document[cite: 3].
  • Pulse Rate: Monitor the heart rate (beats per minute) by palpating an artery (e.g., radial) and document[cite: 4]. Note its rhythm and strength.
  • Respiration Rate: Monitor the number of breaths per minute and document[cite: 4]. Note depth and character of breathing.
  • Blood Pressure: Monitor using a sphygmomanometer and stethoscope, and document systolic and diastolic pressures[cite: 3].

Always ensure equipment is obtained [cite: 2] and properly cleaned after use[cite: 4]. Be aware of normal ranges for adults and report any significant deviations.

Quick Check: Which instrument is used with a stethoscope to measure blood pressure?

Correct! A sphygmomanometer (blood pressure cuff) and a stethoscope are used together[cite: 2].

Page 4: Oxygen Administration Basics

Some patients in radiation therapy may require oxygen (O2) administration.

Procedure Overview[cite: 6]:

  • Perform proper patient identification, instruction, and communication[cite: 6].
  • Obtain necessary equipment, such as a nasal cannula or mask, and an oxygen tank or wall outlet with a flowmeter[cite: 7].
  • Ensure the oxygen is adjusted to the correct setting (flow rate in liters per minute) as prescribed or per protocol[cite: 7].
  • Monitor the patient's respiration rate and document any changes or distress[cite: 8].
  • Ensure equipment is cleaned after use and universal precautions are observed[cite: 8].

Safety with Oxygen:

  • Oxygen supports combustion; ensure no smoking or open flames are nearby.
  • Handle oxygen cylinders securely to prevent falls or damage.
  • Ensure all connections are tight to prevent leaks.

Page 5: Safe Patient Transfers & Body Mechanics

Safely transferring patients is a key skill to prevent injury to both the patient and the therapist[cite: 9].

Key Steps for Patient Transfer[cite: 9]:

  • Greet and Assist Patient: Approach the patient calmly and professionally.
  • Confirm Identity: Always use two patient identifiers.
  • Introduce Yourself: State your name and role.
  • Explain the Transfer: Clearly communicate what you are going to do.
  • Assess Patient's Ability: Determine how much assistance the patient will need.
  • Ensure Safe Environment: Lock wheels on wheelchairs/stretchers, clear pathways.
  • Use Proper Body Mechanics: (See below).
  • Support Patient: Assist and/or support the patient as they move to/from a wheelchair, stretcher, bed, or treatment couch.

Proper Body Mechanics for Therapists:

  • Maintain a wide base of support (feet shoulder-width apart).
  • Keep your back straight; bend at your knees and hips, not your waist.
  • Use your leg muscles to lift, not your back.
  • Keep the patient (or object) close to your body.
  • Pivot your feet; avoid twisting your back.
  • Ask for help if the patient is heavy or requires significant assistance.

Page 6: Infection Control: Universal Precautions & Cleanliness

Universal Precautions (now often referred to as Standard Precautions) are essential to prevent the transmission of infections in healthcare settings[cite: 5]. They assume that all blood and body fluids are potentially infectious.

Key Practices[cite: 5, 8]:

  • Hand Hygiene: This is the single most important practice. Wash hands thoroughly with soap and water or use an alcohol-based hand sanitizer before and after every patient contact, after contact with potentially contaminated surfaces, and after removing gloves.
  • Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): Use gloves, gowns, masks, and eye protection as appropriate, depending on the anticipated exposure.
  • Safe Handling of Sharps: Dispose of needles and other sharp objects in designated puncture-resistant containers immediately after use.
  • Cleaning and Disinfection: Regularly clean and disinfect patient care equipment and environmental surfaces (e.g., treatment couch, immobilization devices, control panels) between patients[cite: 4, 8].
  • Respiratory Hygiene/Cough Etiquette: Educate patients and staff to cover coughs and sneezes.

Adhering to these precautions protects both patients and healthcare workers.

Page 7: Emergency Preparedness: BLS & AED Overview

Radiation Therapists, like all healthcare providers, must be prepared to respond to medical emergencies.

Basic Life Support (BLS)[cite: 5]:

  • Certification: Healthcare providers are typically required to maintain current BLS certification (e.g., from the American Heart Association).
  • Core Sequence (C-A-B):
    1. Compressions: Provide high-quality chest compressions.
    2. Airway: Open the airway.
    3. Breathing: Provide rescue breaths.
  • Early recognition of cardiac arrest and prompt action are key.

Automated External Defibrillator (AED)[cite: 6]:

  • An AED is a portable device that can analyze the heart's rhythm and deliver an electrical shock (defibrillation) if needed to treat certain life-threatening arrhythmias.
  • RTTs should know the location of AEDs in their department and be familiar with their correct use as part of BLS protocols.

This is an overview. Comprehensive, hands-on BLS/CPR training and certification are mandatory.

Quick Check: What does AED stand for?

Correct! AED stands for Automated External Defibrillator[cite: 6].

Page 8: Summary & Next Steps

This lesson covered essential patient care skills for Radiation Therapists:

  • Accurate Patient Identification and clear Communication.
  • Proficiency in measuring and understanding Vital Signs.
  • Basic principles of Oxygen Administration.
  • Techniques for Safe Patient Transfer using proper body mechanics.
  • Adherence to Universal Precautions for infection control.
  • Awareness of BLS/AED protocols for emergencies.

Mastering these foundational skills is critical for providing safe, effective, and compassionate care. These objectives will be revisited and built upon throughout your clinical education.
