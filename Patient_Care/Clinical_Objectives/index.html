Page 1: The Compassionate & Competent Therapist

Purpose: Why This Matters

This lesson introduces fundamental patient care skills crucial for Radiation Therapists, focusing on safety, communication, and basic clinical procedures to ensure a positive treatment experience.

Task: What You Will Do

Learn about patient identification, communication, vital signs, safe patient handling, oxygen administration, infection control, and BLS/AED overview.

Criteria for Success

Understand steps for patient ID, vital signs importance, safe transfer principles, O2 basics, universal precautions, and BLS/AED components.

Your care significantly impacts the patient's experience.

Page 2: Patient Identification, Instruction & Communication

Accurate patient identification and clear communication are paramount.

Accurate Patient Identification:

  • Always use at least two patient identifiers (e.g., full name, DOB, MRN).
  • Actively involve the patient: ask them to state their name and DOB.

Clear Communication:

  • Introduce yourself clearly (name and title).
  • Explain procedures in simple terms.
  • Verify understanding and encourage questions. Practice active listening.

Quick Check: Minimum patient identifiers to use?

Correct! Two identifiers are standard.

Page 3: Measuring & Understanding Vital Signs

Vital signs indicate essential body functions.

Key Vital Signs:

  • Weight: Document accurately; crucial for some calculations.
  • Temperature: Oral normal: 97.0-99.0°F (36.1-37.2°C). Fever can indicate infection.
  • Pulse (Heart Rate): Normal adult: 60-100 bpm. Note rate, rhythm, strength.
  • Respiration Rate: Normal adult: 12-20 breaths/min. Note rate, depth, effort.
  • Blood Pressure: Normal adult: <120/80 mmHg. Use correct cuff size.

Report significant deviations. Clean equipment properly.

Quick Check: Which instrument is used with a stethoscope for BP?

Correct! Sphygmomanometer (BP cuff) and stethoscope.

Page 4: Oxygen Administration Basics

RTTs should understand basic oxygen (O2) administration.

Procedure Overview:

  • Patient ID, instruction, communication.
  • Equipment: Nasal cannula/mask, O2 source with flowmeter.
  • Adjust to prescribed flow rate.
  • Monitor patient response (respirations, comfort).
  • Clean equipment; observe universal precautions.

Oxygen Safety:

  • O2 supports combustion: No smoking/open flames. Secure cylinders. Check connections.

Page 5: Safe Patient Transfers & Body Mechanics

Safe transfers protect both patient and therapist.

Key Transfer Steps:

  • Greet, ID, introduce self, explain transfer, assess patient's ability.
  • Ensure safe environment (lock wheels, clear path).
  • Use proper body mechanics.
  • Support patient during move (wheelchair, stretcher, couch).

Proper Body Mechanics:

  • Wide base, back straight, bend knees/hips, use legs, keep load close, pivot feet (no twisting), ask for help.

Page 6: Infection Control: Universal Precautions

Universal (Standard) Precautions assume all body fluids are potentially infectious.

Key Practices:

  • Hand Hygiene: Most important! Wash or sanitize before/after patient contact, etc.
  • PPE: Use gloves, gowns, masks, eye protection as needed.
  • Safe Sharps Handling: Dispose in puncture-resistant containers.
  • Cleaning/Disinfection: Equipment and surfaces between patients.
  • Respiratory Hygiene/Cough Etiquette.

Page 7: Emergency Preparedness: BLS & AED

RTTs must be prepared for medical emergencies.

Basic Life Support (BLS):

  • Certification is typically required.
  • Core Sequence: C-A-B (Compressions, Airway, Breathing).
  • Early recognition and action are key.

Automated External Defibrillator (AED):

  • Portable device that analyzes heart rhythm and can deliver a shock.
  • Know AED locations and use as per BLS protocols.

This is an overview; comprehensive hands-on training is mandatory.

Quick Check: What does AED stand for?

Correct! AED stands for Automated External Defibrillator.
