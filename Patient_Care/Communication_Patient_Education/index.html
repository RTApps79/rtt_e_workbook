Communication & Patient Education

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: Communication Is the Job, Not a Soft Skill

Securing a patient's cooperation, day after day, through simulation and every treatment fraction, is one of the most important and most challenging parts of being a radiation therapist. Communication and patient safety go hand in hand — a patient who understands and trusts you is a patient who holds still, follows instructions, and tells you when something feels wrong.

All communication with patients must be patient-focused, accurate, timely, and within your scope of practice.

Page 2: Human Diversity and Cultural Competency

Every patient brings their own age, race, religion, ethnicity, gender identity, ability, and life circumstances into the treatment room. None of that is a barrier to good care — it's context you need to work with.

Four core values underpin respectful, rights-based care: autonomy, dignity, equality, and solidarity. Consent for treatment cannot happen without sensitivity to a patient's cultural and language needs — use professional interpreters, not family members, whenever language is a barrier.

Becoming culturally competent is a process: recognize diversity as a value, build awareness of how cross-cultural interactions play out, and practice honest self-assessment of your own assumptions.

Page 3: Verbal and Nonverbal Communication

Verbal communication includes spoken and written words — and also slang, jargon, and humor, all of which can land very differently than intended with an anxious patient.

Nonverbal communication (sometimes called paralanguage) often says more than your words: body language, touch, professional appearance, physical presence, eye contact, and personal hygiene all shape whether a patient feels safe with you.

Quick Check!

Which of the following is an example of nonverbal communication?

B. Eye contact and posture are nonverbal (paralanguage) cues, unlike spoken explanations or written orders.

Page 4: Age, Impairments, and Communication Challenges

Age is not a barrier to effective communication — but it does change your approach. With older patients: maintain eye contact, speak clearly and a bit more slowly, speak to them rather than around them, and treat them with patience and respect.

Language barriers, cultural and social factors, and physical, sensory, or cognitive impairments all call for the same underlying skill: slow down, check understanding frequently, and adjust your explanation rather than repeating the same words louder.

Page 5: Terminal Patients

Radiation oncology staff work with terminal patients often. Death is part of the natural cycle of life, and healthcare's attitude toward it has shifted toward being more open and more respectful of a dying patient's own wishes.

Patients, families, and loved ones need to move through grief in their own individualized timeframe. Patient autonomy — respecting the choices a patient makes about their own care and how they want to spend their remaining time — is central here.

Page 6: Communicating with Family and Friends

Before you say anything to a patient's family or friends about their condition or care, you need the patient's permission — otherwise you risk a HIPAA violation, regardless of good intentions.

When you do have permission: introduce yourself professionally, briefly explain the procedure, and explain why they need to step out of the room during beam-on. Empathy toward family members is just as powerful as empathy toward the patient.

Quick Check!

A patient's adult child asks you how the treatment is going. What should you do first?

B. Being family doesn't automatically grant access to health information — you need the patient's permission first, every time.

Page 7: The AIDET Framework

AIDET is a widely used structure for consistent, compassionate patient communication:

  • Acknowledge — greet the patient by name, make eye contact
  • Introduce — yourself, your role, and your experience
  • Duration — how long the visit or step will take
  • Explanation — what you're about to do and why
  • Thank you — for their time, patience, or trust

Running through AIDET at each new step of a visit turns a routine interaction into one that feels respectful and predictable — which matters enormously to an anxious patient.

Page 8: Patient Education

Part of your role is explaining, in plain language: the purpose of a treatment or procedure, how long it will take, and what dose or exposure is involved. You'll also field questions comparing radiation therapy to other imaging modalities, and reinforce pre- and post-treatment instructions (skin care, diet, medications).

Clear education supports treatment compliance — a patient who understands why they need to hold still, keep their positioning marks, or follow a skin-care routine is far more likely to actually do it.

Page 9: Support Services

You're one part of a larger care team. Know when and how to loop in:

  • Hospice — for patients transitioning to comfort-focused, palliative care
  • Dietitians — for nutrition and side-effect management
  • Clergy / chaplaincy — for spiritual support
  • Social workers — for financial, transportation, or family support needs

Recognizing when a patient needs support beyond what you can offer — and knowing who to call — is itself a communication skill.

Page 10: Wrap-Up

You've covered cultural competency, verbal and nonverbal communication, age and impairment-related adjustments, terminal patient care, family communication and HIPAA, the AIDET framework, patient education, and the broader support team.

None of this replaces clinical judgment in the moment — but it gives you a consistent foundation to build on with every patient you meet.
