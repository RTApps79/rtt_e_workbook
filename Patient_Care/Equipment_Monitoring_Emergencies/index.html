Page 1: Why This Matters for RTTs

As a radiation therapist, you're not just operating equipment — you're often the only clinical staff member with a patient during simulation and daily treatment. Recognizing abnormal vital signs, managing common tubes and lines safely, and responding to a medical emergency are core parts of the job, not someone else's responsibility.

This lesson covers vital signs, oxygen therapy, chest tubes and lines, and the basics of CPR — then hands you off to the Vital Signs Simulator for hands-on practice.

Page 2: The Vital Signs

Vital signs are objective, non-invasive measurements that can be taken quickly: temperature, pulse, respiration rate, and blood pressure are the core four, with oxygen saturation now considered essential in most clinical settings.

Vital signs assessment should be fast and objective — it's a snapshot, not a diagnosis. A single abnormal reading is a cue to look closer and reassess, not necessarily an emergency by itself.

Page 3: Temperature and Respiration

Body temperature abnormalities: hypothermia is a temperature below 97.7°F; hyperthermia (fever) is an oral temperature above 99.5°F.

Respiration is the rhythmic exchange of gases through the lungs, measured as breaths per minute. Key abnormalities:

  • Tachypnea — rapid breathing, greater than 20 breaths/min
  • Bradypnea — abnormally slow respiratory rate
  • Dyspnea — difficult or labored breathing
  • Orthopnea — difficulty breathing unless sitting or standing upright
  • Apnea — absence of spontaneous breathing

Accurate assessment means tracking rate, depth, and pattern — not just a number.

Page 4: Pulse and Blood Pressure

Pulse reflects the heart's contractions transmitted through the arterial walls. Common measurement sites: radial (wrist), brachial (inner elbow — common in infants), and carotid (neck — the standard site during CPR).

Normal adult pulse: 60–100 bpm. Tachycardia is above 100 bpm; bradycardia is an abnormally low rate. Children run faster: 70–120 bpm (ages 1–10), 100–160 bpm (newborns).

Blood pressure is measured in mmHg over the brachial artery. Normal is roughly 120/80: systolic (contraction) under 120, diastolic (relaxation) under 80. Hypotension is below normal; hypertension is above — often called the "silent killer" since patients are frequently asymptomatic.

Quick Check!

During adult CPR, pulse is typically checked at which site?

B. The carotid artery is the standard adult CPR pulse-check site, assessed for no more than 10 seconds.

Page 5: Oxygen Therapy Basics

Oxygen is essential for cellular metabolism, makes up 21% of atmospheric gas, and is legally treated as a drug — it must be ordered by a physician, not administered at a therapist's discretion.

Oxygen is not flammable itself, but it strongly supports combustion — a real fire-safety consideration around electrical equipment.

Oxygen flow is measured in liters per minute (LPM). The universal color for oxygen equipment is green — the flowmeter is green and labeled "OXYGEN."

Page 6: Oxygen Delivery Devices

Devices fall into two broad categories:

  • Low-flow devices — nasal cannula, simple masks
  • High-flow devices — aerosol masks, non-rebreathing masks, tents/oxyhoods, ventilators

If transporting a patient on portable oxygen, check the supply before transport to make sure there's enough for the trip plus the full procedure — and secure the tank so it can't roll or fall.

Quick Check!

A patient arrives on oxygen via nasal cannula for a simulation scan. What's the correct action regarding the oxygen device?

B. An oxygen device should never be completely removed for the sake of a procedure without appropriate medical consent or supervision.

Page 7: Chest Tubes, Central Lines, and Airway Tubes

You'll encounter patients with a range of tubes and lines, especially in inpatient or post-surgical settings:

  • Endotracheal (ET) tubes — placed in the trachea for mechanical ventilation or oxygen delivery
  • Chest tubes (thoracostomy) — drain the pleural space or mediastinum. Never clamp a chest drain unless specifically ordered, and never lift the drainage unit above chest level (this can cause fluid/air to flow back toward the patient).
  • Central venous pressure (CVP) lines — inserted into a major vein for medication administration, fluid management, blood draws/transfusions, and monitoring cardiac pressures
  • Pulmonary arterial lines — have a balloon tip that floats and wedges into the pulmonary artery for pressure monitoring

The common thread: these lines exist for a medical reason you may not know the details of. When in doubt, don't disconnect, clamp, or reposition — ask.

Page 8: Other Equipment You'll Assist With

Beyond oxygen and IV-type lines, you'll routinely assist patients with:

  • Nasogastric (NG) tubes — for feeding or decompression; be aware of them during transfers and positioning
  • Urinary catheters, urinals, and bedpans — maintaining dignity and proper drainage bag positioning (below bladder level) matters during setup
  • Infusion pumps — keep the pump upright and be mindful of tubing length/tension when moving or positioning a patient

The general principle across all of these: know what's attached to your patient before you move them, and never disconnect something to make positioning "easier."

Page 9: Medical Emergencies and CPR Basics

If a patient goes into cardiac arrest, the sequence is: verify (check responsiveness and breathing, assess carotid pulse for no more than 10 seconds) → call for help/activate emergency response and get the AED → begin CPR immediately.

Adult CPR: 100–120 compressions/minute, 2 inches (5 cm) depth, ratio of 30 compressions to 2 breaths (one-person). In two-person CPR, one person compresses while the other ventilates, same 30:2 ratio.

Child CPR (ages 1–12): compression depth about 1/3 the chest diameter (~2 in). One-person ratio 30:2; two-person ratio 15:2.

Infant CPR (under 1 year): two or three fingers at the center of the chest just below the nipple line, depth about 1.25 inches. One-person ratio 30:2; two-person ratio 15:2.

Quick Check!

In two-person adult CPR, what compression-to-breath ratio is used?

B. Two-person adult CPR still uses a 30:2 ratio — it's the child/infant two-person ratio that changes to 15:2.

Other emergencies you may encounter — allergic reactions, seizures, diabetic reactions, and physical trauma — all share the same first steps: stay with the patient, call for help, and don't attempt treatment beyond your scope.
