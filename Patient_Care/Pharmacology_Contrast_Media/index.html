Pharmacology & Contrast Media

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: Why This Matters for RTTs

Radiation therapists aren't pharmacists, but contrast media and basic pharmacology come up regularly — contrast-enhanced CT simulation scans, patients on medications that affect treatment scheduling, and recognizing when a patient is having an adverse reaction. You need working knowledge, not prescribing authority.

Page 2: Types of Contrast Media

Contrast media are classified by how they interact with X-rays:

  • Negative contrast agents — air or gas, which appear dark on imaging (used less often in RT simulation)
  • Positive contrast agents — appear bright/white on imaging:
    • Barium sulfate — used for GI tract studies, not injected
    • Water-soluble iodinated contrast — injected intravenously for CT simulation to help distinguish vessels, organs, and some tumors from surrounding tissue

Page 3: Ionic vs. Non-Ionic Iodinated Contrast

Water-soluble iodinated contrast comes in two chemical classes:

Ionic contrast dissociates into charged particles in solution — higher osmolality, generally higher reaction risk, and increasingly less common in modern practice.

Non-ionic contrast does not dissociate — lower osmolality, better tolerated, and the standard choice in most facilities today.

Page 4: Appropriateness of Contrast to the Procedure

Before contrast is given, several patient factors need to be checked:

  • Patient condition — known allergies, prior reactions to contrast
  • Age and weight — dosing considerations
  • Laboratory values — particularly BUN, creatinine, and eGFR, since iodinated contrast is cleared by the kidneys and can cause contrast-induced nephropathy in patients with impaired renal function

Quick Check!

Which lab values are most relevant before administering iodinated contrast?

B. BUN, creatinine, and eGFR reflect kidney function, which determines how safely a patient can clear iodinated contrast.

Page 5: Contrast Reactions

Local effects — extravasation/infiltration (contrast leaking into surrounding tissue instead of the vein) and phlebitis (vein inflammation).

Systemic effects, graded by severity:

  • Mild — nausea, mild hives, warmth/flushing
  • Moderate — more pronounced hives, wheezing, vomiting
  • Severe — anaphylaxis, cardiac or respiratory compromise — a true emergency

Know your department's emergency medication cart location and your role if a reaction occurs — typically staying with the patient, calling for help, and documenting exactly what happened and when.

Page 6: Radiopharmaceuticals

Radiopharmaceuticals combine a radioactive isotope with a pharmaceutical carrier, used mainly in diagnostic imaging like PET (Positron Emission Tomography) — FDG-PET is commonly fused with CT simulation datasets for treatment planning.

Because these involve unsealed radioactive sources, special precautions apply: proper handling by qualified personnel, patient-specific radiation safety instructions, and awareness of decay time before certain procedures.

Page 7: Your Role and Documentation

If any adverse reaction occurs — to contrast, a medication, or anything else — your responsibilities are the same regardless of severity: recognize it early, get appropriate help immediately, and document exactly what was observed, when, and what response was taken.

Quick Check!

A patient develops hives and mild wheezing shortly after contrast injection. What's your first action?

B. Any contrast reaction, even one that looks moderate, needs an immediate response — stay with the patient and get help right away.

Page 8: Wrap-Up

You've covered contrast media types, ionic vs. non-ionic iodinated contrast, appropriateness screening (including renal function labs), reaction severity levels, radiopharmaceuticals, and your role if something goes wrong.

You're not expected to manage a reaction independently — you're expected to recognize one quickly and get the right help fast.
