Patient Evaluation & Assessment

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: Evaluation and Assessment in RT

Before and throughout treatment, you're continually gathering information about how a patient is doing — from the initial history taken at consultation through the side effects you observe on treatment day 20. This isn't diagnosing; it's noticing and reporting accurately.

Page 2: Subjective vs. Objective Data

Good history-taking combines two kinds of information:

Subjective data comes from the patient's own report — their emotions, pain level, or how they describe a symptom. Objective data is measurable and observable — vital signs, visible findings, test results.

Good questioning skills draw out accurate subjective data without leading the patient toward an answer.

Page 3: Cancer Risk Factors and Screening

Understanding common risk factors (tobacco use, family history, prior radiation exposure, certain infections, occupational exposures) helps you understand why a patient's treatment plan looks the way it does, and supports informed conversations about screening and prevention with patients and their families.

Page 4: Head and Neck Cancer Side Effects

Head and neck radiation carries a distinct side-effect profile:

  • Dysphagia — difficulty swallowing
  • Mucositis/stomatitis — inflamed, radiosensitive mucous membranes that can ulcerate
  • Xerostomia — dry mouth, raising the risk of dental caries and oral infection
  • Taste changes
  • Periodontal disease — healing after radiation is poor, so extractions afterward risk osteoradionecrosis

Management includes saliva substitutes for xerostomia, "magic mouthwash" for mucositis pain, and early dietitian involvement given how much these effects impact eating.

Page 5: Pelvic, Breast, and Lung Cancer Side Effects

Pelvic treatments commonly cause acute GI effects — diarrhea, abdominal cramping, rectal discomfort — alongside skin erythema.

Breast treatments typically involve acute skin changes, fatigue, and a risk of lymphedema.

Lung treatments can cause esophagitis (with dysphagia), anorexia, weight loss, and skin changes in the treatment field.

In every case, the pattern is the same: side effects cluster around the anatomy in the beam path, and management is proactive, not just reactive.

Page 6: Skin Reaction Staging

Radiation skin reactions progress in a predictable sequence as dose accumulates:

Faint erythema (~1600 cGy) — slight redness. Avoid alcohol-based creams, heat/cold/wind/soaps/shaving on the area; wear loose cotton clothing.

Erythema (~2000–3000 cGy) — more pronounced redness, possible itching. Same care, plus hydrocortisone cream if itchy.

Dry desquamation (~3000–4000 cGy) — dry, flaking, peeling skin. May need a prescription cream.

Moist desquamation (~4000–6000 cGy) — seeping, wet skin, sloughing — most common late in treatment, and the highest infection risk of the four stages.

Page 7: Dietary Counseling by Treatment Site

Nutrition needs shift by treatment site and by how side effects progress:

  • Head & neck: maintain weight and hydration; shift toward liquid/pureed diet and protein supplement drinks as swallowing worsens; avoid spicy, coarse, or extreme-temperature foods
  • Pelvic: high protein, low residue, high water intake; simple carbohydrates tend to be better tolerated
  • Breast: generally a typical diet with high water/protein and produce; avoid alcohol
  • Lung: softer foods, high water and protein, liquid diet if swallowing becomes difficult

Common problems (poor appetite, taste changes, swallowing difficulty) all trace back to identifiable causes tied to the treatment site — which is why dietary management is site-specific, not one-size-fits-all.

Page 8: Blood Studies Relevant to RT

Common labs you'll see referenced in a patient's chart:

  • CBC (Complete Blood Count) — monitors for treatment-related myelosuppression, especially relevant with concurrent chemotherapy or large-field/marrow-involving treatments
  • BUN/Creatinine/eGFR — kidney function, relevant to contrast safety (see Pharmacology & Contrast Media)
  • PSA — used in prostate cancer monitoring

Factors like hydration, medications, and recent illness can all affect these values — a single abnormal result is a prompt to look closer, not necessarily a crisis.

Page 9: Wrap-Up

You've covered subjective vs. objective data, cancer risk factors, site-specific side effects, the four stages of radiation skin reaction, dietary counseling by site, and relevant blood studies.

Good assessment is about noticing changes early and reporting them accurately — you're the team member seeing the patient most often, which makes your observations genuinely clinically valuable.
