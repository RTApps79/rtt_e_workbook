Medical Record Documentation

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: The Treatment Record Is a Legal Document

The treatment record must be detailed enough that another qualified radiation therapy professional, unfamiliar with the patient, could reconstruct their entire treatment history from it alone — something that genuinely happens when patients move or need retreatment years later, sometimes in a different state or country.

Most clinics today keep this as an Electronic Medical Record (EMR) rather than a paper chart.

Page 2: What's in the Treatment Record

A complete treatment record includes the written directive — the physician's prescription for dose delivery, which must be clear, unambiguous, and signed by an authorized user — plus:

  • Treatment planning specifics: field size, beam angles, DRRs (digitally reconstructed radiographs)
  • Patient setup from CT simulation, including positioning, and a patient face photo
  • Daily and total delivered dose, area treated, calculated SSDs

Page 3: Daily Recorded Documentation

Every single treatment day, the record captures: date, time, therapist initials, MU setting/timer, daily dose, accumulated dose, elapsed days since treatment began, fraction number, and OAR doses.

The treatment record is reviewed daily by the treating therapist. Weekly chart checks are done by a supervisor, physician, and physicist. A full chart check happens any time a change is made to the patient's treatment.

Quick Check!

Who reviews the treatment record daily?

B. The treating therapist reviews the record every day; the physician, physicist, and supervisor add a weekly review on top of that.

Page 4: Record and Verify Systems

A Record and Verify (R&V) system compares the treatment set up at the console against the intended, planned treatment before it delivers — catching things like the wrong wedge, wrong gantry angle, or an incorrect MU.

R&V is a powerful safety net, but it has a real limit: it cannot prevent the wrong patient from being treated. That verification is still on you.

Page 5: Patient Identification

Every treatment requires a minimum of two identifiers before you proceed — typically name, date of birth, and/or wristband. Some clinics allow a patient photo as a supplementary identifier, but not as one of the two required identifiers on its own everywhere.

Treatment charts typically include a patient face photo as well as photos of the treatment port/field setup, both used for ongoing identity and setup verification.

Page 6: Handling Documentation Errors

Errors happen — how you correct them matters:

Paper chart: never use white-out. Draw a single line through the error, then initial and date next to it.

Electronic chart: document the correct information along with the date; the original entry stays in the database rather than being erased.

Every error needs to be dated and documented at the time it's found — not silently fixed and forgotten.

Quick Check!

You find an incorrect entry on a paper chart. What's the correct way to fix it?

B. A single line through the error, initialed and dated, preserves the original entry while clearly marking the correction — never white-out on a legal document.

Page 7: Reducing Errors

Common error-reduction strategies used across radiation therapy departments:

  • Therapists working in partners, cross-checking field placement and machine parameters
  • Skin marks to outline fields, and photos of setups
  • Measuring SSDs or field-margin distances from bony landmarks
  • Isocentric treatment techniques, which require little to no movement between fields
  • Lasers to assist setup, plus weekly (or daily) port film/image review
  • Weekly clinical exams and weekly chart review

Most of these strategies start as early as CT simulation — error prevention isn't a treatment-day-only concern.

Page 8: Billing and Coding Basics

ICD-10-CM codes classify diagnoses (including distinguishing primary, secondary, and metastatic disease). CPT (Current Procedural Terminology) codes report the procedures and services performed.

In radiation therapy, billing codes reflect beam energy, number of fields, planning complexity, and any accessories or special technique used. As the therapist, CT simulation and treatment charges are largely your responsibility to generate — typically via an electronic charge sheet created after each session.

Specific CPT codes themselves aren't covered here — the goal is understanding that billable services trace directly back to what you document.

Page 9: Recording Reportable Events

Certain treatment errors must be formally recorded and, in some cases, reported to the Radiation Safety Officer, the facility's Radiation Safety Committee, or the NRC/state agency:

  • Delivered dose exceeding 15% over the weekly prescription, or missing a written directive/daily dose recording
  • Tumor dose errors exceeding ±5%
  • Misadministration — treating the wrong patient or site, a weekly dose over 30% of prescription, or a total dose over 20% above prescription — requires immediate notification of the NRC or state agency

Quick Check!

Which of these requires immediate notification of the NRC or state agency?

B. Misadministration — including treating the wrong patient — requires immediate regulatory notification; small, within-tolerance daily variances do not.

Page 10: Wrap-Up

You've covered what belongs in the treatment record, daily documentation requirements, Record and Verify systems and their limits, patient identification, error correction, error-reduction strategies, billing basics, and reportable events.

Good documentation isn't paperwork bolted onto the "real" job — for a legal, retreatable, auditable record of what actually happened to a patient, it is the job.
