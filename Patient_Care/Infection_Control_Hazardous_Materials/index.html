Infection Control & Hazardous Materials

Interactive Lesson

Page 1: Why Infection Control Is Non-Negotiable

Radiation therapy patients are frequently immunocompromised — from their cancer, from treatment, or from both. You handle multiple patients back-to-back, often touching shared equipment (couch, immobilization devices, positioning aids) between them. Consistent infection control isn't optional paperwork; it's how you avoid becoming the link that spreads infection between vulnerable patients.

Page 2: The Chain of Infection

Infection spreads through a predictable chain, and breaking any single link stops transmission:

  • Pathogen — the organism itself
  • Reservoir — where it lives and grows
  • Portal of exit — how it leaves the reservoir
  • Mode of transmission — direct (droplet, direct contact) or indirect (airborne, vehicle-borne/fomite, vector-borne)
  • Portal of entry — how it enters a new host
  • Susceptible host — someone whose defenses can't fight it off

Hand hygiene, PPE, and cleaning protocols each target a different link in this chain.

Page 3: Hand Hygiene

Hand washing is the single simplest and most effective method of environmental infection control available, and it must be performed before and after every patient contact — no exceptions for a "quick" interaction.

Alcohol-based hand sanitizer is acceptable for most routine contact, but true hand washing with soap and water is required when hands are visibly soiled, and for certain organisms (like C. difficile) that alcohol alone won't reliably kill.

Quick Check!

When should you perform hand hygiene relative to a patient encounter?

C. Hand hygiene is required both before and after every single patient contact.

Page 4: Asepsis

Medical asepsis ("clean technique") reduces the number of organisms present and prevents their spread — hand hygiene, cleaning surfaces, wearing gloves for routine contact.

Surgical/sterile asepsis ("sterile technique") eliminates all organisms from a defined field — sterile gowning and gloving, sterile draping, and maintaining a sterile field are used for invasive procedures like certain brachytherapy or line placements you may assist with.

Equipment itself requires the correct level of care: some items need only disinfection between patients, others require full sterilization.

Page 5: CDC Standard Precautions

Standard Precautions apply to every patient, every time — regardless of known infection status, because you often don't know:

  • Hand hygiene
  • Personal protective equipment (gloves, gowns, masks) matched to the expected exposure
  • Safe handling of contaminated equipment and surfaces
  • Safe disposal of linens, needles, patient supplies, and blood/body fluids
  • Safe needle practices (never recap by hand)

Page 6: Transmission-Based Precautions

When a specific infection is known or suspected, Standard Precautions get layered with transmission-based precautions matched to how that organism spreads:

  • Contact precautions — gown and gloves for direct or indirect contact organisms
  • Droplet precautions — mask for organisms spread by respiratory droplets over short distances
  • Airborne precautions — N95 respirator and negative-pressure room for organisms that travel on tiny particles over longer distances

Quick Check!

A patient with a suspected airborne illness needs which level of respiratory protection?

B. Airborne precautions require an N95 (or higher) respirator, not a standard surgical mask.

Page 7: Additional Precautions

Neutropenic precautions (reverse isolation) protect an immunocompromised patient from you and the environment — the direction of protection flips compared to standard precautions. Many radiation therapy patients undergoing chemotherapy fall into this category.

Be alert for signs of healthcare-associated (nosocomial) infections — infections a patient didn't have on admission, and report anything concerning through your facility's protocol.

Page 8: Handling and Disposal of Hazardous Materials

Beyond biological infection control, you'll encounter other hazardous materials:

  • Chemicals (cleaning agents, disinfectants) — know their proper storage and handling
  • Chemotherapy agents — require special handling precautions; patients receiving concurrent chemo may have contaminated bodily fluids for a period afterward
  • Metals (e.g., block alloy used in shielding) — handled with appropriate precautions during fabrication/storage

The Safety Data Sheet (SDS) for any hazardous material tells you its risks, safe handling procedures, and what to do in case of exposure — know where your department's SDS binder or database is located.

Page 9: Wrap-Up

You've covered the chain of infection, hand hygiene, asepsis, CDC standard and transmission-based precautions, neutropenic precautions, and hazardous material handling.

The common thread across all of it: know the risk in front of you, and match your precautions to it — consistently, every patient, every time.
